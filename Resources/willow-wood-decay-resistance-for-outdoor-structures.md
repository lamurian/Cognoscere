Black willow wood has no natural resistance to decay. The USDA Forest Service rates willow as not durable under conditions favorable to decay, placing it alongside aspen, basswood, and cottonwood as the least durable North American hardwoods [@krinard1985]. Purdue Extension confirms the wood has no resistance to wood decay [@cassens2007]. Virginia Tech rates it as poor for decay resistance [@virginiatechdepartmentofwoodscience2000].

For fence posts or building structures in ground contact, untreated black willow decays rapidly. The tree itself typically begins to die by age 55, with the oldest known decay-free tree at 70 years [@krinard1985]. Willow sapwood does accept preservative treatment relatively easily, making pressure-treated willow viable for outdoor use where decay is a concern. Without treatment, willow is one of the poorest choices among commercial woods for structural or fence applications exposed to moisture.
