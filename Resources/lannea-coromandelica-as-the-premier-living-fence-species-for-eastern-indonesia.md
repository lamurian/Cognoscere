Lannea coromandelica (syn. Lannea grandis), known locally in various Indonesian names including kayu jawa, is documented as the most popular living fence and boundary marker tree across Eastern Indonesia, including Nusa Tenggara, Sumbawa, Flores, and Timor [@reville2017]. Large cuttings planted directly into the ground root within a few months, making propagation extremely simple. The tree tolerates drought, salt spray, and virtually any soil type, making it uniquely suited for West Timor's challenging semi-arid conditions.

This species is dry deciduous, losing its leaves during the dry season, but remains evergreen if it has access to groundwater [@reville2017]. In Sumbawa, farmers cut excess growth and feed the leaves to goats during the dry season, providing critical fodder when other feed is scarce. Despite being the most common living fence tree in the region, scientific literature on Lannea coromandelica remains surprisingly limited -- Wikipedia describes it as merely a stub, highlighting a gap between local practical knowledge and academic documentation.

The tree rarely produces seeds in Indonesia and is exclusively propagated by large cuttings, which simplifies fence establishment. Its rapid growth, easy rooting from cuttings, drought tolerance, and ability to grow in poor soils make it the ideal starter species for living fences in West Timor. Once established, living fences of Lannea catch seeds deposited by birds and gradually increase biodiversity, evolving into richer hedgerow ecosystems over time [@reville2017].
