## Summary

The Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) is a 21-item self-report instrument measuring depression severity over the past two weeks, aligned with DSM-IV (and subsequently DSM-5) criteria for major depressive disorder. Items cover cognitive-affective symptoms (sadness, pessimism, guilt, self-dislike, self-criticalness, suicidal thoughts, worthlessness) and somatic-vegetative symptoms (sleep changes, appetite changes, loss of energy, concentration difficulty, fatigue, loss of sexual interest). Each item is rated 0-3 with total scores ranging from 0-63. Severity thresholds: 0-13 minimal, 14-19 mild, 20-28 moderate, 29-63 severe.

The original manual (Beck, Steer, & Brown, 1996) reported internal consistency of 0.92-0.93 and test-retest reliability of 0.93 [@beck1996]. A comprehensive psychometric review by Wang and Gorenstein (2013) covering 118 studies found average Cronbach's alpha around 0.9 (range 0.83-0.96) and retest reliability ranging from 0.73 to 0.96 across populations [@wang2013]. Sensitivity for detecting major depression was >=0.70 across studies, with AUC typically >=0.75.

Cutoff scores vary by population: non-clinical samples use cutoff 10-16 (sensitivity 0.80-0.90, specificity 0.70-0.85), medical samples use cutoff 7-20, and psychiatric samples use cutoff 19-31. The two-factor structure (cognitive-affective and somatic-vegetative) has been replicated across multiple populations and language versions through confirmatory factor analysis.

## Key Points

- 21 items, 0-3 scale, total 0-63, 2-week recall aligned with DSM-IV/DSM-5 MDD criteria
- Original manual (Beck 1996): alpha 0.92-0.93, retest 0.93 [@beck1996]
- Comprehensive review (Wang 2013, 118 studies): alpha ~0.9, retest 0.73-0.96, sensitivity >=0.70 [@wang2013]
- Factor structure: general depression with cognitive-affective and somatic-vegetative dimensions
- Cutoff varies by population: non-clinical 10-16, medical 7-20, psychiatric 19-31
- Copyrighted material must be purchased from the publisher
- Recommended as screening tool (first phase of two-stage studies), not standalone diagnostic instrument
