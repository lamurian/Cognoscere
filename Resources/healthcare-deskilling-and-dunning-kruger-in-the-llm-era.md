Healthcare emerges as the most heavily impacted industry from the Dunning-Kruger effect in the LLM era. Choudhury and Chaudhry (2024) identify a critical self-referential feedback loop: as clinicians increasingly trust LLMs for diagnosis, treatment planning, and patient note summarization, their own diagnostic and critical thinking skills may atrophy. This deskilling risk is most acute for the next generation of medical professionals who grow up with AI doing the bulk of analytical work [@choudhury2024].

Naqvi et al. (2025) warn that overreliance on generative AI in health sciences education risks "cognitive complacency" — students and practitioners trading critical thinking for speed. They note that higher-order cognitive functions like emotional intelligence, ethical reasoning, and diagnostic judgment cannot be encoded by pre-trained models. The paper documents real failures: an externally validated AI sepsis prediction model that led to delayed interventions despite insufficient accuracy, and inappropriate drug recommendations [@naqvi2025].

The Dunning-Kruger mechanism in healthcare is two-layered: (1) clinicians with limited AI literacy may overestimate their ability to evaluate LLM outputs, and (2) the very expertise required to detect AI errors (e.g., specialist dermatological knowledge) is what the novice user lacks — precisely the condition that creates the Dunning-Kruger paradox of not knowing what you don't know.
