## Summary

The preference for organic, curved forms in architecture is grounded in biophilic design theory — the innate human tendency to seek connections with nature. Research shows that buildings incorporating natural forms, materials, and spatial configurations reduce cortisol levels, lower blood pressure, improve cognitive function, and enhance emotional wellbeing [@zhong2022]. Simon Dale articulates this directly: "My aesthetic is generally soft and curved, coming mostly from the natural world... Rectangular rooms do feel box-like and slightly claustrophobic" [@voiceofnature2022]. This aligns with biophilia as evolutionary adaptation — humans evolved in natural environments with curved forms, and the rectilinear built environment is a recent departure from that context.

Biophilic design principles relevant to elven-style housing include: visual connection with nature (views of greenery), non-visual connection (natural ventilation, water sounds), thermal and airflow variability (natural materials regulate humidity), dynamic and diffuse light (filtered through greenery), and biomorphic forms (curves, arches, organic geometries). Together these create "restorative environmental design" — spaces that actively replenish cognitive and emotional resources. Neuroarchitecture research confirms that environmentally friendly building materials (wood, earth, straw) positively impact mental health through improved indoor air quality and reduced chemical off-gassing [@zhong2022].

## Key Points
- Curved forms trigger positive neural responses; rectilinear spaces correlate with elevated cortisol
- Natural materials improve indoor air quality by regulating humidity (30-60% RH optimal)
- The fantasy appeal of elven architecture taps innate biophilic preferences from evolutionary history
- Combining multiple biophilic elements creates synergistic restorative effects beyond any single feature
- Biophilic design is not aesthetic luxury — it measurably improves cognitive performance and healing rates
