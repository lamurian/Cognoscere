The simulated affect valence distribution (M=0.034, SD=0.082, range=[-0.187, 0.279] on [-1,1]) deviates from well-established empirical norms. PANAS norms show PA mean ~3.0-3.5 (SD ~0.7-0.8) and NA mean ~1.5-2.0 (SD ~0.6-0.8) on 1-5 [@watson1988]. When mapped to a bipolar [-1,1] valence continuum, these suggest population valence mean ~0.1-0.2 with SD ~0.3-0.4, consistent with the positivity bias which finds most people report mildly positive affect (Diener & Diener, 1996).

The simulated mean of 0.034 is barely above zero, substantially lower than the empirical 0.1-0.2 range. The SD of 0.082 is approximately 4x narrower than the empirical ~0.3-0.4, producing an extremely compressed range where all agents fall within ±0.28 of zero. The simulated skew of +0.225 (right-skewed) is also inconsistent with the positivity bias, which should produce left-skew (most people above the midpoint). This means the simulation has nearly all agents clustered at neutral affect, missing the mild positivity that characterises general populations and that drives approach-oriented coping and social engagement.
