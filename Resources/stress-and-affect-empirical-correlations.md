## Summary

Perceived stress correlates negatively with positive affect and positively with negative affect. For a unified affect continuum (bipolar scale from negative to positive), the correlation with stress is moderate-to-strong negative, approximately r = -0.35 to -0.50.

Acoba [@acoba2024] examined perceived stress (PSS-10) and positive affect (PANAS) in a Filipino adult sample (N = 426). The bivariate correlations were: PSS-10 with positive affect r = -0.176 (p < 0.01), PSS-10 with anxiety r = 0.454 (p < 0.01), and PSS-10 with depression r = 0.505 (p < 0.01). These indicate that higher perceived stress is associated with lower positive affect and higher negative affect.

When affect is measured as a unified bipolar continuum (e.g., the valence dimension of the circumplex model), the correlation with stress sits between the positive and negative affect correlations. Theoretical models of stress and emotion (Lazarus & Folkman's transactional model) posit that stress appraisal directly influences affective valence — threat appraisals generate negative affect, while challenge appraisals maintain positive affect.

Wollny and Jacobs [@wollny2021] found CD-RISC-10 (resilience) and PSS-4 correlated at r = -0.49, and resilience correlated with WHO-5 well-being at r = 0.48, further supporting the stress-affect link through the mediating role of resilience.

## Key Points
- PSS-10 with positive affect: r = -0.18 (small negative) [@acoba2024]
- PSS-10 with anxiety: r = 0.45 (moderate positive) [@acoba2024]
- PSS-10 with depression: r = 0.51 (strong positive) [@acoba2024]
- For a unified bipolar affect scale, expected correlation with stress: r ≈ -0.35 to -0.50
