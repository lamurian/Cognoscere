## Summary

The steel skeleton + living plant infill concept uses a permanent lightweight steel frame (light gauge steel) as the structural skeleton, while living woody plants grown within or along the frame serve as infill, replacing bricks, mortar, and conventional cladding. This differs from Baubotanik where steel scaffolding is temporary and removed once trees self-support [@mylo2023]. Here, the steel stays permanently as the primary load path, while living plants provide infill, insulation, shading, and aesthetic functions.

Light gauge steel framing (LGFS) uses cold-formed steel studs and tracks at typically 16-24in centers, forming walls that are strong, non-combustible, and dimensionally stable. Current infill materials include foam panels, gypsum board, and concrete. Replacing these with living plant infill — woven willow/crape myrtle rods, pleached branches, or inosculated tree panels — creates a hybrid where the steel carries structural loads while the living layer provides thermal mass, air purification, microclimate regulation, and self-renewing aesthetics.

The Plane Tree Cube (Nagold, 2012) comes closest: steel frame with London plane trees growing at 6 levels, though the steel there is designed for eventual removal [@mylo2023]. A permanent steel frame + living infill system would be simpler to engineer since the living component never needs to become load-bearing.
