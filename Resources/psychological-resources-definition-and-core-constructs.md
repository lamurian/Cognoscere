Psychological resources are a central concept in positive psychology, stress research, and resilience science. The most influential framework is Hobfoll's Conservation of Resources (COR) theory, which defines resources as "entities that are either centrally valued in their own right (e.g., self-esteem, health, peace of mind) or that act as a means to obtain centrally valued ends (e.g., social support, personal mastery, optimism)" [@hobfoll2002]. COR theory classifies resources into four categories: object resources (material assets), condition resources (employment, marriage, social roles), personal resources (skills, self-efficacy, optimism), and energy resources (time, money, knowledge).

In positive psychology, the Psychological Capital (PsyCap) framework operationalised by Luthans, Youssef-Morgan, and Avolio identifies four key psychological resources: self-efficacy (confidence to succeed at challenging tasks), hope (perseverance toward goals and ability to generate alternative pathways), optimism (positive expectations about future outcomes), and resilience (capacity to bounce back from adversity) [@luthans2017]. Importantly, within PsyCap, resilience is itself treated as one of several psychological resources, creating a nested relationship that complicates demarcation.

Pellerin and Raufaste expanded the set to nine well-validated psychological resources: self-efficacy, optimism, hope, personal wisdom, self-transcendence, gratitude toward the world, gratitude for being, peaceful disengagement, and acceptance [@pellerin2022]. Zlobina identified optimism, self-efficacy, and emotional stability as the three key psychological resources that maintain psychological stability under prolonged wartime stress [@zlobina2024].

Common across these frameworks is that psychological resources are relatively stable individual-level dispositions that can be measured, cultivated, and depleted. They function as protective factors that influence how individuals appraise and cope with stressors.
