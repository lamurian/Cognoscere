## Summary

The quantitative evidence base for workplace burnout and resilience interventions uses a range of research designs and validated instruments, each with distinct strengths and limitations.

**Common research designs:** Across two major systematic reviews covering 44 studies, the most common designs were RCTs (n=18), quasi-experimental studies with waitlist controls (n=10), and single-group pretest-posttest designs (n=20) [@cohen2023; @arajo2026]. Only 4 of 33 studies in one review achieved all four quality indicators: large sample (>30), control group, post-intervention follow-up, and statistically significant positive outcomes [@cohen2023].

**Validated burnout measures:**
- Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) — the most used, measuring emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and personal accomplishment
- Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI) — exhaustion and disengagement
- Shirom-Melamed Burnout Measure (SMBM) — cognitive weariness, physical fatigue, emotional exhaustion
- Professional Quality of Life Scale (ProQOL-5) — burnout, compassion satisfaction, secondary traumatic stress
- Professional Fulfillment Index (PFI) — burnout component

**Validated resilience measures:**
- Brief Resilience Scale (BRS) — 6 items, measures ability to bounce back
- Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC) — 10 or 25 items
- Cognitive Hardiness Scale — 10-18 items
- Psychological Capital Questionnaire (PCQ-12) — includes resilience subscale

**Methodological limitations:** Most studies rely on self-report measures creating common method bias risk. Only 3 of 33 studies used organisational-level interventions; 30 focused on individual behaviour change. Follow-up beyond immediate post-intervention is rare, with only a handful of studies conducting 3-12 month follow-ups. The healthcare sector is over-represented, limiting generalizability.

## Key Points

- Only 12% of studies meet all four quality criteria (large sample, control, follow-up, significant positive outcome)
- MBI is the most common burnout measure; BRS and CD-RISC lead for resilience
- Self-report bias is a widespread concern in this literature
- Long-term follow-up is rare — most evidence reflects short-term effects only
- The evidence base is concentrated in healthcare settings
