## Summary

The mPareshan intervention in rural Pakistan demonstrates a feasible model for combining community health workers (CHWs) with digital tools to address the severe mental health treatment gap. With psychiatrist-to-population ratios as low as 0.19 per 100,000 in Pakistan, task-sharing through community frontline workers is essential. The mPareshan study assessed the acceptability and appropriateness of a technology-assisted mental health intervention where Lady Health Workers used mobile devices to deliver evidence-based psychological support [@akhtar2025].

This model builds on prior evidence that cognitive behaviour therapy delivered by Lady Health Workers can markedly improve postnatal depression in rural Pakistan. The technology component (a mobile application) equipped CHWs with: structured assessment tools, guided intervention protocols, supervisor communication channels, and data collection capabilities. Qualitative findings showed that both CHWs and community members found the approach acceptable because it reduced stigma (technology was seen as modern and confidential), leveraged existing community trust in CHWs, and enabled consistent service delivery despite low literacy levels through visual and audio components [@akhtar2025].

Key success factors included: integration with existing community health infrastructure, training and ongoing supervision for CHWs, culturally adapted content in local languages, and the mobile app reducing the cognitive burden on CHWs by guiding session flow. Challenges included: technology literacy barriers for older CHWs, occasional network connectivity issues, and the need for clear referral pathways for severe cases.

The mPareshan model aligns with the broader task-sharing evidence base from other LMICs: the Friendship Bench in Zimbabwe (community health workers delivering talk therapy), the Programme for Improving Mental Health Care (PRIME) in Ethiopia and Nepal, and WHO's mhGAP framework. The addition of digital tools to CHW-delivered care represents an evolution of task-sharing that can improve fidelity, supervision, and data collection at scale.

## Key Points

- Technology-assisted CHW mental health intervention is acceptable and appropriate in rural Pakistan
- Mobile apps reduce cognitive burden on CHWs by structuring assessment and intervention protocols
- Technology reduces stigma (perceived as modern and confidential)
- Integration with existing community health infrastructure is critical for sustainability
- Challenges: digital literacy, connectivity, referral pathways for severe cases
- Aligns with broader evidence on task-sharing (Friendship Bench, PRIME, mhGAP)
