## Summary

Rather than choosing between specialized care or infrastructure investment, the strongest evidence supports interventions that integrate both by embedding specialization within robust primary care systems. Four categories of interventions show measurable impact.

**1. Team-based primary care with embedded specialists**: The US NASEM report recommends interprofessional primary care teams that include behavioral health providers, social workers, pharmacists, and care managers working alongside primary care clinicians [@krist2025]. Maryland's Primary Care Program demonstrated that shifting Medicare payment from fee-for-service to population-based models enables practices to hire care coordinators and expand team composition, reducing inpatient use by 21% [@phillips2022].

**2. Task-sharing and community health workers**: Expanding the primary care workforce through task-sharing — training community health workers, peer support specialists, and nurses to deliver evidence-based interventions previously reserved for specialists — is effective across high- and low-resource settings. Patel et al. argue this should be a permanent workforce expansion, not a stop-gap, and apply to all countries regardless of income level [@patel2023].

**3. Integrated care pathways with specialist-primary care coordination**: Condition-specific integrated care pathways that define clear roles between primary care and specialist providers improve outcomes for chronic diseases. Examples include collaborative care models for mental health (where primary care providers consult with psychiatric specialists) and stroke care pathways where primary care teams manage long-term follow-up [@gumas2024].

**4. Hybrid payment reform**: Moving from pure fee-for-service to hybrid payment models that include population-based prospective payments enables primary care teams to deliver comprehensive care without being restricted to billable visits. The CMS has introduced Advanced Primary Care Management codes and prospective monthly payments, though the NASEM Standing Committee emphasizes that much more federal action is needed to achieve the necessary scale [@krist2025].

## Key Points

- Team-based primary care with embedded specialists reduces hospital use by 21% (Maryland evidence)
- Task-sharing expands workforce capacity and is effective across all resource settings
- Integrated care pathways define clear specialist-primary care roles for chronic conditions
- Hybrid payment (population-based + fee-for-service) enables comprehensive team-based care
- The strongest reforms do not choose between specialization and infrastructure but integrate both

## Confidence: Moderately high (supported by multiple peer-reviewed evaluations and institutional reports)
