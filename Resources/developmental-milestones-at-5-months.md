## Summary

At five months, the infant consolidates motor skills and expands social communication. Weight reaches roughly 7.3 kg (16.1 lb) and length 65 cm (25.6 in). The infant can now roll in both directions and may begin to sit with support, dramatically expanding their ability to explore the environment [@misirliyan2023].

## Key Points

**Body Weight and Height:** Median weight ~7.3 kg (range 6.2–8.6 kg); median length ~65 cm. Weight gain slows to roughly 100–140 g/week. First teeth may begin emerging (lower central incisors), though timing varies widely.

**Emotional Maturity:** Expresses a wider range of emotions — joy, frustration, curiosity are now distinct. May show preference for primary caregivers; sometimes wary of strangers though not yet full stranger anxiety. Enjoys looking at self in mirror. Laughs during social play and anticipates routines.

**Psychological Development:** Late Piaget's substage 2 / early substage 3 (secondary circular reactions). The infant now repeats actions that produce interesting effects in the external environment (not just their own body). Explores objects by mouthing, shaking, banging. Shows interest in cause and effect — shakes rattle deliberately to produce sound. Recognises own name.

**Motoric Development:** Gross motor: rolls from back to front and front to back; sits with support (tripod position); bears weight on legs when held standing; rocks on stomach, may pivot. Fine motor: reaches with one hand; transfers objects from hand to hand; uses raking grasp to pick up small objects; bangs objects on surface.

**Speech and Language Development:** Begins to produce consonant-vowel combinations ("ba," "ga"). Takes turns vocalising with caregiver — proto-conversation emerges. Responds to own name. Uses voice to express pleasure and displeasure. Makes raspberries and other oral-motor sounds [@nationalinstituteondeafnessandothercommunicationdisorders2022].

**Signs of Growth Retardation:** Red flags: not rolling in either direction by 5.5 months; unable to sit with support; no reaching for objects; persistent fisting; not turning toward sounds or voices; no vowel-consonant combinations; no laughter; extreme floppiness or stiffness; not showing interest in people or toys; failure to gain weight consistently.
