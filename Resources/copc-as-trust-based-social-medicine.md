## Summary

Community-Oriented Primary Care (COPC), pioneered by South African doctors Sidney and Emily Kark at Pholela Health Centre in 1940, represents the first health system to systematically make people's social relationships an integral part of daily medical practice [@marcus2016]. The Karks developed a model where healthcare starts with the individual, then expands to the family and community—using epidemiological and social scientific information from defined geographic areas to develop services with individuals, families, and whole communities.

COPC was built on trust rather than institutional authority. The Pholela team overcame initial community resentment by hiring local people as community health workers (a term coined by Sidney Kark), holding meetings with chiefs and elders, and demonstrating that the health centre would benefit the community [@marcus2016]. Within two years, the majority of the health team were local women and men from Pholela itself.

The core innovation was integrating clinic-based care with home visits. Community health workers were assigned to 25-30 homesteads each, conducting demographic surveys, basic epidemiology, and health education in people's homes. This approach to building trust and integrating care directly into the community proved effective: the infant mortality rate in COPC-served areas dropped from 25.6% to 16.6% within two years, while remaining unchanged in areas without COPC [@marcus2016].

## Key Points

- COPC starts from the individual and expands outward to family and community, exactly as the user's framework describes.
- The Pholela team demonstrated that health is built through relationships, not through isolated clinical encounters.
- Community health workers from the local population were essential for building trust and cultural understanding.
- The COPC model showed measurable impact: reduced IMR, improved nutrition, better epidemic control.
- Key principle: "concentrated on how the health care team could assist the individual, the family and the community to do things for themselves" rather than doing things for patients.
