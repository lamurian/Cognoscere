## Summary

Resilience measures consistently show a moderate-to-strong positive correlation with positive affect and a modest negative correlation with negative affect. Reported correlations with positive affect range from r = 0.36 to polychoric γ = 0.70.

Yang et al. @yang2020 reported r = 0.360 (p < 0.01) between CD-RISC-25 and PANAS positive affect in a Chinese clinical sample. Montero-Marin et al. @monteromarin2015 found a strong polychoric correlation (γ = 0.70) between CD-RISC-10 and positive affect latent factors in Spanish primary care physicians, and γ = -0.35 between CD-RISC-10 and negative affect.

The strong association between resilience and positive affect supports the broaden-and-build theory: individuals higher in resilience experience more positive emotions, which in turn build psychological resources [@monteromarin2015].

## Key Points
- Resilience and positive affect: r ≈ 0.30 to 0.70 (moderate to strong positive)
- The weaker correlation in @yang2020 (r = 0.360) vs @monteromarin2015 (γ = 0.70) may reflect population differences (clinical SUD vs healthy physicians)
- Resilience and negative affect: r ≈ -0.20 to -0.45 (moderate negative)
- The asserted bound |r| < 0.6 for resilience ↔ affect in tests #4 and #14 is too restrictive on the upper end, as strong correlations (r > 0.6) are documented
- The ad-hoc bound |r| < 0.5 is even more restrictive and empirically questionable

## Sources
- @monteromarin2015 - n=622 physicians, CD-RISC vs PANAS: γ = 0.70 (positive affect), γ = -0.35 (negative affect)
- @yang2020 - n=415 SUD patients, CD-RISC vs PANAS positive affect: r = 0.360
