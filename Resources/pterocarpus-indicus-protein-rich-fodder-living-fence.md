## Summary

Pterocarpus indicus (angsana/sena) is a large deciduous tree used in West Timor living fences, valued primarily for protein-rich leaves lopped as goat fodder during the dry season when grasses are scarce [@reville2017]. It is a leguminous species capable of nitrogen fixation. Its extensive root system stabilizes soil on slopes, making it valuable for erosion control. Best uses: dry-season goat fodder, erosion control on sloping land, shade, and high-value timber (used for construction and furniture). Typically used as widely spaced posts with wire rather than dense hedges. Also important in the traditional mamar agroforestry system. Plant from seedlings or large cuttings at wet season onset. Prefers deep, well-drained soils. Tolerates seasonal drought once established. Avoid in narrow boundary plantings where space is limited. Not as fast-growing as Lannea or Gliricidia. Deciduous in dry season, so no windbreak during driest months. Young trees need protection from livestock. Symbiotic benefits: fixes atmospheric nitrogen improving soil fertility, extensive root system stabilizes slopes and reduces erosion, dense canopy reduces soil evaporation, leaf litter enriches organic matter, flowers provide nectar for bees, and the tree provides bird and insect habitat.

Key sources: @reville2017, @guterres2022.
