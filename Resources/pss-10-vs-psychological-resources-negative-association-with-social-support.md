Perceived stress as measured by PSS-10 shows a small but consistent negative correlation with perceived social support. Social support functions as a psychological resource in the stress appraisal process.

Acoba [@acoba2024] reported in a cross-sectional study of 426 Filipino adults: Family Support vs PSS-10 r = -0.180 (p < .01); Significant Other Support vs PSS-10 r = -0.119 (p < .05); Friend Support vs PSS-10 r = -0.075 (n.s.). Family support was the strongest inverse predictor (a = -0.18, p < .001). Close family ties buffer stress perception more effectively than friendships.

This aligns with Lazarus and Folkman's stress-and-coping theory. Social support is a key resource in secondary appraisal. When people perceive that support is available, they judge stressors as less threatening, reducing overall perceived stress. The buffering effect depends on the support source. Family and significant others provide more tangible and emotional resource replenishment than general friendships.
