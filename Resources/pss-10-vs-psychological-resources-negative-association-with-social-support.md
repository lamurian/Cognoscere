Perceived stress as measured by PSS-10 shows a small but consistent negative correlation with perceived social support (psychological resources).

Acoba (2024) reported in a cross-sectional study of 426 Filipino adults: Family Support vs PSS-10 r = -0.180 (p < .01); Significant Other Support vs PSS-10 r = -0.119 (p < .05); Friend Support vs PSS-10 r = -0.075 (n.s.). Family support was the strongest inverse predictor (a = -0.18, p < .001), suggesting that close family ties buffer stress perception more effectively than friendships.

This aligns with Lazarus and Folkman's stress-and-coping theory: social support is a key resource in the secondary appraisal process. When individuals perceive that support is available, they judge stressors as less threatening, reducing overall perceived stress. The buffering effect is specific to support source — family and significant others provide more tangible and emotional resource replenishment than general friendships.
