## Summary\n\nThis research expanded on [Top Living Fence Species for West Timor Drylands](Resources/top-living-fence-species-for-west-timor-drylands.md) by deep-diving into growth speed, best uses, planting recommendations, and symbiotic environmental benefits for each of the 12 identified species. New sources included the USDA Gliricidia plant guide [@ogoshi2023], a peer-reviewed study on Gliricidia nitrogen efficiency [@figueiredo2023], a Leucaena biomass allometry study from a valley savanna with rainfall similar to West Timor [@liu2018], the ECHO living fences technical note [@martin2012], a biodiversity study on legumes in East Timorese agroforestry systems [@guterres2022], and NC State Extension profiles for several ornamental species.\n\n## Species Summaries\n\n[Lannea coromandelica: Growth Speed and Optimal Uses](Resources/lannea-coromandelica-growth-speed-and-optimal-uses.md) -- Fastest establishment from cuttings (roots within months). Best for boundary marking, goat fodder, firewood. Grows in any soil, no inputs needed. Avoid where evergreen windbreak needed.\n\n[Gliricidia sepium: Growth Rates and Agroecological Benefits](Resources/gliricidia-sepium-growth-rates-and-agroecological-benefits.md) -- Moderate growth, 7-month establishment in 21-inch rainfall. Best for soil conservation, green manure, fodder. At 1000 plants/ha achieves 89% N recovery efficiency. Requires pruning to avoid crop competition.\n\n[Leucaena leucocephala: Growth and Symbiotic Contributions](Resources/leucaena-leucocephala-growth-and-symbiotic-contributions.md) -- 11cm DBH, 9m height after 17 years in 584mm rainfall. Best for high-protein fodder (25% protein), soil conservation. Deep taproot to 8m. Avoid near Eucalyptus due to competition.\n\n[Sesbania grandiflora: Fast-Growing Legume Living Fence](Resources/sesbania-grandiflora-fast-growing-legume-living-fence.md) -- Fast growth, edible flowers/pods. Best for food, fodder, green manure. Requires >1000mm rainfall. Less drought-tolerant than Lannea/Gliricidia.\n\n[Moringa oleifera: Nutritious Fast-Growing Living Fence](Resources/moringa-oleifera-nutritious-fast-growing-living-fence.md) -- Ultra-fast first-year growth, 1m spacing for palisade. Best for highly nutritious leaves (16-17% protein). Avoid waterlogged soils. Needs regular harvesting.\n\n[Pterocarpus indicus: Protein-Rich Fodder Living Fence](Resources/pterocarpus-indicus-protein-rich-fodder-living-fence.md) -- Valuable for dry-season goat fodder and erosion control. Legume with nitrogen fixation. Not as fast as Lannea. Deciduous in dry season.\n\n[Euphorbia tirucalli: Impenetrable Succulent Living Fence](Resources/euphorbia-tirucalli-impenetrable-succulent-living-fence.md) -- Slow-growing succulent, forms impenetrable hedge. Best for security barriers. Extremely drought-tolerant. Toxic sap requires careful handling.\n\n[Thevetia peruviana: Drought-Tolerant Ornamental Living Hedge](Resources/thevetia-peruviana-drought-tolerant-ornamental-living-hedge.md) -- Medium growth, evergreen, showy flowers. Best for ornamental hedges and privacy screens. Highly toxic -- keep from children and livestock.\n\n[Jatropha curcas: Animal-Proof Protective Living Fence](Resources/jatropha-curcas-animal-proof-protective-living-fence.md) -- Moderate growth, animals won't browse it. Best for kitchen garden protection. Seeds yield biofuel and medicinal oil.\n\n[Cassava as a Temporary Interwoven Living Fence Species](Resources/cassava-as-a-temporary-interwoven-living-fence-species.md) -- Fast-growing, dual food-and-fence function. Temporary filler while permanent species establish. Not suitable as long-term post.\n\n[Bougainvillea: Thorny Drought-Tolerant Ornamental Barrier](Resources/bougainvillea-thorny-drought-tolerant-ornamental-barrier.md) -- Rapid growing, thorny, spectacular color displays. Best for security hedges with ornamental value. Needs training support.\n\n[Pomegranate: Multi-Purpose Fruiting Living Hedge](Resources/pomegranate-multi-purpose-fruiting-living-hedge.md) -- Medium growth, edible fruit, showy flowers. Best for dual-purpose boundary hedge. Not impenetrable alone -- combine with thornier species.\n\n## Key Recommendations for West Timor\n\nFor rapid, low-cost living fence establishment: use **Lannea coromandelica** as the backbone species. Complement with **Gliricidia sepium** for soil conservation and nitrogen fixation. Add **Leucaena leucocephala** (cv. Tarramba) for high-protein fodder. Use **Euphorbia tirucalli** or **Jatropha curcas** around kitchen gardens for animal-proof barriers. Integrate **Moringa oleifera** for nutritional security. Use **cassava** as temporary filler during establishment.\n\n## Confidence Assessment\n\n- **Lannea** growth data: Moderate (single detailed field source, highly consistent across locations) [@reville2017]\n- **Gliricidia** growth and N efficiency: High (peer-reviewed USDA plant guide + multiple academic studies) [@ogoshi2023; @figueiredo2023; @matheus2009]\n- **Leucaena** growth in dry conditions: High (peer-reviewed study in similar rainfall regime) [@liu2018]\n- **Euphorbia, Thevetia, Bougainvillea, Pomegranate**: Moderate (authoritative extension sources, but not specific to West Timor)\n- **Cassava, Jatropha, Pterocarpus**: Low-Moderate (limited direct academic evidence for living fence function in NTT)\n\n## Related Notes\n\n[Top Living Fence Species for West Timor Drylands](Resources/top-living-fence-species-for-west-timor-drylands.md)\n[Gliricidia and Leucaena as Proven Hedgerow Species for NTT Drylands](Resources/gliricidia-and-leucaena-as-proven-hedgerow-species-for-ntt-drylands.md)\n[Lannea coromandelica as the Premier Living Fence Species for Eastern Indonesia](Resources/lannea-coromandelica-as-the-premier-living-fence-species-for-eastern-indonesia.md)\n[Ecological and Socio-Economic Benefits of Living Fences in West Timor](Resources/ecological-and-socio-economic-benefits-of-living-fences-in-west-timor.md)"
