The Positive Appraisal Style Theory of Resilience (PASTOR) posits that a person's general tendency to evaluate stressors in a non-negative, non-threatening, or positive way is the central mechanism underlying psychological resilience [@kalisch2015]. This framework emerged from the observation that many people maintain mental health despite severe adversity, shifting research focus from pathophysiology to protective mechanisms.

According to PASTOR, stimulus appraisal (evaluation) processes play a causal role in generating emotional responses to potential stressors. A positive appraisal style protects against the detrimental effects of stress and mediates the effects of other known resilience factors such as social support, self-efficacy, and optimism.

The theory identifies three classes of cognitive processes that shape appraisal style: (1) positive situation classification — the tendency to initially classify ambiguous situations in a benign or positive way; (2) reappraisal — the ability to re-interpret a stressful situation to reduce its negative emotional impact; and (3) interference inhibition — the capacity to prevent negative thoughts or memories from interfering with ongoing processing. These processes can be investigated at the neural level, with prefrontal cortical regions exerting regulatory control over amygdala reactivity [@kalisch2015].

Conflicting perspectives suggest that positive appraisal may not always be adaptive. Freund and Staudinger [@freund2015] argue that negative appraisals can serve important functions, such as motivating behaviour change or enabling realistic threat assessment. Context-dependency is critical — the effectiveness of any appraisal strategy depends on the controllability and severity of the stressor.
