## Summary

The evidence points to three appropriate models of community-based digital intervention for preventing and mitigating mental ill health, each suited to different contexts and population needs.

**Model 1: Digital peer support communities.** Informal, naturally occurring peer support on interactive platforms (apps, social media, video) produces large effects on mental health (SMD = 0.53), comparable to professional therapy [@yeo2025]. This model works through five mechanisms: reciprocal interaction, experiential knowledge, shared understanding, social support, and safe community environment [@yuan2025]. It is most appropriate for mild-to-moderate distress, prevention, and early intervention in populations with high digital access. Success factors include anonymity, moderation, and interactive platform design.

**Model 2: Digital self-help with non-specialist guidance.** The WHO STARS chatbot demonstrates a structured transdiagnostic approach combining automated digital content (10 chatbot sessions) with brief human guidance from trained non-specialists (5 weekly 15-minute calls) [@keyan2025]. This hybrid model balances scalability with engagement, addressing the high dropout rates of fully self-guided digital interventions. It is appropriate for moderate distress in LMICs where specialist shortages are severe.

**Model 3: Technology-assisted community health worker programmes.** The mPareshan model equips existing CHWs with mobile tools to deliver evidence-based psychological interventions [@akhtar2025]. The technology reduces cognitive burden, ensures fidelity, and enables supervision at scale. This model is most appropriate for reaching underserved populations where CHW infrastructure already exists and where digital literacy barriers preclude fully self-guided digital tools.

**Choosing the appropriate model** depends on: (a) severity (peer support for mild prevention, guided chatbot for moderate, CHW-digital for severe with specialist referral); (b) digital access (high → peer support/chatbot, low → CHW-digital); (c) existing infrastructure (strong CHW network → technology-assisted task-sharing, strong primary care → integrated digital tools); (d) cultural context (collectivist societies show stronger digital peer support effects for mental health); (e) age (youth prefer chatbot/peer formats, while CHW models work across ages).

## Key Points

- Three appropriate models: digital peer support, chatbot with non-specialist guidance, technology-assisted CHW programmes
- Model choice depends on severity, digital access, existing infrastructure, cultural context, and age group
- Hybrid models (digital + human guidance) balance scalability with outcomes
- Digital peer support is most cost-effective for prevention and mild distress
- Technology-assisted CHW models reach the most underserved populations
- All models require: privacy safeguards, cultural adaptation, moderation, and clear referral pathways
