## Summary

Primary care systems improve population health through four interconnected mechanisms: first-contact accessibility, continuity of care, comprehensiveness, and coordination of care. These '4Cs' form the theoretical basis for why primary care infrastructure outperforms specialist-oriented models [@gumas2024; @fitzgerald2022].

**First-contact accessibility**: Patients with a regular primary care provider are more likely to receive preventive services including immunizations, blood pressure screenings, and cancer screenings. In countries where patients are automatically registered with a primary care physician (e.g., Norway, UK), lower proportions of adults forgo needed care [@fitzgerald2022]. The US has the lowest rates of having a regular doctor and the lowest after-hours care access among high-income countries, contributing to higher emergency department utilization [@gumas2024].

**Continuity of care**: Longstanding patient-provider relationships are associated with lower total health care costs, fewer hospitalizations, and improved care coordination. Only about 40% of US adults have had their primary care provider for five or more years, compared to 74% in the Netherlands and Germany [@fitzgerald2022]. Relational continuity builds trust and enables earlier detection of health problems [@gumas2024].

**Comprehensiveness**: Primary care teams that screen for social needs (housing, food security, financial stability) and integrate behavioral health services address the upstream determinants of health. However, coordination with community social services remains a challenge across all countries, with over 75% of physicians reporting major challenges [@gumas2024].

**Coordination**: Effective coordination between primary care, specialists, and hospitals reduces fragmented care. In the UK and New Zealand, over two-thirds of physicians report regularly receiving information about changes to patients' care plans, compared to only four in 10 in the US [@gumas2024]. Fragmentation leads to duplicate testing, medication errors, and worse outcomes.

## Key Points

- The 4Cs framework (first contact, continuity, comprehensiveness, coordination) explains primary care's population health advantage
- Countries with automatic patient registration systems achieve better access and continuity
- Relational continuity (5+ years with same provider) reduces hospitalizations and costs
- Comprehensiveness includes addressing social determinants and integrating behavioral health
- Poor care coordination is a key driver of health system fragmentation and worse outcomes

## Confidence: High (supported by multiple cross-national surveys and systematic reviews)
