## Summary

A growing body of critical scholarship argues that the global mental health movement's reliance on standardized Western psychiatric models—exported primarily through task-sharing programs like WHO mhGAP—constitutes a form of blanket intervention that often lacks individualized root cause diagnosis and cultural adaptation [@osborne2026]. Three key critiques emerge.

First, cultural mismatch: Western diagnostic categories (DSM/ICD) may not capture local idioms of distress. In many LMICs, psychological distress is expressed somatically—as 'burning in the head' or 'pressure in the heart'—rather than as sadness or hopelessness [@osborne2026]. Standardized screening tools developed in Western contexts may miss these presentations or misclassify them as medically unexplained symptoms. This represents a failure of root cause diagnosis: the underlying social and cultural determinants are not addressed.

Second, medicalization of social suffering: The Western model frames mental health problems as individual disorders amenable to diagnosis and pharmacological treatment, obscuring structural determinants such as poverty, violence, forced migration, and inequality [@osborne2026]. Standardized protocols that prioritize medication over psychosocial intervention may treat symptoms while ignoring root causes embedded in social and economic conditions.

Third, neglect of indigenous healing systems: Across Africa, Asia, and the Pacific, people traditionally seek help from healers, religious leaders, and community elders. Standardized global mental health initiatives often marginalize or exclude these actors, dismissing their practices as unscientific [@osborne2026]. This creates a disconnect between biomedical services and community trust. The Plessas et al. (2021) review of Indigenous populations in CANZUS nations found that psychological interventions often fail to account for cultural variables, and culture-tailored interventions should not be regarded as 'one-size-fits-all' [@plessas2021].

However, these critiques must be balanced against the pragmatic reality of severe workforce shortages. Task-sharing and standardized protocols have expanded access to basic mental health care where none existed before. The question is whether these approaches can evolve toward more individualized, culturally grounded care without sacrificing scalability.

## Key Points

- Western diagnostic categories may not capture local idioms of distress
- Standardized protocols risk medicalizing social suffering and ignoring structural determinants
- Indigenous healing systems are often marginalized by global mental health initiatives
- Plessas et al. (2021) found that interventions for Indigenous populations often lack cultural adaptation
- The tension between scalability (standardized protocols) and specificity (individualized root cause diagnosis) is unresolved
