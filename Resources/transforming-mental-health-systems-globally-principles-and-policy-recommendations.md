## Source

## Summary

The COVID-19 pandemic has exposed and amplified a pre-existing global mental health crisis that disproportionately affects marginalised populations, young people, and those exposed to trauma, conflict, and climate-related events [@patel2023]. Patel and colleagues argue that the dominant framing of mental ill health through categorical psychiatric diagnoses has led to an excessive reliance on specialist-delivered clinical interventions, neglect of prevention and recovery-oriented strategies, and failure to leverage community resources. Despite decades of investment in high-income countries, mental health indicators have not improved, suggesting that business as usual has failed.

The paper proposes five principles for transforming mental health systems: (1) targeting harmful social environments across the life course, particularly in early childhood; (2) replacing categorical diagnosis with a staging model that matches care to the severity and stage of illness; (3) empowering diverse front-line providers (community health workers, peers, teachers) to deliver evidence-based psychosocial interventions; (4) embracing a rights-based approach that eliminates coercion and violence in care; and (5) centring people with lived experience in all aspects of the care system [@patel2023].

To operationalise these principles, the authors recommend four policy actions: a whole-of-society approach to prevention and care that addresses structural determinants of mental health; redesigning the architecture of care delivery to provide a seamless continuum from community-based to specialist services; investing more and more wisely in what works, including task-sharing and community-based interventions; and ensuring accountability through monitoring and acting upon a set of mental health indicators, as exemplified by the Countdown Global Mental Health 2030 initiative [@patel2023]. The authors conclude that all these actions are achievable using resources already available in most communities and countries, requiring only the acceptance that business as usual has failed.

## Key Points

- The global mental health crisis is not solely due to lack of resources or political will, but stems from a narrow biomedical framing of mental ill health through categorical diagnoses that has dominated for 50 years
- Five principles for transformation: target harmful social environments across the life course; use a staging model rather than categorical diagnosis; empower front-line providers to deliver psychosocial interventions; embrace a rights-based approach eliminating coercion; and centre people with lived experience in all aspects of care
- Four policy actions: whole-of-society approach to prevention and care; redesign the architecture of care delivery for a seamless continuum; invest more and wisely in what works; ensure accountability through monitoring and indicators
- The staging model parallels other medical fields (e.g., oncology) by matching interventions to the stage of illness — from mild symptoms to enduring disability — rather than requiring a categorical diagnosis
- Task-sharing with diverse front-line providers (community health workers, peers, teachers) is relevant to all countries, not just low-resource settings, and should be seen as a permanent expansion of the workforce, not a stop-gap
- A rights-based approach requires eliminating coercion, implementing alternatives to violence in care, and aligning mental health care with the same quality standards as physical health care
- The Countdown Global Mental Health 2030 initiative provides a monitoring and accountability framework with indicators spanning determinants, systems, services, and outcomes

## Relevance

This paper synthesises the major Lancet Commissions and WHO reports on global mental health into a coherent, actionable framework. It is directly relevant for understanding the paradigm shift needed in mental health policy and practice — moving from a diagnosis-centred, specialist-driven model to a biopsychosocial, community-based, rights-oriented approach. The five principles and four policy actions provide a structured lens for evaluating mental health systems and designing interventions, particularly in low-resource and primary-care settings.
