Living barriers offer ecological and economic benefits over conventional walls. Ecologically, they sequester carbon, create microclimate cooling and shade, reduce rainfall runoff, remove air pollution, and provide wildlife habitat. Living roots prevent soil erosion along riverbanks and slopes [@wikipediacontributors2025a]. They are naturally self-renewing and self-strengthening, unlike manufactured fences that degrade. Economically, they use low-cost materials (cuttings, simple tools) and enable community participation without special skills [@kalberer2025]. They eliminate the need for manufactured or imported fencing materials. Baubotanik research confirms that living trees in urban architecture provide ecosystem services including cooling, carbon storage, air pollution removal, and runoff reduction [@mylo2023].
