## Summary

Moriano et al. (2021) examined how security providing leadership — leaders who function as attachment figures offering a secure base, safe haven, and emotional support — prevents employee burnout through two mediating pathways [@moriano2021]. In a sample of 655 Spanish employees across 130 organisations, the study used Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM) to test the model.

Key quantitative findings:

- **Security providing leadership was negatively related to burnout** (r=-0.32, p<0.01)
- **Psychological safety climate** mediated the relationship: security providing leadership positively predicted safety climate (β=0.51, p<0.001), which in turn negatively predicted burnout (β=-0.24, p<0.001)
- **Organisational dehumanization** mediated the relationship: security providing leadership negatively predicted dehumanization (β=-0.39, p<0.001), which positively predicted burnout (β=0.41, p<0.001)
- The overall model explained **35% of the variance in burnout**
- This was a **full mediation** — 84.9% of the total effect was explained by the two mediating pathways
- Security providing leadership had a large effect on safety climate (f²=0.35) and a medium effect on dehumanization (f²=0.18)

The study demonstrates that leaders who provide security function as a job resource within the JD-R framework. When employees perceive their leader as available, sensitive, and responsive, they experience greater psychological safety and feel valued as humans rather than interchangeable resources, reducing their risk of burnout.

## Key Points

- Security providing leadership negatively predicts burnout (r=-0.32, p<0.01)
- Effect is fully mediated by psychological safety climate and reduced organisational dehumanization
- Model explains 35% of burnout variance
- Large effect on safety climate (f²=0.35), medium effect on dehumanization (f²=0.18)
- Cross-sectional design limits causal inference
- Leadership style is a modifiable organisational factor that can be targeted in interventions
