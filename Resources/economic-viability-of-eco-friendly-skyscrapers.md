The economic case for eco-friendly skyscrapers combines operational savings, property premiums, and regulatory incentives. Green buildings save on average 19% of operational costs through reduced energy and water consumption [@alkodmany2018]. The Bank of America Tower in New York uses a 5.0 MW cogeneration plant providing 65% of annual electricity and an ice storage system providing 25% of cooling needs, reducing peak demand by 30% [@alkodmany2018]. Pearl River Tower in Guangzhou was designed as a 'zero-energy' skyscraper, using wind turbines, solar panels, and geothermal energy to produce as much power as it consumes [@alkodmany2018]. The main barriers are upfront costs — green walls require irrigation systems, specialized maintenance, and structural reinforcement. However, cities increasingly mandate green features: Singapore covers up to half the cost of greenery integration, Utrecht requires green roofs on all buildings, and NYC has proposed banning all-glass skyscrapers [@alkodmany2023]. Insurance and certification programs (LEED Platinum, BCA Green Mark) provide additional market validation.
