## Summary

The correlation between the Perceived Stress Scale (PSS-10/PSS-14) and psychological resilience measures (CD-RISC-10, BRS) consistently shows a moderate-to-strong negative relationship. Reported Pearson r values cluster in the range of -0.40 to -0.55, depending on the specific instruments and populations studied.

Kermott et al. @kermott2019 reported r = -0.55 between PSS-14 and CD-RISC-10 (10-item Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale) in a large corporate executive sample (n = 1,954). Yang et al. @yang2020 found r = -0.400 between PSS-14 and the full 25-item CD-RISC in a Chinese substance use disorder sample (n = 415). The Psychometric literature on PSS-10 convergent validity suggests correlations with resilience typically do not exceed |r| = 0.60, as resilience and perceived stress are related but distinct constructs.

## Key Points
- PSS-10/14 and CD-RISC correlate at r ≈ -0.40 to -0.55, indicating moderate negative association
- Higher perceived stress is consistently associated with lower resilience
- The magnitude suggests they share approximately 16-30% variance, confirming discriminant validity
- No studies report correlations exceeding |r| = 0.60, making the asserted bound of |r| < 0.6 in the test suite reasonable as an upper bound but the lower bound of zero in the ad-hoc entries is too restrictive

## Sources
