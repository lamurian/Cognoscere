The simulated resilience distribution (M=0.768, SD=0.054, range=[0.613, 0.875] on [0,1]) has a mean that aligns well with empirical CD-RISC-10 norms. The CD-RISC-25 general population mean was 80.4 (SD=12.8) on 0-100, or ~0.804 on [0,1] [@connor2003]. Large-scale Nordic data (DBDS, N=57,031) reports M=31.2 (SD=5.9) on 0-40, or ~0.78 (SD~0.148) [@didriksen2025]. The German CD-RISC-10 validation (N=360 students) found M=2.76 (SD=0.54) on 0-4, or ~0.69 (SD~0.135) with total score skew=-0.20 and kurtosis=0.004 [@wollny2021]. In Chinese military personnel (N=3,129), CD-RISC-10 means ranged 30.65-31.26 (SD 7.22-7.66) [@tu2023].

The simulated SD of 0.054 is 2.5-3.5x narrower than empirical SDs (0.128-0.188 on [0,1]), producing a compressed range that excludes both low-resilience and high-resilience extremes. Simulated skew (-0.281) is directionally correct but weaker than empirical values (CD-RISC-10 total score skew -0.20 to -0.94 across samples). In simulation terms, this near-homogeneous resilience distribution means resilience acts as a near-constant trait rather than a differentiating individual characteristic, potentially reducing emergent complexity in agent interactions driven by resilience-dependent coping behaviour.
