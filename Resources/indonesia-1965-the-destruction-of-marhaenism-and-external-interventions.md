## Overview

The destruction of Marhaenism — and the regime that embodied it — was not solely an internal Indonesian affair. The **1965–1966 massacres** and the transition to Suharto's New Order took place within the wider context of the **Cold War**, with the **United States** (and to a lesser extent Britain and Australia) actively supporting the Army's campaign against the PKI and Sukarno.[^1]

This note documents the external interventions that contributed to the destruction of Marhaenism and the installation of a Western-aligned regime in Indonesia.

---

## The Cold War Context

By 1964–1965, Indonesia under Sukarno had become one of the most vocal anti-Western states in the developing world:

- **Close alignment with China** — the Jakarta-Peking Axis (April 1965) aligned Indonesia with Mao's China
- **Konfrontasi with Malaysia** — military confrontation against a British-backed federation, seen as a "neo-colonial" project
- **Withdrawal from the UN** (January 1965) — the only country ever to leave the UN until the US later threatened to
- **Nationalisation of Western assets** — Shell, Stanvac, Goodyear, and other US/UK/Netherlands firms were seized
- **Anti-American rhetoric** — Sukarno notoriously declared "To hell with your aid" in 1964, rejecting US foreign assistance

This made Sukarno's Indonesia a prime target for US Cold War strategy. The US saw the growing PKI as a potential Southeast Asian domino that, if allowed to come to power, would deliver the region's largest country — with its population of 110 million and vast natural resources — to the communist bloc.[^1]

## US Covert Operations Before 1965

US involvement in Indonesia predated the 1965 crisis:

### 1957–1958: PRRI/Permesta Rebellion

The CIA actively supported the regional rebellions in Sumatra and Sulawesi (1957–1958), providing:
- Arms and ammunition to rebel forces
- Air support using CIA pilots (including the downing of Allen Pope's B-26)
- Funding and logistical support

The failure of the rebellion convinced the CIA that the Army — not external rebels — was the most effective vehicle for undermining Sukarno. From 1958 onward, US policy shifted to building relationships with anti-communist Army officers, particularly Nasution, Yani, and later Suharto.[^1]

### 1960–1964: Building Army Ties

- The US provided **military training and equipment** to the Indonesian Army through the International Military Education and Training (IMET) program
- Hundreds of Indonesian officers were trained in the US, including future leaders of the New Order
- The US cultivated personal relationships with key officers through the US Embassy's military attaché network
- The CIA maintained regular contact with Army intelligence (the future Kopkamtib)[^1]

## The 1965 Crisis: US Embassy Documents

In October 2017, the **National Security Archive at George Washington University** published 39 declassified documents from the US Embassy in Jakarta (1964–1968), drawn from a collection of 30,000 pages processed by the National Declassification Center. These documents provide unprecedented detail on US knowledge and involvement.[^1]

### Key Findings

**1. Detailed knowledge of the killings**

US Embassy officials in Jakarta had real-time, detailed knowledge of the Army's campaign of mass murder. Embassy despatches tracked:
- Which PKI leaders had been arrested or executed
- Where and how killings were taking place
- The involvement of Muslim youth groups (Ansor, NU) in carrying out executions
- Army estimates of the number killed (the Embassy noted 100,000+ by December 1965)[^1]

**2. Active support for the destruction of the labour movement**

The Embassy actively supported the Army's campaign to destroy the PKI-affiliated labour federation, **SOBSI** (*Sentral Organisasi Buruh Seluruh Indonesia*). Although Ambassador Marshall Green conceded it was "improbable" that SOBSI had any knowledge of the 30 September Movement, he noted that the Army considered it a threat to its consolidation of power.[^1]

**3. Covert aid to the Army**

In late October 1965, Green requested that the Johnson administration "explore possibility of short-term one shot aid on covert, non-attributable basis" as a sign of US support. This precipitated an expansion of US covert support including:
- Money
- Communications equipment
- Arms

By November 1965, the CIA was providing covert assistance to the Army's anti-PKI operations.[^1]

**4. Knowledge that the "Chinese plot" was fabricated**

US Embassy cables acknowledged internally that the claim that China and Mao Zedong had masterminded the 30 September Movement was a **"hoax"** — a propaganda fabrication by the Army. A 1966 cable from Ambassador Green stated: "We do not think the Chinese were a primary factor in the September 30 Movement." Yet the US did not publicly contradict the narrative, as it served Cold War objectives.[^1]

**5. Tracking the power transition**

Embassy documents closely tracked the political manoeuvring between Sukarno and Suharto, including:
- Army plans to oust Sukarno (mentioned as early as October 12, 1965)
- The creation of parallel Army structures at every level of administration in Java
- The use of the Army Military Police as "thought police" to purge the civil service
- Efforts to attract Western investment back to Indonesia through the 1967 Foreign Investment Law[^1]

## Britain's Role

Britain was a key player in the anti-Sukarno campaign through:

- **Konfrontasi operations** — British forces (including Gurkhas, SAS, and Royal Marines) conducted covert cross-border operations in Borneo against Indonesian infiltrators
- **Psychological warfare** — the British secretly broadcast propaganda into Indonesia, including the creation of a fake "Voice of Free Indonesia" radio station urging the Army to act against Sukarno
- **Secret cooperation with the Army** — British intelligence maintained contacts with anti-communist Army officers, sharing information about the PKI

British documents declassified in the 2000s confirmed that London was aware of and tacitly supported the Army's massacres, viewing the destruction of the PKI as a strategic victory.[^2]

## The New Order's Reward: Western Integration

Once Suharto consolidated power, the Western powers rapidly integrated Indonesia into the capitalist world system:

| Year | Development |
|------|------------|
| 1966 | IMF and World Bank begin providing emergency loans |
| 1967 | Foreign Investment Law passed, reopening Indonesia to multinationals |
| 1967 | IGGI (Inter-Governmental Group on Indonesia) formed — a Western-led aid consortium |
| 1968 | Return to the UN |
| 1970 | Resumption of diplomatic relations with Malaysia |
| 1970s | Oil boom: Pertamina becomes the engine of New Order patronage |
| Post-1975 | Indonesia becomes a key US ally in Southeast Asia after the Vietnam War |

The **technocratic team** around Suharto — the "Berkeley Mafia" of US-educated economists — implemented IMF/World Bank structural adjustment programs: deregulation, privatisation, foreign investment promotion, and fiscal austerity.[^3]

## Long-Term Consequences

The destruction of Marhaenism through external intervention had profound and lasting effects:

1. **Elimination of the left** — The PKI was the third-largest communist party in the world. Its complete destruction eliminated left-wing politics from Indonesia for over three decades, creating an enduring political vacuum on the left

2. **Militarised state** — The Army's political role, originally conceived as a "middle way" under Nasution, expanded to dominate every level of government under the New Order. Military officers held up to 89% of senior civil service positions by the 1980s[^3]

3. **Corruption as system** — Army officers used their positions in state enterprises and permit-granting agencies to extract rents, creating a system of systematic corruption that persisted for decades[^3]

4. **Authoritarian development model** — The New Order became a model for "authoritarian developmentalism" — rapid growth under political repression — that influenced other Southeast Asian regimes

5. **Historical amnesia** — For 32 years, the Suharto regime suppressed discussion of the 1965–66 massacres. The events remain a deeply contested and traumatic subject in Indonesian public discourse today
