## Summary

Evaluating whether opening new medical faculties and expanding specialist training programs actually improves healthcare delivery requires a multi-dimensional indicator framework that looks beyond simple graduate counts.

**Graduate production indicators**: Number of medical graduates per 100,000 population provides a baseline measure. In Finland, this was 10.4 per 100,000 in 2017, below the EU average of 13.9 [@schneider2022a]. For Indonesia, the current output of 12,000 GPs per year from all medical faculties represents approximately 4.3 per 100,000 population. The ratio of graduates to practising stock (5.4% in Finland vs 4.3% EU average) indicates replacement capacity [@schneider2022a].

**Workforce stock and density indicators**: Practising doctors per 1,000 population (with sub-indicators for GPs vs. specialists) is the most commonly tracked metric. Indonesia's 0.62 per 1,000 compares with Malaysia's 2.1, Thailand's 0.8, and the OECD average of 3.9 [@oecd2025]. The WHO threshold of 4.45 health workers per 1,000 provides a minimum benchmark, while the SDG indicator 3.c.1 tracks progress toward this target.

**Distribution equity indicators**: The Gini Index for specialist and GP distribution at district level is the most important indicator for Indonesia. The current inter-district Gini of 0.53 for specialists indicates severe maldistribution [@muharram2024]. The percentage of specialists working outside Java island and the proportion of puskesmas meeting minimum staffing requirements (currently only 65%) are practical proxy measures.

**Service delivery impact indicators**: Beyond workforce numbers, the percentage of hospitals with complete essential specialist coverage (currently 78.1%) directly measures whether training expansion improves service availability [@worldhealthorganizationindonesia2026]. Average wait times for specialist consultations, referral completion rates, and the proportion of patients receiving specialist care within their province of residence are outcome-oriented indicators.

**Quality and retention indicators**: Specialist board exam pass rates, early-career attrition rates, and proportion of bonded scholarship recipients who complete their return-of-service obligation are critical for assessing whether expansion maintains quality. Indonesia faces particular challenges ensuring that new graduates in underserved regions have adequate supervision, equipment, and infrastructure to practice effectively.

## Key Points

- Graduate production (per 100,000 population) and replacement ratio are baseline indicators
- Density (doctors/1,000) with GP vs specialist breakdown is the most commonly tracked metric
- Gini Index for within-country distribution is critical for Indonesia's archipelagic context
- Hospital and puskesmas staffing completion rates directly measure service delivery impact
- Retention and quality indicators (board pass rates, return-of-service compliance) prevent quantity-quality tradeoffs
