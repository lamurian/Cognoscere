Son cubano (or simply son) is a genre of music and dance that originated in the highlands of eastern Cuba in the late 19th century. It blends Spanish elements (tres guitar, vocal style, lyrical metre) with African traditions (clave rhythm, call-and-response, percussion). The word "son" derives from Latin sonus, meaning a pleasant sound. The genre evolved from rural styles like changüí and nengón, and around 1909 it reached Havana where it absorbed clave rhythm from rumba. The first recordings were made in 1917 by Cuarteto Oriental [@wikipediacontributors2025d].

Ensembles grew from trios to sextetos (1920s: guitar, tres, bongos, claves, maracas, double bass), then septetos (1930s with trumpet), and finally conjuntos (1940s with piano, congas, multiple trumpets). The clave rhythm (son clave in 2-3 or 3-2 pattern) is the structural core that all instruments reference. Key figures include Sexteto Habanero, Trío Matamoros, Ignacio Piñeiro, Beny Moré, and Arsenio Rodríguez. Son spawned Congolese rumba via radio broadcasts, and in New York during the 1960s it combined with other Latin styles to form salsa. Within Cuba it evolved into timba and songo. In 2025 UNESCO inscribed Cuban Son on its Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage [@wikipediacontributors2025d].
