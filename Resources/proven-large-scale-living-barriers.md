The Great Hedge of India (1840-1880) was a 2,500-mile continuous living barrier of thorny babul and Indian plum, reaching 12-14 feet tall and several feet wide. Built to prevent salt smuggling, it was maintained for decades and remained effective until India's independence [@moxham2001]. This remains the largest living barrier ever constructed.

Arthur Wiechula's 120-meter living wall of Canadian poplars in early 20th-century Germany served as a railway snow fence. His V-cut lateral bending technique bent tree trunks into precise shapes, with reaction wood closing wounds within one season and permanently locking curves [@wikipediacontributors2025c].

Modern Baubotanik (University of Stuttgart, 2007-present) has built the Plane Tree Cube -- a three-story living tower with crosswise-connected stems. Steel scaffolding supports structures during establishment and is removed once root anchorage develops [@mylo2023].

## Key Points
- Great Hedge: 2,500 miles of impenetrable thorn barrier.
- Wiechula: 120m V-cut living wall, self-healing curves.
- Baubotanik: three-story load-bearing living tower.
- All three prove engineered living barriers work at scale.
