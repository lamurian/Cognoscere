Indonesia's economy has grown ~5% annually for two decades, yet the Gini remains high at 0.381 — sixth globally in income disparity [@ong2025]. Benefits of growth have flowed primarily to the richest 20%, leaving 80% behind [@worldbank2016].\n\n**WHY inequality persists.** Three interconnected structural factors drive this. [[root-causes-of-indonesia-s-high-income-inequality]]: FDI concentrates in Java and capital-intensive sectors, corruption skews resources toward elites, and 50 billionaires grew wealth from US$152B to US$323B (2019-2024) while wages grew only 15% [@askar2024]. [[inequality-of-opportunity-and-human-capital-gaps-in-indonesia]]: 37% child stunting, bottom-5% PISA scores, and declining family planning perpetuate intergenerational poverty [@worldbank2016]. [[regional-and-spatial-inequality-in-indonesia]]: urban Gini (0.395) far exceeds rural (0.299), eastern Indonesia lacks basic infrastructure [@lpemfebui2024].\n\n**HOW to fix it.** Four evidence-based pathways. [[progressive-taxation-and-wealth-tax-for-indonesia]]: raise 10% tax-to-GDP ratio toward 19.8% Asia-Pacific average; a 2% wealth tax on the top 50 could raise US$5B/year [@askar2024; @bridgegip2026]. [[social-spending-and-human-capital-investment-for-inequality]]: expand CCTs, universal health coverage, and skills training — currently reaching only 1% of youth [@worldbank2016]. [[land-reform-and-inclusive-industrial-policy-for-indonesia]]: redistribute 68% of land held by 1%, reform rigid labor regulations, and ensure downstreaming benefits local communities [@askar2024; @worldbank2016].\n\n**Confidence:** WHY findings — HIGH (supported by World Bank, AFD/LPEM FEB UI, CELIOS, and academic econometric studies). HOW findings — MODERATE-HIGH (taxation and social spending reforms have strong cross-country evidence, Indonesia-specific implementation evidence still emerging). [@ong2025; @askar2024; @worldbank2016; @lpemfebui2024]
