Indonesia ranks sixth globally in income disparity despite decades of 5% GDP growth [@ong2025]. Four structural factors drive this. FDI inflows have a statistically significant positive relationship with inequality — FDI concentrates in capital-intensive sectors and Java, creating high-wage jobs for a minority while most remain in low-wage positions [@ong2025].

Corruption is a significant driver: a 1% increase in the Corruption Perception Index correlates with 0.07% higher inequality. Indonesia ranks 115th/180 on Transparency International's CPI, and weakened justice systems allow wealthy elites to control legal systems [@ong2025].

The extractive industry concentrates wealth dramatically — half of the 50 richest Indonesians operate in mining, accumulating US$142 billion [@askar2024]. The top 50 billionaires' wealth grew from US$152B (2019) to US$323B (2024), while workers' wages grew only 15% [@askar2024]. Four cabinet ministers with mining affiliations hold US$825 million combined. Population growth increases labor supply pressure, pushing workers into low-paying jobs [@ong2025].
