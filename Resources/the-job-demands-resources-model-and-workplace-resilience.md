The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model provides a complementary framework for understanding resilience in working populations. According to Bakker and Demerouti [@bakker2017a], job demands (workload, emotional demands, role conflict) can lead to burnout and health impairment, while job resources (autonomy, social support, feedback, skill variety) foster engagement and well-being. Resilience acts as a personal resource that moderates the relationship between job demands and strain.

Longitudinal evidence from healthcare professionals during COVID-19 demonstrates that day-level interactions between challenge and hindrance demands affect work engagement and emotional exhaustion, with resilience serving as a buffer [@vignoli2024]. Employees with higher resilience show less reactivity to daily hindrance demands and recover more quickly from stress exposure.

The mediating role of resilience was demonstrated in a cross-sectional study of 333 physician educators in Hong Kong, where resilience partially mediated the relationship between well-being and psychological distress, while average working hours had a direct positive effect on distress [@wong2026]. Age, relationship status, and years as an educator were not significantly associated with resilience or distress, suggesting that modifiable factors (working hours, well-being) are more important than demographic characteristics.

Organisational interventions should focus on reducing hindrance demands, enhancing job resources, and building individual resilience through evidence-based programmes that target optimism and self-compassion [@hannibal2026].
