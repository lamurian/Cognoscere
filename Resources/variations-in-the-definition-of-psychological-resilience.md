## Summary

Psychological resilience lacks a single agreed-upon definition across the empirical literature. Three broad conceptualisations dominate: resilience as a **trait** (a stable personal quality that helps individuals bounce back), resilience as a **process** (a dynamic interaction between person and environment over time), and resilience as an **outcome** (successful adaptation following adversity) [@aburn2016].

Aburn et al. @aburn2016 conducted an integrative review of the empirical literature and found no universal definition of resilience. They identified five common themes: rising above adversity, adaptation and adjustment, dynamic process, ordinary magic (ordinary rather than extraordinary processes), and mental illness as a marker of resilience. The absence of a standard definition creates a jingle-jangle problem — the same term is used for different constructs and different terms are used for the same construct — which complicates cross-study comparison and evidence synthesis.

Masten et al. @masten2021 frame resilience through a multisystem lens, arguing that resilience depends on interactions across individual, family, community, cultural, and ecological systems. This perspective shifts the definition from an individual trait toward a distributed, context-dependent process. Troy et al. @troy2023 propose an affect-regulation framework, defining resilience as the capacity to regulate emotions effectively in the face of adversity. Each framework emphasises different mechanisms and measurement strategies, reflecting the field's ongoing fragmentation.

The choice of definition has practical consequences. Trait-based definitions favour dispositional measures such as the Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC). Process-based definitions require longitudinal designs that capture person-environment interactions. Outcome-based definitions focus on observable functioning after adversity. Studies that mismatch their definition with their measurement instrument risk internal inconsistency, as documented in recent systematic reviews.
