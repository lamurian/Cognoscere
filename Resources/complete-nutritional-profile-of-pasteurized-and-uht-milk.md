## Summary

One cup (240 mL / ~244 g) of pasteurized whole cow's milk (3.25% fat) provides approximately 149 kcal of energy, with 8 g protein, 11.3 g carbohydrate (entirely as lactose, no added sugars), and 7.8 g fat (4.5 g saturated, 1.7 g monounsaturated, 0.3 g polyunsaturated). It contains 29 mg cholesterol, 300 mg calcium (23% DV), 246 mg phosphorus, 366 mg potassium, 29 mg magnesium, 93 mg sodium, 1 mg zinc, and 4.6 mcg selenium [@dairyuk2025].

In terms of vitamins, pasteurized whole milk provides: vitamin A 78 mcg RAE (9% DV), vitamin D 2.7 mcg (14% DV, when fortified), vitamin B12 1.3 mcg (55% DV), riboflavin 0.34 mg (26% DV), thiamin 0.14 mg (11% DV), vitamin B6 0.15 mg (9% DV), niacin 0.26 mg (2% DV), folate 8–12 mcg, choline 43 mg, vitamin E 0.12 mg, vitamin C 0–2 mg, and iodine approximately 50 mcg. Milk is naturally low in iron (trace), copper (trace), and manganese (trace), and contains no dietary fibre [@dairyuk2025].

Compared to pasteurized milk, UHT (ultra-high temperature) processed semi-skimmed milk has a very similar macronutrient profile (same protein, carbohydrate, and fat content) but shows minor losses in heat-sensitive vitamins. Per 240 mL, UHT milk has lower folate (~5 mcg vs ~19 mcg in pasteurized semi-skimmed), lower vitamin B12 (0.7 mcg vs 0.96 mcg), lower pantothenate (0.82 mg vs 1.68 mg), lower biotin (4.4 mcg vs 7.4 mcg), and virtually no measurable vitamin C (trace vs ~5 mg). However, UHT milk has slightly higher vitamin E (0.74 mg vs 0.10 mg). Mineral content including calcium, potassium, magnesium, phosphorus, and iodine is comparable between both processing methods [@dairyuk2025]. These differences are small and do not meaningfully affect milk's status as a nutrient-dense food providing 13 essential nutrients per serving.
