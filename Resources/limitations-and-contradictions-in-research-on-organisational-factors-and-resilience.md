The evidence base for organisational-level factors modulating individual resilience mechanisms has several important limitations and contradictions.

Conceptual clarity remains a major issue. Britt et al. (2016) argue that much employee resilience research suffers from a lack of definitional precision -- studies often fail to ensure that participants have actually experienced significant adversity before measuring 'resilience' [@britt2016]. Without verifying adversity exposure, studies risk measuring positive adjustment in general rather than resilience specifically. Hollaar et al. (2025) found that 46% of reviewed studies showed inconsistencies between their definition of resilience, intervention content, and measurement instruments -- the jingle-jangle problem [@hollaar2025].

The Challenge-Hindrance Stress Model faces significant empirical challenges. Mazzola and Disselhorst (2019) meta-analysis found limited support for the model's core predictions: challenge and hindrance stressors showed differential relationships with task performance but not with citizenship behaviour, counterproductive behaviour, job attitudes, retention, or strain [@mazzola2019]. This suggests that the simple challenge-hindrance distinction may not adequately capture how job demands affect resilience mechanisms. Horan et al. (2020) advocate a paradigm shift incorporating explicit measurement of individual appraisals rather than a priori stressor classification, acknowledging that the same stressor can be appraised simultaneously as both challenge and hindrance.

The PASTOR theory's emphasis on positive appraisal has been critiqued for potentially overemphasising positivity. Freund and Staudinger (2015) argue that negative appraisals can serve adaptive functions and that accurate appraisal -- matching strategy to context -- may be more important than uniformly positive appraisal [@freund2015]. This applies to the organisational context: a learning-oriented climate that supports accurate diagnosis of problems may be more effective than one that merely promotes positive reframing.

Causal evidence is limited. Most studies on organisational factors and employee resilience are cross-sectional, precluding causal conclusions about whether organisational factors cause changes in resilience mechanisms or whether resilient employees select into better organisational environments. Longitudinal and experimental studies remain rare. Social support's effects may be bidirectional: initial support buffers stress, but prolonged distress can erode support networks over time [@sippel2015].

Self-report bias is pervasive. Most studies rely on self-report measures of both organisational factors and resilience, inflating associations through common method variance. Objective measures of organisational factors (archival data on turnover, climate audits) and resilience (behavioural indicators, physiological markers, supervisor ratings) are needed. Neurobiological evidence linking organisational factors to resilience mechanisms in working populations is almost entirely absent, with most evidence drawn from general population, clinical, or animal studies.

Cultural and contextual generalisability is unknown. The majority of evidence comes from East Asian and Western high-income contexts. How organisational factors modulate resilience in low- and middle-income country contexts remains unexplored [@sippel2015].
