## Summary

Biomimetic urban homesteads deliver environmental benefits beyond individual resource savings. Buildings designed as regenerative systems actively restore urban biodiversity, sequester carbon, purify air and water, and regulate microclimate [@pedersenzari2020]. Council House 2 in Melbourne demonstrates this: designed as a 'tree' that filters air, regulates temperature, and produces its own energy, achieving a regenerative effect on its surroundings [@verbrugghe2023]. Urban ecosystem services from biomimetic buildings reduce pressure on distant ecosystems by localising resource production and waste treatment.

Social benefits include enhanced biophilic wellbeing — our innate connection to nature is strengthened when buildings incorporate living systems. The French Biomim'City Lab found biodiversity hosting was the second-most-cited objective (13 of 16 projects) after thermal comfort [@blanco2021]. Studies show nature access in urban environments reduces stress, improves cognitive function, and strengthens community bonds [@pedersenzari2020]. Climate resilience is another critical benefit: a biomimetic off-grid homestead with decentralised water, energy, and food systems is inherently more resilient to grid failures and extreme weather. The Sahara Forest Project proves this by producing food, fresh water, renewable energy, and regenerating desertified land simultaneously in a closed-loop system [@verbrugghe2023].

## Key Points
- Regenerative buildings restore ecosystems, not just reduce harm
- Biodiversity hosting and thermal comfort are top objectives in practice [@blanco2021]
- Biophilic outcomes emerge naturally from biomimetic design
- Decentralised off-grid systems provide resilience against grid disruptions
