Geothermal energy extracts thermal energy from the Earth's crust, combining residual heat from planetary formation and ongoing radioactive decay. As of 2025, 16 GW of geothermal power was available worldwide. An additional 28 GW provided direct heat for district heating, spas, and industrial processes.

## Power Generation Technologies

**Dry Steam Plants**: Direct geothermal steam to turn turbines. The simplest and oldest design, used at Larderello (Italy, since 1911) and The Geysers (California). Requires high-temperature reservoirs (240-300C).

**Flash Steam Plants**: Most common for high-temperature liquid-dominated reservoirs (over 180C). Hot water is flashed to steam under reduced pressure to drive turbines. Cerro Prieto (Mexico) generates 750 MW using this method.

**Binary Cycle Plants**: Use a secondary working fluid (organic compound) with a lower boiling point than water. Heat from geothermal water vaporizes the working fluid via a heat exchanger, which drives a turbine. Enables power generation from lower-temperature resources (as low as 57C). Dominates new installations.

## Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)

EGS injects water under high pressure into hot dry rock to create or expand fissures, enabling heat extraction where natural permeability is insufficient. Uses directional drilling and proppants (sand, ceramic) to keep fractures open. Demonstration projects operate in France, Germany, and Australia. Potential to vastly expand geothermal's geographic range.

## Closed-Loop Geothermal Systems

Next-generation systems like Eavor's technology circulate a working fluid through a closed loop of deeply buried pipes, eliminating the need for natural permeability, geofluid, and reducing induced seismicity risk. Produces heat (and potentially electricity) in sedimentary basins.

## Geothermal Heat Pumps (GSHP)

Ground-source heat pumps use shallow ground temperatures (10-16C at 6m depth) for heating and cooling. Highly efficient (300-500% efficiency ratio) and applicable almost anywhere. As of 2015, they provided over 70 GW of heating capacity globally.

## Economics and Sustainability

Geothermal LCOE averages $0.05/kWh (US DOE 2021 estimate). Capital costs dominate (drilling accounts for over half). Plants produce base-load power at 80-95% capacity factor with minimal intermittency. Resource depletion can occur over decades if extraction exceeds replenishment, but can be managed through injection and reduced production.

## Environmental Impact

Geothermal plants emit about 45 g CO2/kWh on average -- less than 5% of coal. Land use is minimal (3.5 km2 per GW) compared to coal (32 km2) or wind (12 km2). Closed-loop designs can achieve zero emissions. Induced seismicity and subsidence are risks requiring careful site management.
