## Summary

IMTA provides measurable ecological benefits by converting waste into valuable biomass. Fed fish convert feed into biomass but excrete dissolved nitrogen (ammonia) and phosphorus. Seaweeds absorb these dissolved nutrients, with genera like Gracilaria and Saccharina showing high bioremediation potential [@nissar2023]. Bivalves filter particulate organic matter, reducing benthic enrichment. A 100 ha Gracilaria farm can theoretically remove 80% of dissolved nitrogen from a 1,500 ton salmon farm [@buck2018].

Key mechanisms: (1) seaweed nutrient uptake — species-specific removal rates for ammonium, nitrate, and phosphate; (2) bivalve filtration — mussels and oysters capture suspended solids and phytoplankton; (3) deposit-feeder processing — sea cucumbers and polychaetes consume organic sediments, reducing benthic impact [@buck2018; @nissar2023].

Long-term marine ecosystem benefits include reduced eutrophication risk, enhanced biodiversity around farm structures, and improved assimilative capacity. IMTA also reduces disease and parasite risks through dilution effects from multi-species cultivation [@buck2018]. However, scaling requires careful site selection — offshore nutrient concentrations may limit seaweed growth, and the ratio of extractive to fed species must be optimized for each location.
