## GDP and GDP per Capita: Soeharto vs Prabowo Indonesia

### Soeharto Era (1966-1998)

Under Soeharto, Indonesia's GDP grew from a very low base. Nominal GDP reached US$99.3 billion in 1980 and climbed to US$274.7 billion by 1996. GDP per capita in nominal terms rose from ~US$70 in 1966 to US$1,394 in 1996. In PPP terms, GDP per capita grew from US$806 (1966) to US$5,136 (1997). Indonesia's share of world GDP (PPP) increased from 1.24% in 1980 to 1.68% in 1990. The economy dramatically expanded its manufacturing sector from less than 10% of GDP (1966) to 25% (1997). By 1996, Indonesia was classified as a lower-middle-income country on the verge of upper-middle-income status.

### Prabowo Era (2024-present)

Indonesia's nominal GDP in 2026 is projected at US$1.54 trillion with nominal GDP per capita of US$5,362. In PPP terms, GDP per capita reaches US$18,973. Indonesia ranks 17th globally by nominal GDP and 7th by PPP. The country is classified as upper-middle-income by the World Bank. Indonesia's share of world GDP (PPP) reached 2.42% in 2025. The economy remains dominated by services (45.4%), industry (41%), and agriculture (13.7%).

### Key Differences

- **Scale**: Indonesia's economy today (US$1.54 trillion) is about 5.6 times larger than Soeharto's peak nominal GDP of US$274.7 billion (1996).
- **Income level**: Soeharto took Indonesia from low-income to lower-middle-income. Prabowo inherited upper-middle-income status.
- **Economic complexity**: Manufacturing share of GDP is similar (~25%) but services have grown significantly.
- **Global standing**: Under Soeharto, Indonesia was an emerging Asian tiger. Under Prabowo, Indonesia is a G20 member and BRICS member with vastly more global integration.

### Similarities

- Both eras saw steady expansion in GDP per capita, improving living standards over time.
- Both benefited from natural resource wealth (oil under Soeharto, coal/palm oil/nickel under current era).
- Both experienced currency depreciation against the US dollar over their periods.
- Manufacturing as a share of GDP has remained structurally important in both eras.
