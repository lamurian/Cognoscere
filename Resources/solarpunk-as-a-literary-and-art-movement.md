As a literary subgenre, solarpunk emerged as a cyberpunk derivative projecting a utopian counterpoint to cyberpunk's dystopian warnings. Where cyberpunk depicts alienation under hypercapitalism and technology, solarpunk depicts maker-heroes who have witnessed environmental disaster and work to build sustainable communities.

**Key literary works:**
- Proto-solarpunk: Ursula K. Le Guin's 'The Dispossessed' (1974) and 'Always Coming Home' (1985), Ernest Callenbach's 'Ecotopia' (1975), Kim Stanley Robinson's 'Pacific Edge' (1990)
- First explicit anthologies: 'Solarpunk: Ecological and Fantastical Stories in a Sustainable World' (2012), 'Wings of Renewal' (2015), 'Sunvault' (2017), 'Glass and Gardens' (2018)
- Becky Chambers' Monk & Robot series: 'A Psalm for the Wild-Built' (2021) and 'A Prayer for the Crown-Shy' (2022)

**Visual aesthetic:**
Solarpunk art draws on Art Nouveau (sinuous lines, plant motifs), Arts and Crafts movement (ornamental integration), and Frank Lloyd Wright's organic architecture. Colours are natural greens and blues with diverse cultural references. The aesthetic contrasts sharply with cyberpunk's dark, grim palette.

**Real-world architectural examples:**
- Bosco Verticale (Vertical Forest) in Milan - Boeri Studio's residential towers covered in trees and shrubs
- Wakanda in Marvel's Black Panther films - Afrofuturist-solarpunk fusion
- Studio Ghibli films, particularly 'Nausicaa of the Valley of the Wind' and 'Castle in the Sky'
- Earthship sustainable homes built from recycled materials

**Criticism:** Scholar Adam Flynn warns of 'greenwashing' - luxury condominiums with green roofs that price out communities and cause greater environmental harm being marketed as solarpunk. Authentic solarpunk addresses root causes of environmental issues rather than surface aesthetics.
