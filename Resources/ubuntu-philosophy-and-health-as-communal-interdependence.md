## Summary

Ubuntu philosophy, rooted in Southern African Bantu languages and articulated by scholars like John Mbiti, Desmond Tutu, and Kwame Gyekye, centres on the principle "I am because we are" [@udah2025]. This directly supports the user's argument that health systems must start from the individual and expand to community, and that curing a patient requires caring for their immediate social contacts.

In Ubuntu, personhood is relational: "a person is a person through other people" [@udah2025]. The self is constituted through relationships with family, community, environment, and ancestors. This ontology has profound implications for healthcare. If individual identity is inseparable from community, then individual health cannot be separated from community health. Ill health in one person represents a disruption in the relational fabric that affects all connected to them.

The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated Ubuntu in practice: in Lagos's Makoko settlement, community leaders organized resource-sharing networks, distributing masks and coordinating health education—less formalized than European institutional responses but fostering social cohesion where state infrastructure was limited [@anyikwa2025]. Similarly, Kenyan Harambee (collective fundraising) and Ugandan burial aid groups illustrate how Ubuntu operationalizes collective responsibility for health as a daily practice [@udah2025].

Ubuntu's emphasis on relationality shifts the focus toward the ethical obligation to care for others, particularly the most vulnerable. This directly addresses the user's point about healthcare being delivered on the basis of trust from and towards society. Trust in Ubuntu societies is built through mutual recognition and reciprocity—the greeting "salibonani" ("we have seen each other") acknowledges that one cannot be well when another is not well [@udah2025].

## Key Points

- Ubuntu defines personhood as inherently relational: individual health is inseparable from community health.
- The principle of recognition and reciprocity means trust is foundational to any health intervention.
- Communal support systems (Harambee, burial aid groups, stokvels) operationalize collective responsibility for health.
- Ubuntu aligns with the user's argument: curing a patient means caring for their immediate social contacts because the self is constituted through those relationships.
- Hybridisation with European institutional frameworks offers a path for inclusive global health systems.
