All living hedges and fences benefit from the trapezoid pruning rule: keep the base wider than the top so sunlight reaches lower leaves. Cutting sides vertically causes top branches to shade out the bottom, resulting in a hedge that is thick on top but bare and leggy at ground level [@gardengreenland2026]. For formal hedges, use a string line to guide straight cuts and trim frequently (every 4-6 weeks in growing season) for a crisp finish [@plantgrowerworld2025].

For informal living fences (like willow fedges), the trapezoid shape is still important but less rigid — the goal is maintaining density and preventing bare patches. Thinning — reaching inside to remove individual branches at their origin — allows air and light into the center and reduces disease pressure [@gardengreenland2026]. Shearing (hedge-trimming the surface) is faster but creates a dense outer shell that eventually blocks light from the interior. A mix of annual thinning plus seasonal surface trimming produces the healthiest living fence over its lifespan.
