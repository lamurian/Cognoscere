## Summary

Thailand achieved universal health coverage in 2001-2002 through the introduction of the Universal Coverage Scheme (UCS), also known as the Gold Card or 30-baht scheme. The UCS extended existing public health insurance to cover the uninsured 30% of the population, building on earlier schemes including the Medical Welfare Scheme (1975) and Voluntary Health Card Scheme (1983) [@yiengprugsawan2010].

The UCS is a tax-funded, non-contributory scheme administered by the National Health Security Office (NHSO). It uses capitation payments as the primary financing mechanism for ambulatory care and Diagnosis Related Groups for inpatient care. At launch, a 30-baht co-payment (~US $0.75) was required per visit, which was abolished in 2006. The scheme is directly funded from the national budget and allocated on a mixed per-capita basis [@yiengprugsawan2010].

Key design features include mandatory registration at a primary care facility, with the primary care network acting as gatekeeper to higher-level hospitals. The benefit package has three components: a curative package covering most common diagnoses, a high-cost care package, and a preventive package. The UCS relies on a solid primary healthcare foundation built over the preceding 2-3 decades, including the district health system and community hospital network [@yiengprugsawan2010].

Thailand also implements mandatory rural service for new medical graduates, requiring them to work in public facilities in underserved areas for a specified period. This policy, combined with the UCS's capitation-based contracting model that incentivizes primary care over specialist care, ensures equitable distribution of health services alongside the VHV network.

## Key Points

- UCS launched in 2001-2002 covering previously uninsured 30% of population
- Tax-funded, non-contributory, administered by NHSO
- Capitation payments for ambulatory care, DRG for inpatient care
- Primary care gatekeeping: patients must register at a contracted primary care unit
- Built on 2-3 decades of prior primary care infrastructure investment
- Catastrophic health payments dropped from 31.0% of households (2000) to 14.6% (2004)
- Impoverishment due to health payments fell from 1.01% to 0.49% of households [@yiengprugsawan2010]
- Mandatory rural service for new graduates ensures workforce distribution
