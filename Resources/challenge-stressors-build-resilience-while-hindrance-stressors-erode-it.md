Crane, Searle, and Dollard [@crane2016] applied the challenge-hindrance stressor framework to investigate how different types of workplace stressors affect psychological resilience. Challenge stressors — demands that are demanding but potentially rewarding, such as high workload, time pressure, and job responsibility — were found to promote the capacity for resilience over time. Hindrance stressors — demands that are perceived as obstructing personal growth and goal attainment, such as role ambiguity, organisational politics, and red tape — eroded resilient functioning.

In a two-wave longitudinal design with 208 working adults (48.1% female) over three months, Time 1 challenge stressors significantly predicted higher psychological resilience at Time 2. Conversely, Time 1 hindrance stressors positively predicted strain and negatively predicted psychological resilience at follow-up. Resilience partially mediated the relationship between Time 1 stressors and Time 2 strain, suggesting that resilience is a mechanism through which stressor type influences well-being outcomes.

This research provides a nuanced understanding of workplace stress by showing that not all stress is harmful — certain types of manageable stress can actually build adaptive capacity. The findings have implications for job design: organisations should aim to reduce hindrance stressors while providing employees with adequate resources to meet challenge demands.
