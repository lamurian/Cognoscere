A longitudinal study of UK trainee and newly qualified teachers examined interpretation bias and emotion regulation as cognitive mechanisms of resilience [@gomersall2025]. Across two one-year longitudinal studies (Study 1 commenced pre-COVID, Study 2 during COVID), positive interpretation bias — the tendency to interpret ambiguous situations in a positive way — consistently predicted resilience over time.

The research was grounded in the cognitive model of resilience, which proposes that biased information processing shapes how individuals appraise and respond to stressors. Both positive interpretation bias and adaptive emotion regulation strategies (such as cognitive reappraisal) were assessed alongside resilience, short-term stress, and chronic perceived stress at baseline, 8-month, and 13-month follow-ups.

Findings showed cross-sectional and longitudinal relationships between perceived stress, cognitive mechanisms, and resilience. Positive interpretation bias emerged as a particularly strong predictor of resilience trajectories, suggesting it is a promising target for intervention. These findings align with PASTOR theory [@kalisch2015], which identifies positive situation classification as one of three core cognitive processes underlying resilience.
