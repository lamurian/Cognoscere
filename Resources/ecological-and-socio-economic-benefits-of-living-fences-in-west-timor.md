Living fences (pagar hidup) serve multiple critical functions in West Timor's dryland agricultural systems. The region faces severe soil erosion due to sloping terrain, thin soils (<20cm solum depth), and intense monsoon rainfall followed by extended dry periods [@ngongo2022]. Hedgerow systems using legume trees like Gliricidia, Leucaena, and Sesbania significantly reduce erosion by slowing surface water flow, increasing infiltration rates (up to 0.95 cm/hour with gamal versus 0.59 without), and adding organic matter that improves soil structure [@matheus2009].

Beyond soil conservation, living fences provide essential livestock fodder during the critical August-November dry season when grasses are scarce. Lannea, Gliricidia, Leucacena, Pterocarpus, and other fence species are regularly lopped for cattle and goat feed [@reville2017; @ngongo2022]. Leucaena leaves contain 25% protein, making them a high-quality supplement. Farmers in Amarasi, West Timor, have successfully adopted Leucaena leucocephala cv. Tarramba specifically for integrated cattle feed and soil improvement systems [@ngongo2022].

Living fences also create wildlife corridors in deforested landscapes, host beneficial insects and birds, provide firewood, medicinal plants, fruits, and construction materials [@reville2017]. In NTT where 92% of agricultural land is dryland, these multiple functions are economically significant for subsistence farmers. The traditional Timorese farming system already integrates living fences through the mamar system (local agroforestry) and mixed-cropping practices, demonstrating that living fences are not a foreign technology but an indigenous practice that can be strengthened with improved species selection [@ngongo2022].
