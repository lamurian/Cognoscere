## Summary

Two primary design approaches exist: top-down (problem-first: identify a challenge, seek biological analogies) and bottom-up (solution-first: discover a biological phenomenon, find applications) [@verbrugghe2023]. For urban homesteads, the top-down approach is more practical — start with off-grid requirements (water independence, passive heating/cooling, on-site energy) and search nature for proven solutions. The Biomimicry Institute's Design Spiral formalises this process: define, biologise, discover via AskNature database, abstract, emulate, and evaluate [@verbrugghe2023]. BioGen framework further identifies 'pinnacle' organisms (termites for ventilation, cacti for water storage) and analyses their morphology and behaviour to extract design principles.

Borham's systematic review of 53 source documents identified 184 biomimetic strategies across 9 city systems and 3 scales [@borham2024]. For an urban homestead, key strategies include: passive stack ventilation (S039, inspired by termites), rainwater harvesting (S150), greywater treatment (S157), solar PV integration (S007), green walls (S126), and the critical strategy S184: 'self-sustaining off-grid system (energy, water)'. These strategies are documented with precedent case studies, providing a practical toolkit.

## Key Points
- Top-down approach suits homestead design: identify needs, then find biological models
- Design Spiral and BioGen provide systematic translation methods
- 184 strategies across 9 city systems offer a practical toolkit [@borham2024]
- Strategy S184 specifically addresses self-sustaining off-grid systems
