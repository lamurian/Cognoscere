The public health approach to mental health shifts focus from individual treatment to population-level strategies that address upstream determinants. The CDC Mental Health Strategy takes a primary prevention approach focusing on the drivers of well-being and mental distress, aiming to prevent mental health conditions before they develop or worsen [@centersfordiseasecontrolandprevention2026]. Key pillars include promoting well-being, improving access to supports and opportunities, strengthening surveillance systems, and engaging multisector partners.

A core principle is addressing social determinants of mental health — poverty, housing insecurity, education, employment, and discrimination — which shape mental health outcomes across populations. Public policy interventions can reduce stress among low-income groups through measures such as improving housing security, strengthening social safety nets, and investing in early childhood development. Health equity is central: populations disproportionately affected include racial and ethnic minority groups, sexual minorities, people experiencing homelessness, justice-involved individuals, and rural communities [@centersfordiseasecontrolandprevention2026].

Community interventions that address structural factors and social determinants have the greatest potential for large-scale impact. The WHO guidance similarly emphasizes that mental health is closely linked to social and structural determinants, requiring cross-sector collaboration across health, education, labor, housing, and social protection sectors [@worldhealthorganization2025]. Evidence shows that embedding mental health promotion and prevention interventions at key developmental stages — pregnancy, early childhood, adolescence, and old age — can break intergenerational cycles of poverty and mental ill-health by improving resilience and self-regulation in high-risk contexts [@southafricadepartmentofhealth2023].
