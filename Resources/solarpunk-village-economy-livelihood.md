# Solarpunk Village: Economy & Livelihood

A solarpunk village sits within a **post-growth, regenerative economy**. Work is organised around livelihood, not profit maximisation. Surplus is reinvested in community and ecological restoration.[^1][^2]

## Principles

1. **Livelihood over employment** — multiple income streams, reduced consumption, work-integrated community life
2. **Degrowth compatible** — the village measures success by well-being, not GDP[^1]
3. **Circular flows** — materials, food, energy, and money cycle locally before reaching external markets
4. **Right livelihood** — work aligns with ecological and social values
5. **Shared prosperity** — income sharing or partial sharing reduces inequality

## Income Models

Ecovillages use three income models, often in combination:[^1]

| Model | Prevalence (Kasper 2008) | Description |
|-------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Separate income** | Most common | Members earn independently, contribute fees to community |
| **Partial sharing** | Some | Some income pooled for shared expenses (food, infrastructure) |
| **Full sharing** | Rare (1 of 8 studied) | All income pooled, needs-based allocation |

Ecovillage A (full sharing) achieved >50% food self-sufficiency, communal vehicle ownership, and the smallest ecological footprint.[^1]

## On-Site Livelihoods

A mature village of 50–150 can support a range of internal enterprises:

| Enterprise | Jobs | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| **Market garden / CSA** | 3–6 | Vegetable box schemes for members + local town |
| **Bakery / café** | 2–3 | Dancing Rabbit has a small restaurant/social lounge[^1] |
| **Value-added food** | 1–2 | Ferments, preserves, cheese, baked goods |
| **Natural building crew** | 2–4 | Contract out to neighbouring properties for income |
| **Workshop / education** | 1–3 | Permaculture courses, natural building workshops, ecovillage tours[^2] |
| **Craft / woodwork** | 1–2 | Furniture, tools, artisanal goods |
| **Remote work infrastructure** | (shared) | Coworking space with high-speed internet; members work remotely |

Dancing Rabbit's cottage industries include a general store/internet café, restaurant, and commercial leaseholds for food production. However, Brent (co-owner of the store/café) noted it was "nearly impossible to make a go of it in such a small community" — a common challenge.[^1]

## Complementary Currencies

Many ecovillages use or experiment with local currencies:

- **Timebanking** — 1 hour = 1 time-credit, exchangeable for any other hour of labour
- **Local Exchange Trading System (LETS)** — mutual credit without physical currency[^1]
- **Community scrip** — physical currency usable only within the village
- **Chore credits** — mandatory contribution tracking for rotating duties

These tools keep value circulating locally and buffer against external economic shocks.

## Land Trust Finance

The land trust model creates a permanently affordable land base:

1. Land is purchased collectively (often through loans from member contributions + grants)
2. Members lease plots at affordable monthly fees (instead of buying land)
3. Fees cover property tax, insurance, shared infrastructure, and trust administration
4. Members own buildings and improvements, which can be sold to incoming members
5. No land speculation — the community retains ownership in perpetuity[^2]

> "Since members can't sell their land, there is no ability to speculate and land can be a permanently affordable resource." — Dancing Rabbit[^2]

## External Income

Most ecovillagers still rely partly on external income, especially in early years:

- Remote tech/consulting work
- Teaching and academia
- Healthcare and therapy
- Craft sales at regional markets
- Seasonal tourism and ecovillage internships
- Grant-funded research[^2]

## Cost of Joining

The cost of joining an ecovillage can be prohibitive, working against socioeconomic diversity. Dancing Rabbit's lease fees are modest, but initial investment in building a home on the leased plot still requires capital. Some communities address this through:

- Shared-equity loans
- Sweat-equity programs (labour in exchange for reduced fees)
- Income-adjusted sliding scales
- Community-funded housing co-ops
