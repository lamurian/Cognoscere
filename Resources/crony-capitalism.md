Crony capitalism describes a system where business success depends on political connections rather than market competition. It is characterized by regulatory capture, government bailouts for insiders, no-bid contracts, and rent-seeking through monopoly privileges. The term was first used to describe the Philippine economy under Ferdinand Marcos [@thulin2026].

This variant arises when state and business elites form self-serving networks that stifle competition. Wealth is accumulated through profiteering and rent-seeking rather than productive innovation. Crony capitalism exists along a continuum from mild regulatory favoritism to full kleptocracy. Examples include Marcos-era Philippines, Suharto's Indonesia, and aspects of post-Soviet Russia.
