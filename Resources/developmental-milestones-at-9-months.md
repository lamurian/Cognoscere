## Summary

At nine months, the infant is typically mobile and highly interactive. Weight reaches approximately 8.8 kg (19.4 lb) and length 72 cm (28.3 in). This age is a recommended screening point by the American Academy of Pediatrics, as delays in multiple domains can now be reliably detected [@centersfordiseasecontrolandprevention2026].

## Key Points

**Body Weight and Height:** Median weight ~8.8 kg (range 7.6–10.4 kg); median length ~72 cm. Weight gain ~60–90 g/week. Head circumference ~45 cm. Growth trajectory should be well established by now; crossing major percentile lines warrants investigation.

**Emotional Maturity:** Separation anxiety peaks. May be fearful with strangers and clingy with familiar adults. Has favourite toys. Shows specific attachment to primary caregivers. Expresses clear emotional states — happiness, anger, fear, surprise. Begins to show understanding of others' emotional expressions. Seeks comfort from caregiver when distressed.

**Psychological Development:** Late substage 3 / early Piaget substage 4 (coordination of secondary schemas, 8–12 months). The infant coordinates multiple actions to achieve goals. Searches for objects hidden while they watch — established object permanence. Imitates novel actions after a delay. Plays gesture games (pat-a-cake, peek-a-boo). Understands object function — bangs drum, pushes car.

**Motoric Development:** Gross motor: crawls well on hands and knees; pulls to stand and may lower back down; may cruise along furniture; sits without support indefinitely. Fine motor: developing mature pincer grasp (thumb and index finger tip); bangs two objects together; takes objects out of containers; points at objects with index finger; feeds self finger foods [@misirliyan2023].

**Speech and Language Development:** Says "mama" or "dada" with meaning beginning to emerge; imitates speech sounds and non-speech sounds (cough, click); understands "no"; responds to name consistently; uses gestures (waves bye-bye, raises arms to be picked up); participates in social routines with vocalisations. Babbles with sentence-like intonation [@nationalinstituteondeafnessandothercommunicationdisorders2022].

**Signs of Growth Retardation:** Red flags: not sitting independently; not crawling or making effort to move; uses only one side of body; not using both hands equally; absence of consonant babble; no response to name; does not show wariness of strangers (or extreme, inconsolable fear); no reciprocal social games; persistent tonic-neck reflex; does not bear weight on legs when supported; no pincer grasp emerging.
