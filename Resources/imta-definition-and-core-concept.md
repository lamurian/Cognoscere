## Summary

Integrated Multi-Trophic Aquaculture (IMTA) is the co-cultivation of fed species (finfish or shrimp) together with extractive species — suspension-feeding invertebrates (mussels, oysters), deposit-feeders (sea cucumbers, sea urchins), and macroalgae (kelps) [@buck2018]. The core principle is that waste from fed species becomes nutrients for extractive species, mimicking natural ecosystem dynamics.

IMTA uses a two-component configuration: fed aquaculture (supplied external feed) and extractive aquaculture (feeding on waste products). The extractive component splits into inorganic extractive species (seaweeds absorbing dissolved nutrients like ammonia and phosphate) and organic extractive species (bivalves and echinoderms filtering particulate organic matter) [@nissar2023].

Modern IMTA emerged from the work of Ryther et al. (1975), who quantitatively integrated shellfish, microalgae, and seaweeds in effluent treatment. However, the concept has deep historical roots — Chinese rice-fish culture dates back millennia, and dike-mulberry-fishpond integration to 500 BP [@nissar2023]. Today IMTA is recognized as an ecological engineering approach that enables sustainable intensification of aquaculture while minimizing waste.
