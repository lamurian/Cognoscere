Biodiversity in integrated farming is enhanced by emulating natural ecosystems. In small spaces, polycultures combine vertical layers (e.g., Moringa or Gliricidia trellis, climbing beans, trailing strawberries) to create high-diversity, productive mini-ecosystems [@pedersenzari2020; @borham2024]. At landscape scale, living fences in West Timor using Gliricidia sepium, Leucaena leucocephala, and Lannea coromandelica serve multiple functions: erosion control, livestock fodder, and wildlife corridors, significantly increasing local biodiversity compared to monoculture boundaries [@matheus2009; @ngongo2022; @reville2017]. Traditional Indonesian pekarangan home gardens and the Subak water management system offer culturally embedded models for managing biodiversity through collective governance and layered planting.
