Scaling up mental health services in low- and middle-income countries requires innovative workforce strategies given severe specialist shortages. Psychiatrist-to-population ratios in the most populous LMICs are extremely low: India (0.30 per 100,000), Pakistan (0.19), Nigeria (0.06), and Ethiopia (0.04), compared to a global average of 3.96 per 100,000 [@rathod2017]. Task-sharing — the rational redistribution of tasks from highly qualified specialists to health workers with shorter training — has emerged as a key strategy to close this gap.

Evidence supports the effectiveness of task-sharing approaches across diverse settings. In rural Pakistan, cognitive behaviour therapy delivered by Lady Health Workers markedly improved postnatal depression, with benefits extending to children (reduced diarrhoea episodes and greater immunization likelihood) [@rathod2017]. In Uganda, group interpersonal therapy was highly effective for depression in rural communities and among adolescent girls surviving war [@rathod2017]. The Friendship Bench project in Zimbabwe trains community health workers to deliver talk-based therapy, demonstrating that non-specialist delivery of evidence-based psychological interventions is feasible at scale [@worldhealthorganization2025]. The WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP) provides a framework for integrating mental health into primary care through task-sharing [@abdulla2025].

Community-based rehabilitation for schizophrenia in rural India has shown feasibility with lower disability scores, higher family engagement, and improved medication adherence [@rathod2017]. Transdiagnostic psychological treatments delivered by lay health workers offer a pragmatic path to scalability. However, sustained progress requires adequate training, supervision, quality assurance systems, and integration with referral pathways to specialist care [@rathod2017].
