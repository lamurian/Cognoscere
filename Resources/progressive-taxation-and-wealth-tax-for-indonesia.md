Indonesia's tax system is nominally progressive but fails in practice: tax-to-GDP ratio (~10%) is half the Asia-Pacific average (19.8%), and taxes plus spending reduce the Gini by only 2.5 points [@worldbank2016; @unnadhiro2025]. The system relies on regressive consumption taxes (VAT) while wealthy individuals avoid taxation through loopholes [@rahmat2024].

A 2% annual wealth tax on the 50 richest Indonesians (combined US$323B) could generate US$5.03B — 2.45% of the state budget [@askar2024]. This could fund 339,836 low-income houses or feed 15 million children. Challenges include asset valuation, capital flight, and political resistance from elites controlling strategic industries [@bridgegip2026].

Broader reforms: progressive PIT/CIT rates, closing the 6.4% of GDP tax gap through enforcement, and reforming tax expenditures favoring large corporations. Lowering the SME tax threshold from US$296K to US$37K would prevent entity-splitting abuses [@askar2024].
