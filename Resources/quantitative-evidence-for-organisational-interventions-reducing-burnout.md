## Summary

A 2026 systematic review of 11 organisational intervention studies (1669 participants) found that organisational-level interventions can significantly reduce burnout, though effects are often limited to the short term [@arajo2026]. The most effective strategies combine psychoeducation with third-generation psychotherapies such as Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) and mindfulness.

Key quantitative findings from this review include:

- **Coaching**: Mean burnout scores decreased 21.6% in the intervention group while increasing 2.5% in the control group (p=0.001) [@arajo2026, citing Kiser et al. 2024].
- **Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)**: Significant improvements in emotional exhaustion (p<0.001), depersonalization (p<0.001), and personal accomplishment (p<0.001) [@arajo2026, citing Talebiazar et al. 2024].
- **Brief Mindfulness Program**: Improvements in stress (p<0.001), emotional exhaustion (p=0.004), and depersonalization (p=0.01) [@arajo2026, citing Schroeder et al. 2016].
- **Workplace ACT**: Cognitive weariness (a burnout sub-component) significantly declined from baseline to mid-intervention (p=0.004), post-intervention (p=0.04), and was sustained at follow-up (p=0.02) [@arajo2026, citing Prudenzi et al. 2022].

The review noted that 10 of 11 studies focused on healthcare workers, indicating a significant gap in evidence for other occupational sectors.

A separate systematic review of 33 studies (Cohen et al., 2023) corroborates these findings: 29 of 33 studies reported effective outcomes, with significant improvements in well-being, work engagement, quality of life, and resilience alongside reductions in burnout, perceived stress, anxiety, and depression [@cohen2023]. Only 3 of 33 studies used organisational-level interventions; the rest targeted individual behaviour change.

## Key Points

- Organisational-level interventions (coaching, MBSR, ACT) produce statistically significant burnout reductions
- Best quantitative effect: coaching reduced burnout by 21.6% (p=0.001)
- Mindfulness improves emotional exhaustion (p=0.004) and depersonalization (p=0.01)
- ACT sustains cognitive weariness reduction at follow-up (p=0.02)
- Effects are predominantly short-term; long-term evidence is limited
- Healthcare sector dominates the evidence; generalizability to other sectors is uncertain
