## Summary

Cob is a mixture of clay-rich subsoil, sand, straw, and water, hand-sculpted into monolithic walls. Its plasticity allows for curved walls, alcoves, and organic shapes characteristic of fantasy architecture. Cob walls provide high thermal mass, absorbing heat during the day and releasing it at night, creating stable indoor temperatures [@jideofor2012]. Straw bale construction offers superior insulation (R-value of R-30 to R-40 for typical bale walls), making it ideal for the building envelope of energy-efficient organic homes. When combined with lime or earth renders, straw bale walls can achieve curved forms while providing excellent airtightness [@voiceofnature2022]. Roundwood timber framing uses locally harvested tree trunks with bark peeled, forming structural frames for reciprocal roofs and post-and-beam systems. This technique eliminates the energy-intensive milling process of conventional lumber and creates visually stunning exposed structures that can support green roofs and earth-sheltered designs [@voiceofnature2022].

## Key Points
- Cob enables freeform sculpted walls with high thermal mass (0.3-0.5 W/mK conductivity)
- Straw bale provides R-30+ insulation in curved wall assemblies, sequestering CO₂ during growth
- Roundwood timber framing uses locally sourced poles, reducing embodied energy vs milled lumber
- All three materials are low-carbon, non-toxic, fully biodegradable, and have established regional building codes
- The biomimetic principle "form follows environment" guides material selection for passive climate response [@verbrugghe2023]
