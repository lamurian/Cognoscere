## Summary

Sustainable IMTA operation requires integrated management of multiple trophic levels. Best practices include: (1) species diversification to spread risk and maximize nutrient recycling; (2) adaptive stocking density based on seasonal nutrient flux; (3) submerged cultivation to avoid wave stress in offshore conditions; (4) real-time water quality monitoring (temperature, DO, pH, nutrients) [@buck2018; @nissar2023].

Key performance indicators: system-wide Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR) — smart IMTA systems achieve 0.70 vs. 1.62 for monoculture [@goda2025]; nutrient removal efficiency; biomass yield per trophic level; biofouling management through deployment timing. For offshore operations, ring designs with one-point mooring allow rotation and submergence, reducing mechanical stress [@buck2018].

Optimization considerations: (1) temporal mismatch between fish nutrient release and seaweed growth cycles requires strategic species selection; (2) deposit-feeders (sea cucumbers) capture larger sediments otherwise lost; (3) economic viability requires marketable products at each trophic level or valuation of ecosystem services through nutrient trading credits [@buck2018]. Social acceptance requires demonstrable environmental benefits, stakeholder engagement, and marine spatial planning integration — particularly for multi-use IMTA with offshore wind farms.
