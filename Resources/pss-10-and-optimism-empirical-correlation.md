PSS-10 shows a moderate-to-strong negative correlation with dispositional optimism (LOT-R), consistently across samples. Higgins [@higgins2016] found Pearson r = -0.54 (p < 0.01, n = 813), with optimism as the second strongest predictor (β = -0.32) after self-efficacy. Bodys-Cupak et al. [@bodyscupak2022] reported Spearman rho = -0.389 (p < 0.001, n = 307); in regression, optimism (β = -0.283) and life satisfaction were the only significant predictors, while self-efficacy and self-esteem dropped out.

The range (|r| ≈ 0.39-0.54) aligns with stress-optimism literature. More optimistic individuals appraise stressors as less threatening, consistent with the transactional model [@lazarus1984]. Optimism functions as a key resource in secondary appraisal, increasing perceived coping ability. Sources: @higgins2016, @bodyscupak2022.
