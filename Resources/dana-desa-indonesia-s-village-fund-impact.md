## Overview

The 2014 Village Law (UU Desa No. 6/2014) was a landmark decentralization reform. It granted village governments unprecedented autonomy and direct funding, bypassing district-level intermediaries. From 2015 to 2024, the central government allocated a total of IDR 609.9 trillion to the Village Fund, starting at IDR 20.8 trillion in 2015 and stabilizing around IDR 70 trillion annually by 2019.

## Infrastructure Achievements (2015-2020)

- 261,877 km of village roads constructed
- 11,499 km of bridges
- 39,844 village-owned enterprises (BUMDes) established
- Clean water facilities, health units, and early childhood education centers built across thousands of villages

## Poverty and Development Impact

- Underdeveloped villages dropped 44%: from 12,177 (2021) to 6,803 (2023)
- Rural poverty decreased from 17.9 million people (March 2015) to 15.3 million (March 2020)
- The Village Fund contributed 0.49% to national GDP
- Village open unemployment declined by 1.21%
- The number of self-reliant villages doubled from 2,894 to 5,559 between 2014-2019

## The Next Phase Challenge

So far, the Village Fund has been spent mostly on infrastructure. This improved quality of life but has limited ongoing GDP growth. The Ministry of Villages targets for 2020-2024 include shifting toward:
- Post-harvest agricultural processing and market integration
- BUMDes development for collective enterprise
- Digital e-commerce platforms for village products
- Human capital development through skills training and digital literacy
- Rural tourism and creative business development

## Corruption and Accountability

The KPK (Corruption Eradication Commission) reports 851 Village Fund corruption cases involving 973 individuals, half of whom are village heads -- the largest corruption scandal in state finance management. The Siskeudes (Village Financial System) digital platform was introduced to improve transparency, but faces adoption barriers due to limited electricity, internet, and digital skills in many villages.
