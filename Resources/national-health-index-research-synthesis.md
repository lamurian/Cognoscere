This research synthesises approaches to constructing a national health index for measuring and comparing population health. A national health index aggregates multiple health dimensions into a single score, enabling policy evaluation and cross-country comparison. Single metrics like life expectancy miss morbidity, disability, and mental health, making multidimensional indices essential [@tolonen2021].

The UK England Health Index provides the most detailed methodology, spanning three domains (People, Lives, Places) with 58 indicators. Key methodological decisions include indicator selection, imputation, normalization (z-score anchored to baseline year), weighting (factor analysis), aggregation (arithmetic mean), and sensitivity analysis [@frenisterrantino2022]. Junaid et al. (2025) demonstrated a global health risk factors index across 100 countries using PCA weighting and inverse Euclidean distance aggregation, showing strong correlation with HDI [@junaid2025].

The recommended approach uses winsorization, PCA or optimized weights, geometric mean aggregation, and comprehensive Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis. Cross-country comparability requires standardized definitions, common data sources (WHO GHO, IHME GHDx, OECD), and transparent methodology documentation [@tolonen2021]. Domain-level sub-indices should accompany the composite score for targeted policy insights [@frenisterrantino2022].
