## Summary

At nineteen months, the toddler's vocabulary enters an acceleration phase for many children — the "naming explosion." Weight averages about 11.1 kg (24.5 lb) and length 83.5 cm (32.9 in). The toddler runs with greater control and begins to combine gestures and words more fluently to communicate [@centersfordiseasecontrolandprevention2026].

## Key Points

**Body Weight and Height:** Median weight ~11.1 kg (range 9.6–13.0 kg); median length ~83.5 cm. Weight gain ~30–50 g/week, length gain ~0.8 cm/month. Molars may be erupting.

**Emotional Maturity:** Temper tantrums may peak — the toddler has strong desires but limited language and emotional regulation skills. Shows increased independence but also clinginess. Beginning to label emotions ("happy," "sad") when prompted. Shows empathy more consistently — may comfort a crying peer. Imitates adult emotional displays.

**Psychological Development:** Piaget's late substage 5 / early substage 6 (mental representation, 18–24 months). The toddler begins to solve problems mentally rather than purely through trial and error. Engages in symbolic play — uses one object to represent another (banana as phone). Recognises self in mirror and photos. Follows two-step related commands.

**Motoric Development:** Gross motor: runs with better coordination (fewer falls); walks up stairs with hand held, placing both feet on each step; squats to play; throws ball underhand while standing; climbs on playground equipment. Fine motor: scribbles with purpose; builds tower of 3–4 cubes; uses spoon well; helps undress; turns pages one at a time.

**Speech and Language Development:** Uses 10–25 words; vocabulary growth may be rapid — 1–2 new words per day; begins using two-word phrases ("more milk," "daddy go"); points to several body parts; follows two-step commands; names familiar objects and pictures [@nationalinstituteondeafnessandothercommunicationdisorders2022].

**Signs of Growth Retardation:** Red flags: no words by 19 months; not walking independently; does not point to body parts; does not follow simple commands; no pretend play; not imitating actions; no eye contact; not responding to name; not showing interest in other children; loss of previously acquired language; persistent toe-walking.
