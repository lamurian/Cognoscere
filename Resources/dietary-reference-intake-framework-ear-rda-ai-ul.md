## Summary

The Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs) are four reference values developed by the Food and Nutrition Board of the National Academies, introduced in 1997 to replace the single-value RDA model. They aim not just to prevent deficiency diseases but also to enhance health and reduce chronic disease risk [@fung2025]. The four values are: (1) Estimated Average Requirement (EAR)—the intake meeting needs of 50% of healthy individuals; (2) Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA)—sufficient for 97–98% of individuals, calculated as EAR plus two standard deviations; (3) Adequate Intake (AI)—used when evidence for an EAR is insufficient; and (4) Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL)—the maximum daily intake unlikely to cause adverse health effects [@nationalacademiesofsciences2024].

EARs serve as the basis for calculating RDAs and are used for assessing population intake adequacy. RDAs are the target for individual intake. AIs are set for nutrients like vitamin K, potassium, and calcium where data are insufficient for an EAR. ULs help prevent toxicity from excessive supplementation. All DRI values vary by age, sex, and life stage. The Daily Value (DV) is a simplified consumer version used on food labels, calculated for a 2,000-calorie diet and not personalised by age or sex [@usfoodanddrugadministration2024].
