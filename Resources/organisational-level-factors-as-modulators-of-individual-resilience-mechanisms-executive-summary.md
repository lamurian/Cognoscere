## Executive Summary

Organisational-level factors -- leadership behaviour, organisational climate, job design, and social support systems -- systematically modulate the individual-level mechanisms (cognitive appraisal, emotion regulation, motivational orientation, and neurobiological processes) that underlie psychological resilience in working populations. The evidence converges on several key principles.

First, leadership behaviour operates through social information processing pathways [@zhu2019; @zhang2020]. Humble, supportive leaders enhance employee resilience by increasing work-related promotion focus (a 'can do' motivational state) and perceived insider identity (a 'reason to' motivational state). They also create psychological safety and error management climates that enable adaptive risk-taking and learning from setbacks. These leadership effects are mediated by the same cognitive mechanisms (positive appraisal, reappraisal capacity) identified by PASTOR theory as core to individual resilience [@kalisch2015].

Second, organisational climate and culture function as supra-individual modulators of resilience mechanisms. Learning-oriented climates, psychosocial safety climates, and high-support cultures strengthen employees' capacity for positive situation classification and adaptive coping by providing resources that buffer against resource depletion and by shaping shared appraisals of workplace demands [@sippel2015; @kuntz2017]. Organisational cultures that foster strong social networks increase perceived functional support, which reduces physiological stress reactivity through oxytocin-mediated attenuation of HPA axis and amygdala activation [@sippel2015].

Third, job design characteristics modulate resilience through the challenge-hindrance stressor distinction, but this relationship is more complex than initially theorised. Meta-analytic evidence shows limited support for simple challenge-hindrance categorisation, suggesting that individual appraisal processes and temporal dynamics are critical [@mazzola2019; @horan2020]. The JD-R model provides a more robust framework, positioning resilience as a personal resource that buffers the impact of job demands when sufficient job resources are present [@bakker2017a].

Fourth, the evidence for organisational-level interventions remains underdeveloped relative to individual-level interventions. Only 2 of 24 workplace resilience intervention studies adopted a socioecological approach addressing both organisational and individual factors [@hollaar2025]. The existing organisational factors literature is predominantly cross-sectional, self-report-based, and drawn from high-income Western and East Asian contexts, limiting causal inference and generalisability.

## Confidence Assessment

| Question | Confidence | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 (WHY): Why do similar individual resilience mechanisms produce different outcomes across organisational contexts? | High | Multiple peer-reviewed sources across leadership, climate, and JD-R literatures consistently show organisational factors moderate individual mechanisms. Direct evidence from field studies and experiments supports this claim |
| Q2 (HOW): How do organisational factors mechanistically modulate cognitive and neurobiological resilience processes? | Moderate | Cognitive and motivational pathways (promotion focus, insider identity, psychological safety) are well-supported. Neurobiological pathways are theorised from general population studies but direct workplace evidence is limited. The challenge-hindrance model shows mixed support |

## Known Gaps

- Direct neurobiological evidence linking specific organisational factors (leadership style, job design) to HPA axis functioning, oxytocin levels, or prefrontal-amygdala circuitry in working populations is virtually absent.
- Most studies are cross-sectional; causal direction between organisational factors and resilience mechanisms cannot be established.
- Only a handful of studies adopt a true multilevel design measuring both organisational-level and individual-level mechanisms simultaneously.
- The socioecological approach -- combining organisational change with individual training -- remains critically understudied despite theoretical promise [@hollaar2025].
- Conflicting evidence from CHM meta-analyses [@mazzola2019] suggests simple stressor categorisation is insufficient; appraisal processes moderate the effect of organisational demands on resilience.
- Cultural generalisability is limited; evidence comes almost entirely from East Asian and Western high-income contexts.
- Definitional inconsistencies across employee resilience research [@britt2016] undermine cross-study comparison and synthesis.
- It remains unclear whether different organisational factors modulate resilience through common or distinct mechanisms, and whether the same organisational factor affects different individual mechanisms equally.
