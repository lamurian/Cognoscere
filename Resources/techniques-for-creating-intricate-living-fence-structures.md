For willow, the diamond lattice (fedge) method is standard: 1-year rods at least 6 feet long are inserted into prepared soil at 45 degrees, spaced 10 inches apart in opposite-angled pairs to create a diamond pattern [@albert2024]. Tops are tied to a horizontal withy for stability. New shoots are woven into the lattice or rubbed off to maintain the open pattern [@keiren2023]. Site preparation requires clearing weeds, loosening soil 10 inches deep, adding compost, and using weed barrier fabric [@albert2024].

For crape myrtle, the technique is closer to arborsculpture: young trees planted along the fence line have their trunks slowly bent and woven around bamboo or metal frames over several seasons, secured with soft ties to prevent bark damage. Willow fedges achieve full form in 2-3 years while crape myrtle structures take 5+ years. Willow needs consistently moist soil while crape myrtle tolerates dry conditions once established [@moore2006]. Biodegradable twine or thin willow whips are used for ties, replaced as plants thicken [@penn2024].

Best planting time is the dormant season (December-March) for willow and spring or fall for crape myrtle. Willow needs 2-3 structural pruning sessions per year while crape myrtle needs annual shaping and pest monitoring. Willow cuttings root in moist soil within weeks; crape myrtle requires rooting hormone for best results [@moore2006]. For both plants, regular maintenance is essential to maintain structural integrity and prevent the living fence from becoming overgrown.

Key sources: @albert2024, @keiren2023, @penn2024, @moore2006, @stanley2021.
