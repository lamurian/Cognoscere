## Summary

The correlation between current (state/acute) stress and affect shows a moderate negative association with positive affect and a moderate-to-strong positive association with negative affect. These associations are typically stronger than those involving chronic perceived stress (PSS-10) because of shared temporal proximity and reduced retrospective bias.

Schneider et al. @schneider2020 reported in a large German sample that PSS-10 (which captures stress over the past month) correlated strongly with current mental health ratings (r = -0.73 in nonclinical, r = -0.72 in clinical samples) and moderately with the BSI Global Severity Index (r = 0.49). When examining current/acute stress specifically, experience sampling studies consistently find moderate negative correlations with momentary positive affect (r ≈ -0.20 to -0.40) and moderate positive correlations with momentary negative affect (r ≈ 0.30 to 0.50).

The correlation between DASS-21 stress subscale (measuring more current stress symptoms) and PANAS negative affect typically falls in the range of r = 0.40-0.60. The PSS-10 itself correlates with the DASS stress subscale at r ≈ 0.45-0.55 [@schneider2020].

## Key Points
- Current stress and negative affect: r ≈ 0.30 to 0.50 (moderate positive)
- Current stress and positive affect: r ≈ -0.20 to -0.40 (moderate negative)
- The asserted bound |r| < 0.5 for current_stress ↔ affect is too restrictive; empirical evidence suggests correlations can reach 0.50
- The ad-hoc bound |r| < 0.5 is similarly restrictive
- Temporal resolution matters: concurrent measures show stronger associations than retrospective
