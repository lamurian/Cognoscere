German landscape engineer Arthur Wiechula (1867-1941) invented a V-shaped lateral cut technique to precisely bend tree trunks into structural shapes for living walls and buildings. The V-cut allows bending in a desired direction, and the tree's reaction wood closes the wound within one growing season, permanently locking the curve [@wikipediacontributors2025c]. His most notable achievement was a 120-meter (394-foot) living wall of Canadian poplars (Populus canadensis) that served as a snow fence for a railway line -- the earliest documented large-scale living wall. His 1926 book laid the theoretical foundation for modern Baubotanik and arborsculpture [@wikipediacontributors2025a].
