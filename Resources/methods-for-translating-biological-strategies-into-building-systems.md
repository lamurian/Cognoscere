## Summary

Translating biological strategies into building systems requires structured abstraction: extracting functional principles from organisms without copying literal forms. Verbrugghe identifies abstraction as the most difficult phase for architects lacking biological training [@verbrugghe2023]. The BioGen framework addresses this through three stages: exploration (identifying pinnacle organisms matching the design challenge), pinnacle analysis (studying morphology, physiology, and behaviour), and a design path matrix (abstracting strategies into concepts). AskNature (Biomimicry Institute) supports this with 1,600+ biological strategies searchable by function, while BioTRIZ adapts engineering methodology with biological principles to resolve design contradictions [@verbrugghe2023].

Real-world practice reveals key challenges. The French Biomim'City Lab study found design teams often lack time for proper biological research during competitive project phases — the temporal mismatch between long biomimetic R&D and short design timelines was the most cited barrier [@blanco2021]. Successful projects integrated ecologists into early design stages, used pre-validated biological models (termites, cacti, lotus) to reduce research risk, and assembled multi-disciplinary teams combining architects, biologists, and engineers from the competition phase [@blanco2021]. These practices significantly improved the quality and feasibility of biomimetic outcomes.

## Key Points
- Abstraction (extracting functional principles, not copying forms) is the critical translation step
- AskNature provides 1,600+ strategies searchable by function
- BioGen: exploration → pinnacle analysis → design path matrix
- Integrated design teams (architects + biologists) produce better outcomes [@blanco2021]
