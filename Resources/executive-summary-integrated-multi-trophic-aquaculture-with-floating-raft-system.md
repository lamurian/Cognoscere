## Summary\n\nIMTA is the co-cultivation of fed species (finfish) with extractive species (seaweeds, bivalves, echinoderms) in a system that mimics natural ecosystem dynamics, converting waste into valuable biomass [@buck2018; @nissar2023]. The Floating Raft System (FRS) provides a structural platform for suspending extractive species within nutrient plumes from fed aquaculture.\n\n**WHY it matters ecologically:** IMTA reduces nutrient loading through seaweed uptake (Gracilaria can remove 80% of dissolved N from salmon farms), bivalve filtration of particulates, and deposit-feeder processing of sediments [@buck2018]. This prevents eutrophication, enhances biodiversity, and improves assimilative capacity.\n\n**HOW FRS enables IMTA:** The UNH offshore FRS integrates trout (2 t/yr), mussels (6 t/yr), and kelp (1 t/yr). Key ratios for sizing: kelp-to-fish biomass at 6.7:1 (N) for Alaria, 12.9:1 (N) for Saccharina [@buck2018]. Species selection requires trophic compatibility, hydrodynamic tolerance, and market viability.\n\n**Best practices:** submerge cultivation in high-energy sites, one-point mooring rings for kelp, real-time water quality monitoring, adaptive stocking, and biofouling timing. Smart IMTA-FRS achieves 0.70 system-wide FCR vs. 1.62 monoculture [@goda2025].
