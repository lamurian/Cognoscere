## Summary

Stigma operates as a structural barrier at multiple levels in Indonesia's mental health system, functioning as both a cause and consequence of the treatment gap. The expert consensus identified stigma as one of three interconnected essences driving mental health challenges, noting it exists across all socioeconomic layers and across various cultures and demographics [@basrowi2024].

At the individual level, Munira et al. (2023) found that all 90 participants in their study experienced stigma and negative labels. Stigma came from multiple sources: family members who called them crazy, neighbours who gossiped, and healthcare workers who dismissed their symptoms. One participant reported that general practitioners 'blasphemed and underestimated the depression complaints' [@munira2023].

At the systemic level, stigma manifests in healthcare workers' negative attitudes. The Munira study documented cases where GPs refused to provide referral letters, requiring patients to present with self-harm injuries before being believed. This healthcare worker stigma creates a gatekeeping barrier that prevents access to specialist care [@munira2023].

Cultural factors compound stigma. Mental illness is viewed as a shameful affliction in Indonesian culture, leading families to hide affected members. The practice of pasung is partly a response to this shame — families confine relatives rather than seek treatment publicly. An inverse correlation exists between mental health knowledge levels and stigma levels, suggesting that lack of education perpetuates both stigma and treatment avoidance [@basrowi2024; @hidayat2023].

Self-help emerged as a counterforce to stigma. Some participants reported that personal motivation to stabilise and heal outweighed external stigma: 'I do not care if I got stigmatized negatively because I want to be stable and heal anyway' [@munira2023].

## Key Points
- All 90 participants in Munira study experienced stigma from multiple sources
- Healthcare worker stigma creates gatekeeping barriers to specialist referral
- Cultural shame leads families to hide affected members and use pasung
- Inverse correlation between mental health knowledge and stigma levels
- Self-help motivation can overcome stigma when treatment is accessible
- Community-based services can normalise mental health care and reduce stigma

## Confidence: High
Qualitative evidence from 90 participants (Munira et al. 2023) and 45-expert consensus (Basrowi et al. 2024) converge on stigma as a multi-level barrier.
