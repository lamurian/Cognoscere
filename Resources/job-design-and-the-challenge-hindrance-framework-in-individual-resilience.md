Job design characteristics modulate individual resilience mechanisms through the challenge-hindrance stressor framework and the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model.

The Challenge-Hindrance Stress Model (CHM) distinguishes between challenge stressors (workload, time pressure, responsibility) that can energise and promote growth, and hindrance stressors (role ambiguity, organisational constraints, red tape) that consistently impair well-being [@horan2020; @cavanaugh2000]. Challenge stressors may build resilience through mastery experiences when accompanied by adequate resources, while hindrance stressors erode it through resource depletion [@crane2016].

However, meta-analytic evidence reveals that this differential relationship does not consistently hold [@mazzola2019]. The CHM shows support for task performance predictions but not for organisational citizenship behaviour, counterproductive behaviour, job attitudes, or strain outcomes. This has led to calls for a paradigm shift emphasising that stressor classification is fundamentally an appraisal process rather than an a priori categorisation [@horan2020].

The JD-R model positions resilience as a personal resource that moderates the relationship between job demands and strain [@bakker2017a]. Longitudinal evidence from healthcare professionals shows that employees with higher resilience show less reactivity to daily hindrance demands and recover more quickly from stress exposure [@vignoli2024]. Job resources (autonomy, social support, feedback, skill variety) foster engagement and build resilience capacity, while job demands (workload, emotional demands) can lead to burnout if resources are insufficient.

Job crafting -- employees proactively redesigning their jobs to increase challenge demands and decrease hindrance demands -- represents an integration of CHM with JD-R [@horan2020]. Employees who craft their jobs to seek more challenges and resources report higher resilience and work engagement. This suggests that individuals can partially shape how organisational factors modulate their resilience, though organisational constraints limit this agency.

The interaction between job design and individual resilience mechanisms is likely bidirectional and dynamic: job characteristics shape resilience capacity over time, and resilient employees may seek or create more resourceful work environments. Existing studies are predominantly cross-sectional, limiting causal inference about these reciprocal relationships.
