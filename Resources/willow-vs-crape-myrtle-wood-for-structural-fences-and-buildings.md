Willow and crape myrtle differ dramatically as structural timber. Black willow is one of the weakest and least durable commercial hardwoods in North America, with no natural decay resistance [@krinard1985; @cassens2007]. Crape myrtle (Pyinma) is a moderate-density hardwood with moderately durable decay resistance and significantly better mechanical strength [@wooddatabase2010].

For fence posts and building structures exposed to ground contact and moisture, crape myrtle is the clearly superior choice. Its Janka hardness (1,090 lbf) is triple that of willow (350 lbf). Its Modulus of Rupture (14,120 psi) is nearly double willow's (7,800 psi). And its decay resistance — rated moderately durable — contrasts sharply with willow's complete lack of natural durability [@wooddatabase2010; @virginiatechdepartmentofwoodscience2000]. Willow requires full preservative treatment for any outdoor structural use, while crape myrtle can serve in above-ground outdoor applications with minimal treatment.

However, crape myrtle availability limits its use. Dimensional lumber from Lagerstroemia is primarily imported from Southeast Asia. The common landscape crape myrtle (L. indica) in North America typically yields only small-diameter pieces suitable for turning and carving rather than structural posts [@wooddatabase2010]. Willow is more available as commercial lumber in the US South but performs poorly as a structural material without treatment.
