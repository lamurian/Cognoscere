## Summary

Li et al. (2023) implemented and evaluated a primary organisational-level intervention specifically targeting workplace bullying by redesigning the people management practices that create conditions for bullying [@li2023]. The study used a controlled pre-test/post-test design with 10 intervention stores (n=399 at T1, n=339 at T2) compared against 252 non-intervention stores in a major Australian supermarket chain.

The intervention followed a five-phase problem-solving cycle (preparation, diagnosis, solutions, implementation, evaluation) with co-design as the core principle. A behaviourally-anchored risk audit tool diagnosed 10 people management contexts across three domains: coordinating working hours, managing work performance, and shaping relationships/environment. The three lowest-scoring risk areas were: recognising and rewarding job performance, managing tasks and workload, and managing underperformance.

**Key quantitative outcomes:**

- **Workplace bullying exposure (S-NAQ)** decreased significantly at the store level (paired t-test, p<0.05)
- **Job demands** decreased significantly (DISQ 2.1, p<0.05)
- **Job resources** increased significantly (DISQ 2.1, p<0.05)
- **Job demands mediated the relationship** between improved people management practices and reduced bullying exposure (bootstrap mediation, indirect effect significant)
- **Internal complaint records**: Intervention stores showed a 73% reduction in employment relations complaints vs. a 4% increase in control stores
- **Employee advocacy (NPS)** increased from +12 to +28 in intervention stores; control stores declined from +18 to +14
- **Customer satisfaction (NPS)** increased from +28 to +42 in intervention stores; control stores remained stable at +30

The study demonstrated that organisational redesign targeting the root causes of bullying (poor work design, high demands, low resources) is more effective than traditional individual-focused anti-bullying policies. Co-design participation itself functioned as an intervention by building resources (autonomy, support, fairness) and psychological safety.

## Key Points

- Primary organisational intervention reduced bullying exposure, complaints, and improved advocacy/satisfaction
- Job demands significantly decreased and job resources significantly increased post-intervention
- Job demands mediated the effect of improved people management on bullying reduction
- 73% reduction in internal complaints in intervention stores vs 4% increase in controls
- Employee advocacy NPS improved +16 points; customer satisfaction NPS improved +14 points
- Co-design participation builds resources and psychological safety as a mechanism of change
