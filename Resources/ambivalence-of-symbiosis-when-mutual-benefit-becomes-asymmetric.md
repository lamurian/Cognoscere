Company-community symbiosis is constitutively ambivalent: it produces both positive and negative outcomes that coexist. The positive side includes job creation, infrastructure investment, and community revitalization. The negative side includes environmental degradation, community displacement, and asymmetric power relations where local populations bear the costs while value accrues elsewhere [@cuevasvalenzuela2022]. The Chilean port-city of Coronel exemplifies this: forestry and port operations modernized while the community experienced pollution, blocked coastal access, noise, and real estate displacement. Global value chains create uneven development — the \"smile curve\" shows extraction and production generate least value while design, marketing, and sales capture most profit, perpetuating asymmetric symbiosis between periphery and center [@cuevasvalenzuela2022]. Social capital itself can have negative effects, constraining innovation by limiting knowledge searches within networks and enabling cronyism [@cyr2022]. Communities increasingly resist asymmetric arrangements through direct action and demands for inclusive governance [@cuevasvalenzuela2022].
