Hollaar et al. (2025) conducted a systematic review of 24 studies covering 26 resilience-based interventions in public sector workplaces. The review found that most interventions (61% of resilience-directed ones) showed significant positive effects on employee psychological wellbeing.[^1]

**Intervention types and effect sizes.** Group-based educational workshops with higher frequency and duration (5 to 44 sessions) produced medium to large effect sizes (Cohen's d = 0.5 to 0.8+). Digital-only interventions produced small effects (d ~ 0.2), which improved to small-to-medium when combined with in-person elements. The most common approaches were mindfulness training, cognitive-behavioural skill building, emotion management, and stress coping workshops.[^1]

**Definitional issues.** Resilience was defined primarily as a trait (individual ability to bounce back) or a process (dynamic adaptation using internal and external resources). A majority of studies used trait-based definitions and the Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC), though this contradicts the growing consensus that resilience is a dynamic, context-dependent process. About 46% of studies showed inconsistencies between their definition of resilience, intervention content, and measurement instruments—a jingle-jangle problem that hampers cross-study comparison.[^1]

**Individual vs organisational focus.** Most interventions targeted individual-level factors only, despite organisational factors (workload, staffing, leadership) being primary drivers of stress. Only two studies explicitly adopted a socioecological approach addressing both individual and organisational factors. Resilience is increasingly recognised as a shared responsibility between employee and employer, requiring systemic interventions that go beyond individual training.[^1]

**Le et al. (2021)** confirmed that workplace mental health promotion interventions—including resilience-focused programmes—are consistently cost-effective or cost-saving, with return on investment ratios of $1.50 to $7 per $1 invested for workplace interventions targeting employees.[^2]
