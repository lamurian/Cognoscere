# Course Aim

- What is a phamacoeconomics (PE) study
- Conduct a PE analysis
- Understand and interpret the results of a PE study

# Value for Money

- Is the medication worth it?
- How can we organize it so it can cost less?
- Whom will the medication benefit the most?
- Market equilibrium: The crossing point of the demand and supply

# Issues of Market Failure in Health Economics

- Asymmetry of information concerning health interventions between patients and practitioners
- Public goods aspects: The perceived demand curve once the individual gets ill
- Who pays and who chooses: Adverse incentives for practitioners

# Universal Health Coverage

One of the following:
- Cover all people
- Cover all interventions

# Types of PE Studies

- Cost of illness:
  - Focus: How expensive is an illness?
  - Not about specific intervention
  - Example: Cost of schizophrenia, cost of smoking, etc.
  - What drives the complexity: Perspective (individual/patient, payor, government) and scope (side effects, productivity costs, health costs, etc.)
- Budget impact study
  - Focus: What is the impact in terms of total costs related to new disorder/drug?
  - Example: What is the impact on total costs if we start to reimburse a new type of antipsychotic?
  - What drives the complexity: Scope
- Economic evaluation study $\to$ Most discussed throughout the course:
  - Focus: What is the added value of a certain disorder/drug in comparison to an alternative?
  - Example: What is the value for money of a certain new drug compared to the old drug?
  - Types:
    - Cost effectiveness analysis
    - Cost utility analysis
    - Cost minimization analysis
    - Cost benefit analysis

# Comparison in an Economic Evaluation

- An economic evaluation always compares at least two alternatives:
  - Do nothing option
  - Care as usual
  - Another relevant new intervention
- Things to compare:
  - Cost of intervention
  - Cost related to other treatments/disease states
  - Cost of patients, e.g., travel costs
  - Productivity losses 
- Perspective determines which cost to include/exclude
- QALY: Quality adjusted life years; combine quantity and quality of life, also mortality and morbidity
