Cob has significantly lower compressive strength than concrete — 0.45-0.89 MPa (avg 0.70 MPa) compared to 17-41 MPa for typical structural concrete, a 20-50x difference [@pullen2009]. However, cob's strength is sufficient for low-rise residential construction: New Zealand's code prescribes 0.5 MPa design stress, European practice recommends 0.2 MPa, and two-storey buildings with 50 cm walls are feasible [@morel2021]. The key structural difference is variability — cob's coefficient of variation (COV) of 22% far exceeds manufactured materials, requiring conservative safety factors. Historical cob buildings in England dating to the 13th century demonstrate that adequate wall thickness (450-600 mm) and good craftsmanship compensate for the material's lower absolute strength.
