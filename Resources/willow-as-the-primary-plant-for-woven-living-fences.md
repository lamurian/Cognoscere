Willow (Salix species) is the most widely used plant for living fences and woven garden structures because branches root readily from cuttings due to high auxin levels, making the plant self-propagating from simple cut rods [@keiren2023]. Willow grows extremely fast (up to 6 ft/year) and young rods are highly flexible, able to be bent into diamond lattices, arches, domes, tunnels, and furniture shapes [@albert2024; @penn2024]. Preferred species include Salix viminalis (long straight rods), Salix purpurea (deer-resistant), and Salix alba varieties (ornamental stem colors).

Willow structures are planted during the dormant season (December-March) when sap is down and branches are most flexible. Rods are inserted at 45-degree angles in a diamond weave pattern, requiring regular pruning 2-3 times per year to maintain shape [@albert2024]. The structures last 30-50 years and provide basketry material as a secondary resource, but willow's aggressive roots demand placement at least 33 feet from buildings and drains [@greayer2024; @penn2024].

Willow cuttings root easily without rooting hormone, growing up to 6 ft/year and reaching full height in 2-3 years. Rods are planted at 45-degree angles, spaced 10 inches apart, woven in opposite directions, and tied at intersections. Maintenance requires structural pruning 2-3 times per year removing excess vertical growth. Lifespan is 30-50 years. Roots are aggressive near water sources, requiring at least 33 feet from buildings and drains.

Key sources: @keiren2023, @albert2024, @penn2024, @greayer2024, @stanley2021.
