## Summary

Several established methods enable meaningful comparison of health workforce across countries, each with different strengths and data requirements.

**WHO National Health Workforce Accounts (NHWA)**: The WHO NHWA provides a standardized framework for health workforce data collection across 70+ indicators covering education, training, licensing, deployment, and migration. Countries report data through an online platform, enabling standardized comparisons of doctor, nurse, and midwife density. The WHO minimum threshold of 4.45 health workers (physicians, nurses, midwives) per 1,000 population for 80% UHC coverage is derived from this framework [@muharram2024]. SDG indicator 3.c.1 specifically tracks health worker density and distribution.

**OECD Health Statistics and Health at a Glance**: The OECD collects standardized data on practising doctors per 1,000 population, distinguishing between practising doctors, doctors licensed to practise, and doctors providing direct patient care. These data are collected annually with standardized definitions, though variations remain — some countries (Greece, Portugal) report 'all doctors licensed to practise' which overestimates active workforce by up to 20% [@oecd2025]. The OECD also tracks doctor age, gender, specialty, working hours, and geographic distribution using Territorial Level 2 regions.

**Gini Index for distribution measurement**: Muharram et al. (2024) used the Gini Index to quantify health worker distribution inequality in Indonesia at both provincial and district levels. This method allows within-country comparison of distribution equity over time. For Indonesia, this revealed that between-province specialist inequality decreased from 0.57 to 0.44 (1993-2022), but district-level inequality remained high at 0.53 [@muharram2024].

**Quadrant comparative approach**: Muharram et al. (2024) introduced a dual-dimensional quadrant method that simultaneously evaluates the quantity and equity of health worker distribution across provinces. This provides a more holistic assessment than density alone, identifying provinces that have both low density and high inequality vs. those with adequate density but distribution challenges.

**Limitations**: Data comparability is limited by differences in definitions, training standards, and inclusion of inactive/licensed doctors. The WHO cautions that human resources tend to concentrate in urban areas, so national averages do not reflect access for rural populations [@muharram2024].

## Key Points

- WHO NHWA provides standardized framework for international workforce comparison (70+ indicators)
- OECD Health Statistics tracks practising vs. licensed doctors, age, gender, specialty, and regional distribution
- Gini Index quantifies within-country distribution inequality — essential for Indonesia's archipelagic context
- Quadrant comparative approach combines quantity and equity assessment for more holistic policy guidance
- Significant comparability limitations persist due to differences in definition and data collection methods
