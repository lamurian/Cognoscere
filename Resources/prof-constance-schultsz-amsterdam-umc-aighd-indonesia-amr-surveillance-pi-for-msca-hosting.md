## Education

Prof. Constance Schultsz is an **MD** (medical doctorate) and **medical microbiologist** by training. She specialized in medical microbiology and completed her clinical training at Dutch university medical centres. She worked as a **Research Fellow at the International Centre for Diarrheal Diseases Research, Bangladesh (ICDDR)** in Dhaka, Bangladesh, and as a consultant microbiologist at the **VU University Medical Centre**. From 2003 to 2008 she headed the **Microbiology department at the Oxford University Clinical Research Unit (OUCRU), Vietnam**, at the Hospital for Tropical Diseases in Ho Chi Minh City. In 2008 she joined Amsterdam UMC, and in 2016 she was appointed **Professor of Global Health (Emerging Infectious Diseases and Antibiotic Resistance)** at the University of Amsterdam (UvA) and **Deputy Head of the Department of Global Health** [@schultsz-amc; @schultsz-aighd].

## Current Ongoing Research

Prof. Schultsz's research aims to drive changes in the prevention and management of bacterial infections to improve global health, with a focus on antimicrobial drug resistance and zoonotic infections. Key research streams:

1. **SPIN Project (Indonesia)** — Novel strategies and tools for AMR surveillance in Bandung (Java) and Medan (Sumatra), Indonesia. Validates and implements LQAS-based AMR surveillance for urinary tract infections, paired with capacity building in microbiology using tele-microbiology approaches. Partners: Universitas Padjadjaran (UNPAD), Universitas Sumatera Utara (USU) [@schultsz-aighd].
2. **HECTOR Consortium (EU JPI-AMR)** — Coordinating international research on antimicrobial resistance transmission and surveillance.
3. **AMR-Global PPP** — Chairs this public-private partnership bridging academic research with industry solutions for antimicrobial resistance.
4. **Next-generation sequencing for AMR surveillance** — Developing and applying novel genomic tools for tracking resistance determinants.
5. **Streptococcus suis research** — Using this zoonotic pathogen as a model to study emerging infectious diseases from livestock production, primarily in Vietnam and Thailand.
6. **Telemicrobiology** — Digital approaches for remote microbiology capacity building and knowledge exchange in LMICs.

## Relevance to LoRaWAN/FHIR Mesh Network for Rural Digital Health

Prof. Schultsz's work offers a **strong complement** to the proposed research, particularly in the context of digital health for infectious disease management in rural Indonesia:

- **Digital surveillance infrastructure**: Her telemicrobiology work directly parallels the proposed LoRaWAN-Protobuf-FHIR pipeline. Both involve transmitting clinical data from remote/rural settings to central systems for analysis and decision-making. A LoRa mesh network could extend telemicrobiology to the most remote puskesmas without internet connectivity.
- **Indonesia field presence**: The SPIN project's established partnerships with UNPAD (Bandung) and USU (Medan) provide existing field infrastructure, ethics approvals, and community relationships that an MSCA fellow could leverage.
- **Data pipeline architecture**: The SPIN project's LQAS surveillance generates structured microbiological data that could be transmitted via Protobuf over LoRa. Converting this to FHIR would align with Indonesia's SATUSEHAT national HIE platform, enabling integration with the national health data ecosystem.
- **Public-private partnership**: As Chair of AMR-Global PPP, she has direct connections with diagnostic and pharmaceutical industry partners, supporting the industry collaboration dimension of the MSCA proposal.
- **Remote monitoring for AMR**: The proposed sensor-to-cloud architecture could monitor AMR-relevant clinical parameters (e.g., antibiotic usage, treatment outcomes) in rural settings where laboratory access is limited.

**Limitation**: Her primary focus is AMR and infectious diseases rather than general digital health implementation. The MSCA proposal would need to bridge her AMR surveillance expertise with the applicant's digital health/engineering background — a natural fit for a LoRaWAN-based health monitoring system that transmits clinical data from rural health facilities.
