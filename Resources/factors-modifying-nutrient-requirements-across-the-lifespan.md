## Summary

Nutrient requirements vary across the lifespan due to changes in body composition, metabolism, and physiological demands. Age-related differences are substantial: children and adolescents need more calcium and iron per kg body weight for growth, older adults over 70 need more vitamin D (15 mcg vs 5 mcg for younger adults) and vitamin B6 (1.7 mg vs 1.3 mg). Older adults also have reduced gastric acid secretion, impairing B12 absorption—supplemental B12 is recommended after age 50 [@instituteofmedicineuscommitteeonuseofdietaryreferenceintakesinnutritionlabeling2003].

Sex differences reflect body size and reproductive function. Women need more iron (18 mg vs 8 mg) during reproductive years due to menstrual losses, and calcium requirements increase after menopause (1,200 mg) to offset bone loss. Pregnancy and lactation increase requirements substantially—folate rises from 400 to 600 mcg DFE, iron from 18 to 27 mg, and zinc from 8 to 11–12 mg [@instituteofmedicineuscommitteeonuseofdietaryreferenceintakesinnutritionlabeling2003]. Physical activity level modifies energy requirements and, consequently, absolute micronutrient needs. Individuals with high energy expenditure require higher absolute intakes of B vitamins, vitamin C, iron, zinc, and magnesium to support increased metabolism and compensate for sweat losses. Smokers need an additional 35 mg/day of vitamin C compared to non-smokers due to increased oxidative stress [@usfoodanddrugadministration2024].
