## Summary

The WHO UHC Service Coverage Index (SDG 3.8.1) tracks the coverage of essential health services on a 0-100 scale based on 14 tracer indicators across reproductive/maternal/child health, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, and service capacity/access. For 2023, the latest available data shows Thailand at 80, Indonesia at 67, Finland at 86, and Denmark at 85 [@worldhealthorganization2026].

Health expenditure per capita in PPP international dollars reveals stark differences. Using OECD data (2024), Thailand spends $1,102 per capita while Indonesia spends $417. By contrast, Finland spends $6,655 and Denmark $7,071 [@wikipediacontributors2026]. WHO data (2022) shows similar patterns: Thailand $1,107, Indonesia $390, Finland $6,048, Denmark $7,012.

These figures demonstrate that Thailand achieves an SCI of 80 — approaching the Nordic levels of 85-86 — at roughly one-sixth the per capita spending of Finland or Denmark. Indonesia, with an SCI of 67 and spending of ~$400, lags significantly on both service coverage and financial investment. The comparison underscores that system design (primary care centrality, task-shifting, capitation financing) matters as much as absolute spending for achieving UHC coverage.

## Key Points

| Country | UHC SCI (2023) | Health spending/Capita (2024, PPP) | Doctors/1,000 |
|---------|---------------|-----------------------------------|----------------|
| Thailand | 80 | $1,102 | ~0.8 |
| Indonesia | 67 | $417 | ~0.62 |
| Finland | 86 | $6,655 | ~3.3 |
| Denmark | 85 | $7,071 | ~4.0 |

- Thailand achieves SCI 80 at $1,102/capita — 6x less than Nordic peers for comparable coverage
- Indonesia trails both on SCI (67) and spending ($417), needing both more investment and better system design
- Primary care infrastructure and task-shifting (VHVs, nurses) are the key efficiency multipliers for Thailand
- The data reinforces that UHC outcomes depend more on how money is spent than how much is spent
