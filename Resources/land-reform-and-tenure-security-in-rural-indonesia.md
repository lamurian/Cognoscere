## The Structural Problem

Land inequality is the most persistent structural barrier to rural modernization in Indonesia. Key statistics:
- 68% of land is controlled by just 1% of the population
- The government prioritizes large corporate concessions (plantations, mining, infrastructure) over smallholder access
- 3,200+ agrarian conflicts occurred between 2015-2024, affecting 7.4 million hectares and 1.8 million households
- Conflicts often involve smallholder farmers being displaced by plantation permits, mining concessions, or infrastructure projects

## Why Land Tenure Matters for GDP

Without secure tenure, farmers will not invest in long-term improvements:
- Perennial crops (coffee, cocoa, fruit trees) take 3-7 years to mature
- Soil building, terracing, irrigation systems are multi-year investments
- Land title is the primary collateral required for formal credit
- Tenure insecurity drives short-term extraction behavior rather than sustainable stewardship

The World Bank links secure land rights directly to improved livelihoods and climate-resilient spatial planning.

## Recent Progress

The government's agrarian reform program has seen important victories:
- 2023: 135 hectares redistributed to 198 households in Gunung Anten Village, West Java
- 113 hectares to 150 families in Muktisari Village
- Around 400 out of 1,050 land certificates were given to women in successful redistribution cases
- Joint land titling (husband-wife) improves household investment outcomes

Ongoing initiatives include participatory mapping where communities document their own land use and customary rights, and clarifying forest boundaries to resolve overlapping claims.

## Remaining Challenges

- Ambitious redistribution targets often result in incomplete understanding of schemes, inappropriate site allocation, and inadequate consideration of community capacity
- Resources for granting land rights are not matched by support for post-redistribution livelihoods (extension services, credit, market access)
- Farm fragmentation creates ever-decreasing plot sizes, potentially trapping near-landless families
- Adat (customary) land rights are not always recognized in formal land office (BPN) registrations
