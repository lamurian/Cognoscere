The empirical correlation between psychological resources and resilience varies by resource type, measurement instrument, and population. No single meta-analysis aggregates across all resource-resilience pairings, but the available evidence permits a well-bounded estimate.

**Meta-analytic estimates.** Zhao, Tong, and Yang conducted a meta-analysis (66 studies, N > 10,000) of positive psychological resources and quality of life (QoL) in cancer patients, reporting: resilience (treated as a resource per CD-RISC) r = 0.71 (95% CI: 0.55–0.87), hope r = 0.50 (0.43–0.56), self-efficacy r = 0.53 (0.46–0.61), self-esteem r = 0.46 (0.28–0.63), and optimism r = 0.30 (0.19–0.40) [@zhao2022]. While these are correlations with QoL, not resilience itself, they reflect the interconnectedness of the resource network — resources correlate substantially with each other and with well-being outcomes.

**COR theory evidence.** Egozi Farkash et al. applied Hobfoll's COR framework during the COVID-19 pandemic and found: resource loss correlated with PTSD symptoms at r = 0.66 (p < 0.01), resource gain with personal resilience at r = 0.30 (p < 0.01), and resource loss with personal resilience inversely at r = -0.37 (p < 0.01) [@egozifarkash2022]. These represent the most direct resource-resilience correlations.

**Individual psychological resources.** He et al. found that dispositional optimism and psychological resilience (measured by CD-RISC) were significantly positively correlated in burn patients, with optimism predicting resilience at β = 0.48 in SEM [@he2013]. Nes and Segerstrom's meta-analysis found optimism related to active coping at r ≈ 0.17–0.30, depending on coping strategy [@nes2006].

**Range summary.** Across studies, the correlation between specific psychological resources and resilience measures falls in the range:

| Resource | Correlation with Resilience / Well-being | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Hope | r = 0.40–0.50 | Zhao 2022 (meta) |
| Self-efficacy | r = 0.35–0.53 | Zhao 2022 (meta) |
| Optimism | r = 0.20–0.48 | Zhao 2022, He 2013 |
| Self-esteem | r = 0.30–0.46 | Zhao 2022 (meta) |
| Aggregate resource gain | r = 0.30 | Egozi 2022 (COR) |
| Resource loss (inverse) | r = -0.25 to -0.37 | Egozi 2022, Zlobina 2024 |

A conservative synthesis places the typical bivariate correlation between a single psychological resource and a resilience measure in the range **r = 0.20 to 0.55**, with meta-analytic averages around **r ≈ 0.35–0.50** for the strongest predictors (self-efficacy, hope). The correlation is moderate-to-strong but not so high as to suggest construct redundancy, supporting the theoretical demarcation.

A key caveat from COR theory: resource loss correlates more strongly with distress (r ≈ 0.50–0.66) than resource gain correlates with positive outcomes (r ≈ 0.20–0.30) [@hobfoll2002; @egozifarkash2022]. This asymmetry suggests resources and resilience are related but functionally distinct constructs operating through different causal pathways.
