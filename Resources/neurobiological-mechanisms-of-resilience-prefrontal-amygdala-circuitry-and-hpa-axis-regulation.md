The brain is the central organ of stress reactivity, coping, and recovery [@mcewen2007]. Resilience at the neurobiological level involves a distributed neural circuitry that determines what is threatening and coordinates adaptive responses. Key regions include the prefrontal cortex (PFC), amygdala, hippocampus, and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.

Effective prefrontal regulation of amygdala reactivity is a core neural mechanism of resilience. Individuals who show stronger prefrontal-amygdala connectivity during emotion regulation tasks report better psychological outcomes after stress exposure. Cognitive reappraisal — a key process in PASTOR theory — engages the dorsolateral and ventrolateral PFC, which in turn down-regulates amygdala activity [@kalisch2015].

At the molecular level, the LPA-LPA1 signalling pathway in the limbic system is implicated in emotional regulation and coping responses to chronic stress. Dysfunction in this pathway may increase vulnerability to depression and cognitive deficits, particularly in ageing populations [@moreno-villanueva2017]. Other molecular systems involved include the serotonin transporter (5-HTT) gene, brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), and cortisol regulation via FKBP5, which modulate HPA axis sensitivity and stress recovery.

Heart rate variability (HRV) serves as a peripheral index of prefrontal inhibitory control over the amygdala via the neurovisceral integration model. Higher resting HRV is associated with better emotion regulation capacity and greater resilience, providing an objective biomarker for resilience research.
