## Summary

A qualitative study of 90 participants across Sumatra, Java, Kalimantan, and Papua identified three primary barriers to mental health service access in Indonesia. First, geographical barriers and travel costs make services inaccessible for rural populations, with some travelling 60km or more to reach psychiatric units that only exist in large hospitals [@munira2023].

Second, stigma and lack of social support cause significant delays in treatment. Participants reported stigma from family members, neighbours, healthcare workers, and even general practitioners who refused to believe depression complaints or provide referral letters. One participant reported being rejected for referral letters twice before self-harm scars prompted acceptance [@munira2023].

Third, expensive treatment costs without national health insurance (BPJS Kesehatan) create financial barriers. Without BPJS membership, psychiatric treatment costs are prohibitive for low-income populations. The study found 64.4% of participants had household incomes below the district minimum average, and 56.7% were unemployed [@munira2023].

The expert consensus identified three interconnected essences underlying these barriers: high levels of stigma across all socioeconomic layers, lack of awareness in handling mental health issues in workplaces and educational centres, and phenomena of self-diagnosis leading to delayed treatment [@basrowi2024].

## Key Points
- Geographical barriers: rural populations travel 60+ km to psychiatric units
- Stigma from family, community, and healthcare workers delays treatment
- GP refusal to provide referral letters is a documented systemic barrier
- Financial barriers: 64.4% of affected populations have low income
- BPJS Kesehatan membership is a critical facilitator when available
- Three essences: stigma, lack of awareness, self-diagnosis culture

## Confidence: High
Qualitative evidence from Munira et al. 2023 (90 participants, 4 islands) corroborated by expert consensus of 45 specialists (Basrowi et al. 2024).
