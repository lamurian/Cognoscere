Several theoretical frameworks explain how organisational-level factors interact with individual-level resilience mechanisms.

The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model provides the most comprehensive framework, positioning individual resilience as a personal resource that buffers the impact of job demands on strain [@bakker2017a]. Within JD-R, organisational factors (job resources, workplace climate, leadership) are antecedents that either foster or deplete this personal resource. The dual-process pathway means organisational resources trigger a motivational process leading to engagement and resilience-building, while excessive demands trigger a health impairment process that erodes resilience.

Conservation of Resources (COR) theory offers a complementary lens: organisational factors represent resource caravans that can either provide or block access to resources needed for resilience [@hobfoll2001]. Hindrance stressors threaten current resources or block resource acquisition, while challenge stressors represent risky investments with potential resource gains. Resilient employees have larger resource reservoirs and are better positioned to invest resources for further gains.

Systemic resilience theory, as articulated by Ungar (2018), identifies seven cross-system principles: resilience occurs in contexts of adversity; it is a process (not trait); there are trade-offs between co-occurring systems; resilient systems are open, dynamic, and complex; they promote connectivity; they demonstrate experimentation and learning; and they include diversity, redundancy, and participation [@ungar2018]. Applied to organisations, this framework suggests that individual resilience cannot be understood in isolation from the organisational systems in which employees operate, and that interventions at multiple system levels are needed.

The Resilience Activation Framework proposes that access to social resources activates and sustains resilience capabilities after adversity [@abramson2015]. Organisational factors function as social resources that activate individual-level resilience mechanisms. When problems are shared, so must be solutions -- collective resilience capacities emerge from organisational systems.

Transactional models (Jaffee et al., 2007; Lazarus & Folkman, 1984) emphasise that individual resilience is best understood as the interplay between individual characteristics, life circumstances, and context [@sippel2015]. The same individual resilience mechanisms (positive appraisal, emotion regulation) may produce different outcomes depending on organisational context, because context shapes which coping strategies are available, appropriate, and effective.

A key limitation across these frameworks is insufficient integration. The JD-R and CHM frameworks rarely incorporate neurobiological mechanisms, while systemic resilience models often lack the specificity to guide empirical hypothesis-testing in workplace settings.
