Titoism, also called market socialism, was developed by Josip Broz Tito in Yugoslavia after its split from the Soviet Union in 1948. It rejected Soviet domination and Stalinist central planning in favor of workers' self-management, where factory workers elected their own management councils. Market mechanisms allocated goods and services within a socialist ownership framework [@thulin2026].

This model achieved higher living standards and consumer goods availability than Soviet-style economies. Yugoslavia maintained non-aligned foreign policy and more open borders. However, the system faced coordination problems, regional disparities, and ultimately disintegrated along ethnic lines in the 1990s during the Yugoslav Wars.
