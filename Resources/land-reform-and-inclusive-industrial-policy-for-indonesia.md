Land inequality is Indonesia's most persistent structural barrier: 68% of land is controlled by 1% of the population, with 3,200+ agrarian conflicts (2015-2024) affecting 7.4 million hectares. Without secure tenure, farmers cannot invest in improvements or access formal credit. Government redistribution has been limited, and post-redistribution support remains inadequate. See [[Land Reform and Tenure Security in Rural Indonesia]] for details.

Industrial downstreaming, especially nickel, has created enclave economies. The nickel industry caused US$387M in agricultural and fisheries losses while emissions could cause 3,800 deaths annually [@askar2024]. Communities bear pollution costs while profits flow to politically-connected companies.

World Bank recommendations [@worldbank2016]: raise infrastructure investment from 3-4% to 7% of GDP, improve the 114th/189 Ease of Doing Business ranking, and reform rigid labor regulations. High severance costs (100+ weeks) and uncertain minimum wage setting discourage formal hiring, trapping most workers in informal jobs.
