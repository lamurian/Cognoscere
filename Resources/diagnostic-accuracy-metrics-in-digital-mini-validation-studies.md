## Summary

Across validation studies of digital mental health assessment tools (including MINI-based and MINI-validated tools), diagnostic accuracy metrics show wide variability influenced by study design, population, and reference standard.

## Key Points

### Overall Ranges (Martin-Key et al., 2022)
- **Sensitivity**: 0.32 – 1.00
- **Specificity**: 0.37 – 1.00
- **AUC**: 0.57 – 0.98 (poor to excellent)
- **Youden Index**: Used to select optimal cutoffs; ranges varied by tool

### By Tool Type

| Tool Category | Typical Sensitivity | Typical Specificity | Typical AUC |
|---|---|---|---|
| Digitized questionnaires (GAD-7, PHQ-9) | 0.75–0.89 | 0.55–0.79 | 0.65–0.90 |
| Computerized adaptive tests (CAT-MH) | 0.82–0.96 | 0.64–0.88 | 0.80–0.93 |
| Multi-condition screening (WSQ, M-3, e-PASS) | 0.42–0.94 | 0.52–1.00 | 0.72–0.98 |
| MINI as reference standard | — | — | — |

### MINI as Gold Standard Performance
Original MINI validation studies (clinician-administered vs SCID/CIDI):
- Kappa coefficients: 0.65–0.85 across disorders [@sheehan1997a; @lecrubier1997a]
- Sensitivity: 0.75–0.92
- Specificity: 0.90–0.99
- PPV: 0.60–0.86; NPV: 0.92–0.99

### Factors Affecting Accuracy
1. **Population prevalence**: Low prevalence → low PPV, high NPV (WSQ study)
2. **Sample type**: Psychiatric vs general population samples yield different estimates
3. **Time interval**: Same-day to 34 days between index and reference test
4. **Blinding**: Many studies unclear or unblinded reference standard interpretation
5. **Threshold selection**: Few studies used prespecified cutoffs

### Clinical Implications
- Digital tools generally better at ruling out (high NPV) than ruling in (low PPV)
- Specificity tends to exceed sensitivity → fewer false positives, more false negatives
- Adaptive tools (CAT-MH) show promise for efficiency and accuracy
- Simple digitization preserves psychometric properties but doesn't improve them
