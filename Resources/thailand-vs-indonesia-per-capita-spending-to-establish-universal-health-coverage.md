## Summary

Thailand achieved universal health coverage in 2001-2002 through the tax-funded Universal Coverage Scheme (UCS) at a capitation rate of 1,202.40 baht per person per year at launch [@tamronglak2015; @tangcharoensathien2020]. Indonesia launched its contributory National Health Insurance (JKN) in 2014 with a Class III basic premium of IDR 25,500 per person per month (IDR 306,000 per year) [@banerjee2019]. In nominal US dollar terms, these figures are remarkably similar: approximately $28.6/person/year for Thailand (2002) and $26.6/person/year for Indonesia (2014). However, the two programs differ fundamentally in design, institutional preconditions, and the total health expenditure context in which they operate.

### Thailand's UCS launch cost and context

Thailand's UCS was a non-contributory, tax-funded scheme financed from general government revenue. At launch in fiscal year 2002, the NHSO allocated a capitation of 1,202.40 baht per member per year for a comprehensive benefit package covering outpatient, inpatient, preventive, and high-cost care [@tangcharoensathien2020; @yiengprugsawan2010]. In PPP terms (using a 2002 conversion factor of approximately 12-13 THB per international dollar), this equates to roughly $92-100 Int$ per person per year. The total UCS budget for 2003 was 56,091 million baht (~$1.6 billion), accounting for about 1.1% of GDP and 6% of the national budget [@tamronglak2015]. Only a nominal 30-baht co-payment per visit (~$0.75) was charged, abolished entirely in 2006.

Crucially, Thailand's UCS was not built from scratch. It was layered onto a primary healthcare foundation developed over the preceding 2-3 decades, including the district health system (1970s), community hospital network, and the Village Health Volunteer program (since 1977). This prior infrastructure significantly reduced the incremental cost of extending coverage to the final uninsured 30% of the population [@yiengprugsawan2010].

### Indonesia's JKN launch cost and context

Indonesia's JKN, launched on January 1, 2014, operates as a social health insurance model with mandatory contributions. For non-poor informal workers (JKN Mandiri), the Class III basic monthly premium was set at IDR 25,500 per person (~$2), equivalent to approximately 3.5% of average monthly per capita expenditure for eligible households [@banerjee2019]. In annual terms, this is IDR 306,000 per person per year. In PPP terms (2014 conversion factor of approximately 4,500 IDR per Int$), this equals roughly $68 Int$ per person per year.

The poor (Penerima Bantuan Iuran, or PBI) receive fully subsidized coverage at the same rate, funded by central and local government, and this subsidy makes up approximately 42% of the JKN budget. JKN covered 121 million people at launch in 2014 and has since expanded to cover 280 million (98% of the population) as of 2025 [@worldbank2026].

Unlike Thailand, Indonesia faced higher implementation costs because JKN consolidated approximately 300 fragmented health insurance schemes into a single-payer system. The BPJS Kesehatan administrative apparatus had to be built from the existing PT Askes structure, and the country's archipelagic geography, weak civil registry, and severe health workforce shortages (0.62 doctors per 1,000) added further cost pressures [@banerjee2019; @commonwealthfund2026].

## Key Points

### Launch per capita cost comparison

| Metric | Thailand UCS (2002) | Indonesia JKN (2014) |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Per person per year (nominal USD) | ~$28.6 (1,202 THB) | ~$26.6 (IDR 306K) |
| Per person per year (PPP Int$) | ~$92-100 | ~$68 |
| Financing model | Tax-funded (non-contributory) | Contributory (SHI with subsidies) |
| Co-payment at launch | 30 THB/visit (~$0.75) | None (except for certain services) |
| Population covered at launch | ~47M (75% of pop) | ~121M (48% of pop) |
| Prior PHC infrastructure | 2-3 decades of investment | Less developed; puskesmas since 1968 |

### Current outcomes (2023-2024)

| Metric | Thailand | Indonesia |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| UHC Service Coverage Index (2023) | 80 | 67 |
| Health spending per capita (2024, PPP) | $1,102 | $417 |
| Health spending as % of GDP | ~3.5% | ~2.7% |
| Doctors per 1,000 | 0.8 | 0.62 |

### Why Thailand achieved more with comparable launch spending

Three structural factors explain Thailand's superior efficiency. First, the UCS was non-contributory and tax-funded, eliminating the administrative burden of premium collection from informal workers -- a challenge that continues to plague Indonesia's JKN Mandiri, where natural enrollment reached only approximately 8% in the first year [@banerjee2019]. Second, Thailand's capitation model with primary care gatekeeping inherently contains costs by routing patients through lower-cost primary care before specialist referral. Indonesia's JKN also uses capitation for primary care, but its weaker gatekeeping and fragmented referral system drive higher costs. Third, Thailand invested in primary care infrastructure for decades before launching UCS, whereas Indonesia expanded coverage while simultaneously trying to build supply-side capacity, including addressing a shortfall of approximately 140,000 GPs [@muharram2024].

### Cost as percentage of GDP per capita

Thailand's capitation of 1,202 THB represented roughly 1.4% of per capita GDP (~84,000 THB in 2002). Indonesia's JKN premium of IDR 306,000 per person per year represented roughly 0.77% of per capita GDP (~IDR 40 million in 2014). However, when factoring that a household pays for all family members, the burden is higher: for a family of four, JKN costs approximately IDR 1.22M/year, or about 3.1% of per capita GDP per family member.

### Current spending trajectory

Over two decades, Thailand's UCS capitation grew from 1,202 THB (2002) to 2,895 THB (2014), more than doubling, while total health spending per capita reached $1,102 PPP (2024). Indonesia's total health spending per capita ($417 PPP in 2024) remains substantially lower, reflecting both lower income levels and a smaller fiscal commitment to health. Indonesia's UHC SCI of 67 (vs Thailand's 80) indicates significant room for improvement, but doing so will require both increased spending and systematic strengthening of primary care infrastructure, workforce, and institutional capacity -- the same foundations that took Thailand decades to build.
