## Summary

This research synthesis covers daily macro and micronutrient requirements for adults based on the Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs) established by the National Academies. The DRI framework [Resources/dietary-reference-intake-framework-ear-rda-ai-ul] provides four reference values (EAR, RDA, AI, UL) that form the foundation for nutrient recommendations worldwide.

For macronutrients, the Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Ranges (AMDRs) are: carbohydrate 45–65%, fat 20–35%, and protein 10–35% of total energy [Resources/acceptable-macronutrient-distribution-ranges-for-adults]. These ranges balance nutrient adequacy with chronic disease risk reduction. Macronutrients sustain body functions through energy provision, essential amino and fatty acid supply, and metabolic regulation [Resources/evidence-supporting-macronutrient-roles-in-adult-health].

For micronutrients, detailed RDAs and AIs exist for all essential vitamins [Resources/micronutrient-rdas-and-ais-for-adult-vitamins] and minerals [Resources/micronutrient-rdas-and-ais-for-adult-minerals-and-trace-elements]. Understanding these requirements is essential because inadequate intake impairs immune function [Resources/micronutrients-and-immune-function] and contributes to the global burden of non-communicable diseases [Resources/micronutrient-deficiencies-and-chronic-disease-risk]. Requirements are established through biomarker, balance, and epidemiological studies [Resources/methods-for-determining-dietary-reference-intakes], and vary significantly by age, sex, pregnancy status, and activity level [Resources/factors-modifying-nutrient-requirements-across-the-lifespan].

## Key Takeaway

Adequate nutrient intake is fundamental to adult health. The DRI evidence base shows that suboptimal intakes are widespread in both developing and industrialised countries, creating a preventable burden of disease that affects immune competence, metabolic health, and long-term chronic disease risk.
