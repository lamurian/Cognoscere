Economic evaluation in healthcare and workplace interventions rests on four primary frameworks, each defining 'value' and 'effect' differently:[@Le2021Costeffectiveness; @Neumann2018Value]

**Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA)** measures outcomes in natural physical units—symptom-free days, depression cases averted, resilience score improvements on validated scales (e.g., CD-RISC). The result is an Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio (ICER): (Cost_intervention - Cost_control) / (Effect_intervention - Effect_control). Decisions rely on whether the ICER falls below a willingness-to-pay (WTP) threshold per unit of effect.[@Neumann2018Value]

**Cost-Utility Analysis (CUA)** is a subset of CEA that combines morbidity and mortality into a single metric—the Quality-Adjusted Life Year (QALY) or Disability-Adjusted Life Year (DALY). QALYs capture both length and quality of life on a 0-1 utility scale. CUA is the preferred framework for health technology assessment agencies such as NICE (UK) and PBAC (Australia). The standard WTP threshold is typically $50,000 per QALY in the US, or £20,000–30,000 per QALY in the UK.[@Le2021Costeffectiveness; @Nimdet2015Systematic]

**Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)** measures all outcomes in monetary terms, allowing direct comparison of costs and benefits. Willingness-to-pay surveys or revealed preference methods are used to assign dollar values to health improvements. CBA addresses the question: does the monetary value of benefits exceed costs?[@Neumann2018Value]

**Return on Investment (ROI)** compares the net financial returns of an intervention to its costs, often expressed as a ratio (e.g., $3 returned per $1 invested). ROI studies typically capture cost offsets (reduced absenteeism, healthcare savings) rather than health benefits directly, making them narrower than full CBA.[@Le2021Costeffectiveness]

**Perspective matters.** CEA/CUA typically adopt a societal or healthcare sector perspective, while Value-Based Healthcare (VBHC) adopts a patient perspective. For workplace interventions, the employer perspective (focusing on productivity, absenteeism, turnover) is common alongside societal perspectives that include healthcare savings and broader wellbeing gains.[@Neumann2018Value]
