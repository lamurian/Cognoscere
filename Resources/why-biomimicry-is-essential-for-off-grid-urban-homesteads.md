## Summary

Biomimicry is essential for sustainable urban homesteads because nature has already solved the core challenges of off-grid living: energy autonomy, water cycling, waste elimination, and climate regulation. Over 3.8 billion years, natural systems have developed closed-loop processes where waste equals food, energy comes from renewable sources, and form follows function [@verbrugghe2023]. For urban homesteads seeking independence from grid infrastructure, these biological principles provide a proven design framework. The building sector accounts for ~40% of energy-related CO2 emissions, and cities consume 70% of global resources [@borham2024].

Current sustainability focuses on doing less harm, but biomimicry enables regenerative design where buildings restore ecosystems — a shift from degrading to net-positive [@pedersenzari2020]. Biomimicry operates at three nested levels: organism (mimicking form, e.g., lotus-leaf self-cleaning), behaviour (mimicking processes, e.g., termite-mound ventilation), and ecosystem (mimicking closed-loop resource systems) [@verbrugghe2023]. Ecosystem-level biomimicry is most critical for off-grid autonomy as it addresses how buildings interact with their urban environment as living systems rather than isolated machines.

## Key Points
- Nature provides closed-loop models for energy, water, and waste — the pillars of off-grid autonomy
- Three levels of biomimicry (organism, behaviour, ecosystem) offer scalable strategies
- Regenerative buildings produce net-positive ecosystem services [@pedersenzari2020]
