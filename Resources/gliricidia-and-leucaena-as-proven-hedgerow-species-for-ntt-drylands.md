Scientific research conducted in Oebola Village, Fatuleu District, Kupang Regency (West Timor) from 2008-2009 tested three hedgerow plants in a conservation farming system: Leucaena leucocephala (lamtoro), Sesbania grandiflora (turi), and Gliricidia sepium (gamal). The study measured soil physical properties including organic matter content, bulk density, field water capacity, maximum water content, aggregate stability, and infiltration rate [@matheus2009].

Results showed that gamal (Gliricidia sepium) gave the best performance across most parameters. Infiltration rate for gamal was 0.95 cm/hour compared to 0.79 for lamtoro, 0.75 for turi, and only 0.59 for the control without hedgerows. Organic matter content was highest under gamal (2.87%) versus 2.45% for lamtoro and 2.36% for turi, compared to 1.76% in control plots. Bulk density was lowest (best) under gamal at 1.02 g/cm3 versus 1.46 for control, indicating better soil porosity. Field water capacity and maximum water content were also highest under gamal [@matheus2009].

Gamal's superior performance is attributed to its wider, lignin-rich leaves that decompose slowly, maintaining continuous soil cover and moisture retention through the dry season [@matheus2009]. Leucaena leucocephala remains critically important in West Timor farming, particularly the psyllid-resistant cv. Tarramba variety introduced in Amarasi, where its deep tap roots (penetrating 8m) access subsoil moisture and its leaves provide 25% protein animal feed [@reville2017; @ngongo2022]. Leucaena is also traditionally used in the amarasi slash-and-burn system, planted at high density, cut, burnt, and followed by maize, demonstrating its cultural integration into Timorese farming [@ngongo2022].

Key sources: @matheus2009, @reville2017, @ngongo2022.
