An economic system is the framework a society uses to allocate scarce resources, answering what, how, and for whom to produce. Systems range across a spectrum from pure market (capitalism) to pure command (communism), with most nations operating a mixed model [@morganelli2025].

Capitalism is defined by private ownership, market prices, and profit incentives. Its main variants are laissez-faire (minimal state), social market (welfare capitalism), state capitalism (government-led investment), and crony capitalism (patronage-driven business). Each balances efficiency and equity differently [@thulin2026].

Communism aims for common ownership and a classless society. Its main implemented variants are Marxism-Leninism (Soviet central planning), Maoism (peasant-led revolution with continuous struggle), Titoism or market socialism (workers' self-management with markets), and Eurocommunism (democratic parliamentary path). The Soviet Union, China, Yugoslavia, and Western European parties each operationalized these ideals with different outcomes.

The fundamental differences are ownership (private vs state), allocation (markets vs planning), and incentives (profit vs social duty). Both are totalizing industrial-age ideologies with internal factions, and both can coexist with autocracy or democracy. No real economy is purely one type — all are mixed to varying degrees.
