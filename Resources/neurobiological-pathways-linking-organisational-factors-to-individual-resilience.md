The neurobiological pathways through which organisational factors modulate individual resilience involve three interconnected systems: the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, the oxytocinergic system, and prefrontal-amygdala circuitry.

Social support from colleagues, supervisors, and the organisation at large reduces physiological stress reactivity through oxytocin-mediated mechanisms [@sippel2015]. Positive social interactions trigger oxytocin release, which has anxiolytic effects and reduces amygdala reactivity while enhancing prefrontal cortex activity. This neurochemical cascade inhibits sympathetic nervous system and HPA axis activation, lowering cortisol levels and reducing the allostatic load that undermines resilience capacity over time [@sippel2015].

Workplaces characterised by high social support and positive leadership show evidence of reduced cardiovascular and neuroendocrine responses to acute stressors compared to low-support environments [@mcewen2007]. The ventromedial prefrontal cortex detects safety cues and inhibits fear responses, a process enhanced by the presence of supportive others. Coan et al. (2006) demonstrated that neural threat responses were attenuated when participants held a partner's hand, with stronger effects for higher-quality relationships -- a finding with direct implications for organisational support systems [@sippel2015].

Chronic exposure to organisational stressors (role ambiguity, interpersonal conflict, abusive supervision) activates the HPA axis and sympathetic nervous system repeatedly, leading to allostatic load -- the cumulative wear and tear on physiological systems [@mcewen2007]. This impairs prefrontal-amygdala circuitry, reducing cognitive control over emotional reactivity and weakening the neural substrate for reappraisal and interference inhibition identified by PASTOR theory [@kalisch2015].

Genetic vulnerabilities (e.g., serotonin transporter gene short allele) that increase stress sensitivity can be moderated by social support. Kaufman et al. (2004) found that high social support protected against depression in maltreated children carrying the short allele, indicating that organisational support might modify genetic expression or influence [@sippel2015].

The direct neural evidence linking specific organisational factors (leadership style, culture, job design) to neurobiological outcomes in working populations remains scarce. Most evidence comes from general population, clinical, or animal studies, representing a significant gap in the literature.
