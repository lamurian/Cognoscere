## Summary

Natural buildings using cob, straw bale, and earth-sheltering achieve thermal performance comparable to or exceeding conventional construction through different mechanisms. While conventional buildings rely on active mechanical systems and synthetic insulation, natural buildings leverage thermal mass, phase change, and passive solar principles. Cob walls (300-500mm thick) provide high thermal mass with conductivity of 0.3-0.5 W/mK, absorbing solar heat during the day and releasing it slowly at night. Straw bale walls offer R-1.45 per inch (approximately R-35 for a 500mm bale), comparable to high-performance foam insulation but with negative embodied carbon — straw sequesters CO₂ during growth [@jideofor2012].

Earth-sheltered buildings benefit from stable ground temperatures of 10-15°C year-round at depth, reducing heating demand by 30-50% compared to above-grade construction [@jideofor2012]. Passive solar design is essential: south-facing glazing with appropriate overhangs, thermal mass in floors or interior walls, and careful orientation. Simon Dale's Undercroft uses a reclaimed glasshouse as a sunspace on the south side, capturing solar energy while growing food year-round [@voiceofnature2022]. The building sector accounts for approximately 40% of global CO₂ emissions, so even modest efficiency gains from natural building scale significantly [@verbrugghe2023].

## Key Points

- Straw bale walls: R-30+ insulation, zero or negative embodied carbon (CO₂ sequestered in straw)
- Cob thermal mass: conductivity 0.3-0.5 W/mK, stabilises diurnal temperature swings passively
- Earth sheltering: 10-15°C stable ground temp cuts HVAC demand by 30-50%
- Passive solar design is integral, not optional — orientation, glazing ratio, and shading must be designed together
- Natural buildings regulate indoor humidity (30-60% RH) through vapour-permeable wall assemblies, improving comfort and health
