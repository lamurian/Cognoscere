## Summary

Indonesia faces a severe and well-documented shortage of both general practitioners and specialist doctors. As of October 2025, Indonesia had 276,646 registered doctors: 171,388 general practitioners, 54,626 specialists, 44,587 dentists, and 6,045 specialist dentists [@antaranews2025]. The WHO-recommended ratio of 1 doctor per 1,000 population would require approximately 280,000 GPs for Indonesia's 280 million population, leaving a shortfall of roughly 140,000 GPs [@antaranews2025]. Specialist density is even lower at 0.17-0.30 per 1,000 population, compared to an ideal of 0.53 per 1,000 [@muharram2024].

Distribution is severely imbalanced. Muharram et al. (2024) found that Indonesia's health worker ratio stands at 3.84 per 1,000 population, below WHO's threshold of 4.45 needed for 80% UHC coverage. The overall shortfall is approximately 166,000 health workers [@muharram2024]. The Gini Index for specialist distribution at the district level is 0.53, indicating high inequality, with specialists concentrated in major cities and provincial capitals. Approximately 59% of specialists work on Java island, leaving other regions severely underserved [@antaranews2025; @muharram2024].

The WHO Indonesia office reports that only 78.1% of public hospitals have the complete set of seven basic medical specialist types (radiology, anaesthesiology, clinical pathology, surgery, paediatrics, obstetrics and gynecology, internal medicine). Only 65% of puskesmas (primary health centres) meet the minimum requirement of nine basic health worker categories [@worldhealthorganizationindonesia2026]. Current medical faculties produce approximately 12,000 GPs per year, meaning that even with current production, closing the GP gap would take approximately a decade without policy intervention [@antaranews2025]. By 2032, the Ministry of Health estimates a deficit of approximately 65,000 specialists as demand rises with population growth.

## Key Points

- GP shortfall: ~140,000; specialist shortfall: ~70,000; total gap: ~210,000 doctors
- Health worker density: 3.84 per 1,000 (WHO threshold for UHC: 4.45)
- 59% of specialists concentrated on Java island; Gini Index 0.53 at district level
- Only 78.1% of public hospitals have complete essential specialist coverage
- Current production: ~12,000 GPs/year — a decade needed to close gap at current rate

## Confidence: High (supported by multiple government, WHO, and academic sources)
