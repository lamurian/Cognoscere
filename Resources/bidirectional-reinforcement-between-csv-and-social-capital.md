Empirical research confirms a bidirectional relationship between Creating Shared Value (CSV) and social capital. Pre-existing social capital — measured as municipality place attachment, local community impact, intrapersonal social capital, and collective social capital — facilitates successful CSV implementation [@garczarekbk2026]. Communities with strong place attachment and collective efficacy provide fertile ground for corporate-community partnerships. CSV initiatives in turn strengthen social capital by providing new avenues for civic involvement, deepening place attachment, and enriching interpersonal relationships. The covariance between CSV and social capital is very strong (r=0.82, p<0.001) [@garczarekbk2026]. Social capital positively predicts subjective wellbeing (beta=0.75, p<0.001), and wellbeing drives sharing mutual value behaviors. This creates a virtuous cycle where corporate action and community wellbeing reinforce each other. However, CSV initiatives disconnected from core business strategy fail to build genuine trust [@garczarekbk2026].
