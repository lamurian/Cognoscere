The PSS-10 shows a moderate-to-strong negative correlation with resilience as measured by the Brief Resilience Scale (BRS) and Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC). Higher resilience predicts lower perceived stress.

Thomas and Zolkoski [@thomas2020] found r = -0.57 (p < .05) between BRS and PSS-10 in 277 undergraduates, with path analysis showing resilience negatively predicting perceived stress at β = -0.57. Rodriguez-Rey et al. [@rodriguezrey2016] reported a negative BRS-PSS correlation in the Spanish BRS validation, supporting convergent validity.

This relationship fits the stress-and-coping framework. People with higher resilience appraise stressful events as more manageable and recover faster, which lowers perceived stress. Resilience acts as a protective factor that buffers the cognitive and emotional impact of stressors.
