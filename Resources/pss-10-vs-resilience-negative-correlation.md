The Perceived Stress Scale-10 (PSS-10) and resilience measures such as the Brief Resilience Scale (BRS) and Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC) consistently show a moderate-to-strong negative correlation.

Thomas and Zolkoski (2020) found r = -0.57 (p < .05) between BRS and PSS-10 in a sample of 277 undergraduate students, with path analysis showing resilience negatively predicting perceived stress at β = -0.57 [@thomas2020]. Rodríguez-Rey et al. (2016) reported a negative correlation between BRS and PSS in the Spanish BRS validation study, supporting convergent validity.

This relationship is theoretically grounded in the stress-and-coping framework: individuals with higher resilience appraise stressful events as more manageable and recover more quickly, resulting in lower perceived stress. Resilience functions as a protective factor that buffers the cognitive and emotional impact of stressors.
