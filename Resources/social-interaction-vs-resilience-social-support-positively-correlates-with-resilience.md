Perceived social support and the frequency/quality of social interactions show consistent positive associations with resilience.

Chmitorz et al. (2018) reported that the BRS was positively correlated with social support as measured by the Oslo Social Support Scale-3 (OSS-3) in a German population sample (r not reported directly but with significant latent correlations and AVE > squared correlations meeting discriminant validity criteria). Smith et al. (2008) found BRS positively correlated with social support using the Interpersonal Support Evaluation List (ISEL). Acoba (2024) showed family support r = 0.25 and significant other support r = 0.27 with positive affect (a resilience-related outcome), though the direct BRS-social support link was not tested.

From the Ye et al. (2022) study, the CD-RISC captures resource-based resilience which explicitly includes items about close relationships and knowing where to turn for help — making social interaction a component of resilience itself. Social interaction provides emotional regulation support, practical assistance, and belongingness, all of which bolster an individual's capacity to bounce back from adversity.
