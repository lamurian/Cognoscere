Black willow (Salix nigra) is the only commercially important willow in North America. Its wood is one of the lightest North American hardwoods. Dried weight is 27 lbs/cu ft, specific gravity 0.36 [@krinard1985]. This makes it comparable to cottonwood, basswood, and aspen [@cassens2007].

The mechanical properties are poor for structural use. Modulus of Rupture is 7,800 psi dry and crushing strength parallel to grain is 4,100 psi dry. Both are very low among commercial hardwoods. However, shock resistance is moderately high and the wood does not split easily due to interlocked grain [@virginiatechdepartmentofwoodscience2000]. Shrinkage during seasoning is moderately high (8.7% tangential, 3.3% radial) but the wood has excellent stability once seasoned [@krinard1985].

Willow is unsuitable for structural load-bearing applications. Primary uses are factory lumber, veneer, boxes, crates, caskets, and furniture core stock [@cassens2007]. Purdue Extension states willow is "not suitable where strength performance is important."
