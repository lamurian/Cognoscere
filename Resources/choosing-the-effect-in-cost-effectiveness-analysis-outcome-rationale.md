The choice of outcome measure (the 'effect') in a cost-effectiveness analysis fundamentally shapes whether an intervention appears cost-effective. In workplace resilience interventions, four categories of outcomes dominate [@Hollaar2025Resilience; @Le2021Costeffectiveness].

**Clinical/health outcomes.** QALYs and DALYs are preferred by health technology agencies because they enable cross-disease comparison. Resilience interventions that reduce depression or anxiety incidence can be modelled into QALY gains. The Le et al. (2021) review found most workplace interventions reporting QALY-based ICERs fell below the $50,000/QALY threshold, meaning they are considered cost-effective. However, QALYs may miss broader wellbeing improvements that do not reach clinical thresholds [@Le2021Costeffectiveness].

**Productivity outcomes.** From an employer perspective, absenteeism (days off work) and presenteeism (reduced productivity while at work) are the most directly relevant outcomes. The human capital approach values lost workdays at the employee's daily wage. Presenteeism is harder to measure and value but accounts for a larger proportion of productivity loss than absenteeism in mental health contexts. Some ROI studies of workplace resilience programmes report savings of $1.50–$7 per dollar invested through reduced presenteeism alone [@Le2021Costeffectiveness; @Lensberg2013Challenges].

**Organisational outcomes.** Turnover reduction, workers' compensation claims, and disability costs are also used as effect measures. These are appealing to employers because they translate directly to the bottom line, but they may underestimate broader societal benefits of improved employee wellbeing [@Hollaar2025Resilience].

**Resilience-specific scales.** The Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC) and Brief Resilience Scale (BRS) are commonly used. However, because these instruments are trait-oriented and lack a clear link to monetisable outcomes, they are rarely used in formal CEAs—most economic evaluations of resilience interventions convert resilience gains into clinical or productivity proxies rather than using resilience scores as the primary effect measure [@Hollaar2025Resilience].
