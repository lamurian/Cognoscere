Living wood resists decay through compartmentalization, where sapwood actively defends against rot, contrasting with dead wood which decays from the outside in [@wikipediacontributors2025a]. Inosculated joints in living structures are stronger than manufactured joinery because the wood grain flows continuously through connections [@mylo2023; @wikipediacontributors2025a]. For decay prevention in any wooden structure, the most critical factors are design (adequate roof overhangs, ground clearance, ventilation) and regular maintenance (sealant reapplication every 2–3 years, inspection for moisture traps and fungal growth) [@krinard1985; @wooddatabase2010]. Preservative treatment is essential for ground-contact willow, while crape myrtle's moderate durability allows above-ground use with less treatment [@krinard1985; @wooddatabase2010].
