Southeast Asian solarpunk is developing a distinct identity rooted in the region's existing cultural and ecological systems, rather than importing Western frameworks wholesale. The movement is expressed through 'Seapunk' (SEA + punk - a roaming research-and-imagination collective) and platforms like MekarBooks and Glimmr Tales.

What makes SE Asian solarpunk distinct:
Western solarpunk often envisions futures built from scratch. Southeast Asia already has deep traditions that embody solarpunk principles:
- Gotong royong - collective cooperation as a cultural norm, not a radical proposal
- Subak - Balinese water temple irrigation systems that have managed communal rice farming for over a millennium, integrating spiritual, ecological, and agricultural governance
- Kampung culture - informal ingenuity, making things work with available materials
- Archipelago ecology - 17,000+ islands, each a distinct biome with unique relationships to the sea

Seapunk Studios:
Founded as an open, roaming research-and-imagination collective, Seapunk seeks fresh solarpunk-inspired ideas for Southeast Asia and the Global South, inspired by the region's historical open common sea and the emergent thriving 'punk' worlds it fostered. The initiative is led by Miguel Lopes Jeronimo and partners at EnergyLab Asia, Sambor Village, and Micro Galleries.

Cultural production:
- MekarBooks' Glimmr Tales imprint publishes solarpunk fiction written in English by Indonesian authors, rooted in the specific textures of Indonesia
- ASEF Culture360 launched a Southeast Asian Solarpunk Art Project digital art exhibition (2025) calling for digital artworks envisioning sustainable, hopeful futures rooted in local culture and community resilience
- The Phnom Penh Post (2025) reported on the SE Asian solarpunk project opening calls for artists, emphasising optimism, local resilience, and cultural specificity

Decolonial angle:
SE Asian solarpunk functions as a decolonial alternative to Western science fiction. Rather than inventing community from scratch, it looks at what already exists - wisdom traditions, communal systems, ecological knowledge - and asks: 'What if we kept those, and built forward from there?' This challenges the dominance of Western futuristic imaginaries.
