Willow and crape myrtle serve different roles in living fences. Willow excels in structural weaving, rapid establishment, and ease of propagation, making it the traditional choice for complex woven patterns like diamond lattice fedges, archways, and domes. Crape myrtle offers superior aesthetic value with long-lasting summer blooms and ornamental bark, but requires more time to establish and is less flexible for intricate weaving.

Willow produces long flexible rods weavable into tight patterns in a single season, while crape myrtle branches become woody with age requiring multi-year training [@albert2024; @keiren2023; @moore2006]. Willow needs 2-3 annual pruning sessions; crape myrtle requires moderate pruning plus pest management. Willow roots are aggressive within 33 feet of structures while crape myrtle roots are less invasive [@albert2024; @penn2024].

In terms of specific characteristics, willow grows at 6 ft/year and roots easily from cuttings while crape myrtle grows at a moderate pace and needs rooting hormone for propagation. Willow shows low drought tolerance while crape myrtle is highly drought tolerant once established [@moore2006]. Willow has many US native species while crape myrtle is non-native from China and Korea [@cityofroundrock2025]. Willow weaves into complex forms (diamond lattice, arches, domes) while crape myrtle is limited to trunk training over frames. Crape myrtle produces showy flowers for 100+ days while willow only offers catkins. Pest resistance is better in willow; crape myrtle suffers from powdery mildew, aphids, and scale [@moore2006]. Willow lifespan is 30-50 years [@greayer2024]; crape myrtle is 20-50 years.

Key sources: @keiren2023, @albert2024, @moore2006, @greayer2024, @penn2024, @cityofroundrock2025.
