## Summary

Approaches to integrated sustainable farming emphasizing closed-loop ecosystems and biodiversity draw from biomimicry and permaculture. Biomimicry emulates nature's closed-loop cycles where waste becomes food and diversity is prioritized [@verbrugghe2023; @pedersenzari2020]. A practical example is the 1m² urban homestead combining Moringa or Gliricidia as living trellises, nitrogen-fixing beans, and trailing strawberries, creating a high-biodiversity polyculture [@pedersenzari2020; @borham2024]. Nutrient and water loops are closed through vermicomposting, rain barrels, and capacitive soil moisture sensors [@tao2024].

In semi-arid West Timor, living fences of Gliricidia sepium and Leucaena leucocephala provide erosion control, fodder, and wildlife corridors [@matheus2009; @ngongo2022; @reville2017]. Traditional Indonesian pekarangan home gardens and Subak water governance offer culturally rooted models for biodiversity and collaborative water management. Multi-layer food forests, green roofs, and earth-sheltered designs extend these principles to landscape scale, mimicking forest ecosystems with canopy, understory, and ground cover layers [@jideofor2012; @voiceofnature2022].

Key approaches: biomimicry and permaculture underpin closed-loop farming [@verbrugghe2023; @pedersenzari2020]; the 1m² urban homestead shows high-biodiversity polyculture in minimal space [@pedersenzari2020; @borham2024]; vermicomposting and smart irrigation close nutrient and water loops [@tao2024]; living fences in West Timor integrate erosion control, fodder, and conservation [@matheus2009; @ngongo2022; @reville2017]; traditional pekarangan and Subak systems model biodiversity and collaborative governance; food forests, green roofs, and earth-sheltered buildings scale closed-loop design to the landscape [@jideofor2012; @voiceofnature2022].
