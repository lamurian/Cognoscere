Reliable comparison of health indicators across countries requires common indicator definitions, similar data sources, and standardized data collection methods. A study comparing obesity, hypertension, diabetes, and asthma prevalence across international databases found that estimates for the same country differed substantially depending on whether data came from self-reported surveys, measured health examinations, or administrative registers [@tolonen2021].

Diseases requiring regular healthcare contact (e.g., diabetes) showed higher comparability across data sources than asymptomatic conditions (e.g., hypertension). Even within EU member states using standardized surveys, prevalence estimates for obesity varied by up to 10 percentage points depending on the database used, due to differences in indicator definitions and data collection periods [@tolonen2021].

For cross-country comparability, a national health index should: (1) use indicators from standardised international sources (WHO, OECD) where definitions are harmonised, (2) apply consistent age-standardization, (3) anchor to a common reference year, (4) use transparent normalization methods, and (5) document all methodological choices for reproducibility. The UK Health Index approach of anchoring to a baseline year addresses some comparability issues but was designed for subnational comparison within England [@frenisterrantino2022].
