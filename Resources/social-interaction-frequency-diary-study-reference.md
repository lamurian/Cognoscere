Wheeler & Nezlek (1977) [@wheeler1977] conducted the foundational Rochester Interaction Record study, finding that college students averaged approximately 6.7 interactions per day (range: 0-20+, median ~5-7). Each interaction lasted about 25 minutes on average (median ~15-20). Later replication by O'Connor & Rosenblood (1996) [@oconnor1996] using experience sampling found similar rates: ~5-8 interactions/day for students and ~3-6 for community adults.

Interaction frequency varies by: (1) age — younger adults have more daily interactions (~6-8) than older adults (~3-5); (2) employment — employed adults have more work-related interactions; (3) living situation — cohabiting/ partnered individuals have fewer but longer interactions. About 50-60% of interactions are dyadic, 20-30% involve 3+ people.

For simulation: general population mean ~3-6 substantive social interactions per day. The distribution is right-skewed (most people have moderate frequency, a long tail of highly sociable individuals). Weekend vs weekday differences are modest (~10-20% higher on weekends). The coefficient of variation across days is ~0.5-0.7 for the same individual.

Alternative reference: Kahneman et al. (2004) [@kahneman2004] Day Reconstruction Method found employed women averaged ~14-16 social episodes per day including brief encounters. Setting the threshold for substantive interaction matters.

For simulation: subevents_per_day ~4-6 is defensible for substantive interactions; ~2-3 for confidant-quality interactions.

## Sources
- [@wheeler1977] Wheeler, L., & Nezlek, J. (1977). Sex differences in social participation. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 35(10), 742-754.
- [@oconnor1996] O'Connor, S. C., & Rosenblood, L. K. (1996). Affiliation motivation in everyday experience: A theoretical comparison. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 70(3), 513-522.
- [@kahneman2004] Kahneman, D., Krueger, A. B., Schkade, D. A., Schwarz, N., & Stone, A. A. (2004). A survey method for characterizing daily life experience: The Day Reconstruction Method. Science, 306(5702), 1776-1780.
