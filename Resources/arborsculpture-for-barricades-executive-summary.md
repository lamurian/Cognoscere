This research examined practical arborsculpture techniques for constructing living barricades and walls. Five core techniques identified: inosculation (tree joining through natural grafting), pleaching (interweaving branches), willow dome construction (community-built living rods), Wiechula's V-cut bending, and living root bridge cultivation. Each has different timeframes (2-3 years for willow fedges to 15+ years for root bridges). Confidence is High for inosculation, pleaching, and Baubotanik (peer-reviewed studies); Moderate for willow domes; Low-Moderate for Wiechula's method. Ecological benefits include carbon sequestration, microclimate regulation, erosion control, and wildlife habitat. Living wood self-repairs and self-strengthens through compartmentalization. Practical methods range from quick willow fedges (2-3 years, 45-degree diamond lattice) to load-bearing inosculation structures and 600-year root bridges spanning 30m. Species selection depends on climate, soil moisture, and speed requirements -- willow provides fastest results but needs 30-40ft setback from structures.
