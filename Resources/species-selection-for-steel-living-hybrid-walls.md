## Summary

Species selection for steel skeleton + living plant infill walls depends on the balance of speed vs longevity vs structural properties. Three primary candidates cover different use cases.

**Willow (Salix spp.)** — fastest establishment: 1m/year growth, rods flexible enough for weaving through steel stud cavities. Reaches full wall density in 2-3 years. However, individual willow stems live 10-30 years; periodic renewal planting required. Best for quick coverage where long-term replacement is acceptable. Excels at woven diamond lattice infill [@morningchores2025].

**Crape myrtle (Lagerstroemia spp.)** — slower establishment than willow but produces denser, more durable wood with ornamental flowers. Smooth peeling bark and strong stems make it suitable for pleached and inosculated infill. Better for permanent installations where decorative quality matters. Grows 0.5-1m/year depending on cultivar [@reville2017].

**London plane tree (Platanus hispanica)** — the Baubotanik standard for tall living walls. Reaches 30m mature height, tolerates urban pollution, compacted soil, and aggressive pruning. Ideal for 20m structures due to height capacity. Slower to establish (15-30 years to full maturity) but provides centuries-long structural presence [@mylo2023]. Best suited as inosculated panel infill in large steel frames.

A layered approach may be optimal: fast willow for immediate coverage while slower plane trees mature in the same wall cavities.
