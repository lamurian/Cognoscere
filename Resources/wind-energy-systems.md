Wind energy converts kinetic energy from moving air into electricity using wind turbines. In 2025, wind supplied about 2,700 TWh of electricity, over 8% of world electricity. Global installed capacity exceeded 800 GW.

## Turbine Technology

**Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines (HAWT)**: The dominant design -- upwind rotor with three blades, attached to a nacelle atop a tall tubular tower. Modern turbines have rotor diameters exceeding 120m for onshore and 220m for offshore.

**Betz Limit**: No wind turbine can capture more than 59.3% (16/27) of the kinetic energy in wind. Modern turbines approach 70-80% of this theoretical limit.

**Power Equation**: Available power is proportional to the cube of wind speed (P proportional to v cubed). Doubling wind speed increases power eightfold. Turbines typically start generating at 3-5 m/s (cut-in) and reach rated power at 12-14 m/s.

## Onshore vs Offshore

| Parameter | Onshore | Offshore |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Capacity Factor | 25-40% | 35-55% |
| LCOE | $26-50/MWh | $60-100/MWh |
| Visual Impact | Higher | Lower |
| Construction Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Maintenance | Easier | More complex |

Offshore wind benefits from stronger, more consistent winds and less turbulence. Floating wind turbines open up deeper waters (over 60m depth) and are a growing technology, particularly in Europe and Asia.

## Capacity Factor Optimisation

Capacity factor depends on: (1) average wind speed at hub height, (2) turbine reliability, (3) grid curtailment losses. Onshore capacity factors range 25-44%, with best sites exceeding 50%. Optimal siting uses wind resource assessment tools like the Global Wind Atlas from the Technical University of Denmark.

## Grid Integration and Variability

Wind generation is variable and requires: (1) energy storage for balancing, (2) demand-side management, (3) interconnection with other regions via HVDC. Wind and solar are complementary in many regions -- wind often generates more at night and in winter, while solar peaks during daytime and summer.

## Costs

Onshore wind is one of the cheapest electricity sources. Lazard estimates unsubsidized LCOE at $26-50/MWh (2021). New onshore wind is cheaper than new coal or gas plants in many locations. Costs continue to decline due to larger turbines, longer blades, and improved manufacturing.
