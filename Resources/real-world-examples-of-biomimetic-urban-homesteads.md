## Summary

The Eastgate Centre (Harare, 1996) pioneered termite-mound passive ventilation, reducing HVAC energy by 65% using clay brick thermal mass, stack-effect chimneys, and night cooling [@verbrugghe2023]. This principle scales to residential homesteads: a solar chimney, exposed thermal mass, and cross-ventilation maintain comfort without mechanical systems. Council House 2 (Melbourne, 2006) extended this with tree-inspired design: green façades filter air, phase-change materials store thermal energy, and a regenerative approach treats the building as part of the urban ecosystem [@verbrugghe2023].

The Sahara Forest Project (Qatar, Jordan, Tunisia) demonstrates closed-loop off-grid principles at community scale. Inspired by the Namibian desert beetle, its saltwater-cooled greenhouses produce fresh water from condensation, grow food year-round, generate energy from concentrated solar power, and regenerate desertified land — all with zero fossil fuel inputs [@verbrugghe2023]. Cucumbers grew using half the water of conventional agriculture. The French Biomim'City Lab documents 16 urban projects (2007-2019) including Alguesens (algae biofacade for thermal regulation), Quartier de Gally (forest-model neighbourhood with circular water cycles), and Nianing Church (termite-mound passive ventilation) [@blanco2021].

## Key Points
- Eastgate: 65% HVAC reduction via termite-mound ventilation — scales to residential
- Sahara Forest Project: most complete closed-loop energy-water-food system
- Council House 2: tree-inspired regenerative design
- 16 French projects documented; 44% built and operational [@blanco2021]
