This research investigated the structural integrity, longevity, and maintenance requirements of willow and crape myrtle wood for building structures and fences. The findings divide sharply by species.

Willow is structurally inadequate for load-bearing timber applications. Black willow is one of the lightest North American hardwoods (27 lbs/cu ft), very weak in bending and crushing, and has no natural decay resistance [@krinard1985; @cassens2007]. It requires full preservative treatment for any outdoor use and remains unsuitable where strength matters. Willow's value lies in living fence applications, not as harvested timber for structural posts.

Crape myrtle (Lagerstroemia spp.) is a moderate-density hardwood with significantly better mechanical properties — Janka 1,090 lbf, MOR 14,120 psi — and moderately durable decay resistance [@wooddatabase2010]. It supports outdoor furniture and above-ground structures. However, the common ornamental crape myrtle in North America yields only small-diameter lumber, limiting structural post applications.

For fence posts and building structures in ground contact, neither species is ideal. Naturally durable woods like black locust, cedar, or pressure-treated southern pine outperform both. Willow's best role is living fences where the tree remains alive. Crape myrtle serves well for ornamental outdoor furniture and decorative structures with regular maintenance.

Confidence: Willow's poor structural properties and decay resistance — High (3 authoritative USDA/Purdue/VT sources). Crape myrtle's mechanical properties and moderate durability — Moderate (1 authoritative wood database source, limited peer-reviewed research on L. indica specifically).
