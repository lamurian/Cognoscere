## Summary

Comparing Indonesia's health workforce density against similar middle-income countries and high-performing health systems reveals the scale of its challenge and highlights alternative approaches.

**ASEAN peer comparison**: Indonesia's doctor density of approximately 0.62 per 1,000 population [@muharram2024] is among the lowest in Southeast Asia. WHO data shows Malaysia at 2.1 doctors per 1,000, Thailand at 0.8, Vietnam at 0.8, the Philippines at 0.8, and Singapore at 2.5. The OECD average is 3.9 per 1,000 [@oecd2025]. Indonesia's density is comparable to Laos (0.5) and Myanmar (0.6), well below its economic peers. Indonesia's total health worker density (3.84 per 1,000 including nurses and midwives) also falls below the WHO threshold of 4.45 needed for 80% UHC coverage [@muharram2024].

**Nordic benchmark countries**: Finland has 3.3 practising doctors per 1,000 population, below the OECD average of 3.9 but with a much higher nurse density (14.3 per 1,000 vs OECD average 8.9) and a nurse-to-doctor ratio of 4.3, indicating a task-shifted model where nurses handle a larger share of primary care [@schneider2022a]. Sweden has 4.5 doctors per 1,000 and Denmark approximately 4.0. These countries achieve excellent health outcomes with doctor densities 5-7 times higher than Indonesia's, suggesting that Indonesia needs a substantial absolute increase while also optimizing skill mix.

**Key structural differences**: Nordic systems invest heavily in primary care and community health workers, with strong gatekeeping functions and team-based care. Finland's health workforce is 16.2% of total employment (double the EU average), reflecting high investment in health and social care. By contrast, Indonesia underinvests in health workforce across the board — health spending is approximately 3% of GDP compared to 9-11% in Nordic countries.

**Implications**: Indonesia cannot simply copy higher-income country models. The absolute gap is too large to close by training alone within a reasonable timeframe without complementary strategies including task-shifting, expanded use of community health workers, telemedicine, and primary care strengthening.

## Key Points

- Indonesia's doctor density (0.62/1,000) is lowest among major ASEAN economies
- OECD average: 3.9/1,000; Finland: 3.3; Sweden: 4.5; Denmark: ~4.0
- Nordic systems achieve better outcomes through optimized skill mix (higher nurse-to-doctor ratios) and strong primary care
- Indonesia would need 5-7 times current doctors to match OECD levels
- Complementary strategies (task-shifting, PHC strengthening, telemedicine) are essential given the gap size
