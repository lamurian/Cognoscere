Education represents the third major frontier of Dunning-Kruger amplification in the LLM era. Fan et al. (2024) introduced the concept of **metacognitive laziness** — a state where learners offload cognitive effort to generative AI, reducing their motivation to engage in deep processing. Their study found that while GenAI can improve short-term performance, it undermines learning processes and long-term skill development [@fan2024].

Naqvi et al. (2025) extend this to health sciences education specifically, warning that the ability to critically evaluate AI-generated content requires the very skills that overreliance on AI erodes. They document that dental university instructors often mistook AI-generated reflections for student work, highlighting how even experts struggle to detect AI output [@naqvi2025].

The metacognitive demands identified by Tankelevitch et al. (2024) — prompting, evaluating, relying on outputs, and optimizing workflows — are particularly challenging for students who have not yet developed strong domain knowledge. Without the foundational understanding needed to evaluate AI outputs, students are in the classic Dunning-Kruger position: they lack the metacognitive awareness to recognize when AI-generated content is wrong [@tankelevitch2024].
