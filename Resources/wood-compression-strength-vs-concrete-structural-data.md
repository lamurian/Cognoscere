Wood in compression parallel to grain achieves ~80 MPa characteristic strength, while standard concrete ranges from 17-40 MPa [@swedishwood2022]. A living tree trunk of sufficient diameter bears greater compressive loads than a concrete pillar of equivalent cross-section.

The comparison is nuanced. Concrete is isotropic (uniform in all directions) and can be steel-reinforced for tension. Wood is anisotropic: strong along grain but weaker perpendicular. Concrete has higher modulus of elasticity (25-30 GPa vs 8-15 GPa for wood), deforming less under equal loads.

Living trees have advantages over sawn timber. Reaction wood forms in response to mechanical stress, increasing local density. Inosculated joints create continuous grain flow eliminating weak points [@mylo2023]. The root-soil system provides massive distributed foundation anchorage. For barriers, wood's fibrous structure absorbs impact energy through plastic deformation without catastrophic failure -- concrete spalls and cracks.

## Key Points
- Wood compression parallel to grain: ~80 MPa. Concrete: 17-40 MPa.
- Concrete modulus: 25-30 GPa. Wood: 8-15 GPa.
- Living wood absorbs impact through fibrous deformation; concrete spalls.
- Inosculated joints eliminate mechanical fastener weakness.
