## Source

**Title:** A Validation Study of the Web Screening Questionnaire (WSQ) Compared With the Mini-International Neuropsychiatric Interview-Plus (MINI-Plus)

**Authors:** Meuldijk D, Giltay EJ, Carlier IV, van Vliet IM, van Hemert AM, Zitman FG

**Journal:** JMIR Mental Health, 4(3), e35 (2017)

**DOI:** 10.2196/mental.5453

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5596296/

**Citation:** @meuldijk2017

## Summary

This study validated the Web Screening Questionnaire (WSQ), a 15-item self-report instrument designed to screen for common psychiatric disorders including depressive disorders, anxiety disorders (panic disorder, agoraphobia, social phobia, specific phobia, generalized anxiety disorder, post-traumatic stress disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder), and alcohol abuse or dependence. The WSQ was compared against the Mini-International Neuropsychiatric Interview-Plus (MINI-Plus) as the gold standard diagnostic interview in a combined sample of 1,292 adults (1,117 from the general population recruited via primary care, and 175 psychiatric outpatients) in the Netherlands.

The WSQ demonstrated high specificity (0.89–0.97) for most disorders, meaning it accurately identifies individuals without the disorder. However, specificity was lower for post-traumatic stress disorder (0.52) and specific phobia (0.73). Sensitivity ranged from 0.67 to 1.00 for most disorders but was notably lower for depressive disorder (0.56) and alcohol abuse or dependence (0.56). Negative predictive values were very high (≥0.97) due to low disorder prevalence in the general population, while positive predictive values were poor (0.02–0.33). Area under the curve (AUC) values indicated good to excellent discriminative ability for panic disorder (0.98), panic disorder with agoraphobia (0.98), and depressive disorder (0.83), but were lower for other conditions.

The authors conclude that the WSQ is a useful screening tool to rule out depressive and anxiety disorders (high NPV), but a positive screen requires follow-up diagnostic assessment due to low PPV. The WSQ's brevity (15 items, <5 minutes) makes it practical for routine clinical screening, particularly in primary care settings.

## Key Points

- **Sample:** 1,292 adults (18–65 years) from Dutch primary care (general population) and psychiatric outpatient clinics
- **Reference standard:** MINI-Plus diagnostic interview (1-month prevalence, DSM-IV-TR)
- **WSQ characteristics:** 15 items, self-report, <5 minutes completion, web-based
- **Specificity range:** 0.89–0.97 for most disorders; lower for PTSD (0.52) and specific phobia (0.73)
- **Sensitivity range:** 0.67–1.00 for most disorders; lower for depressive disorder (0.56) and alcohol abuse/dependence (0.56)
- **AUC values:** Excellent for panic disorder (0.98), panic disorder with agoraphobia (0.98); good for depressive disorder (0.83)
- **Predictive values:** NPV ≥0.97 across disorders; PPV poor (0.02–0.33) due to low prevalence
- **Clinical utility:** Strong rule-out tool (high NPV); positive screens require confirmatory diagnostic interview
- **Limitations:** Low sensitivity for depression and alcohol disorders; PTSD and specific phobia poorly detected; general population prevalence limits PPV

## Relevance

This study provides evidence for the WSQ as a practical, brief screening instrument suitable for primary care and mental health triage settings where ruling out common psychiatric disorders is the priority. The high NPV supports its use as a "rule-out" tool, reducing unnecessary referrals for diagnostic interviews. However, the low sensitivity for depression and alcohol disorders, and poor detection of PTSD and specific phobia, mean it should not be used as a standalone diagnostic tool. The findings are relevant for clinicians implementing stepped-care models, digital mental health screening programs, and researchers evaluating brief screening instruments against structured diagnostic interviews. The Dutch primary care context (near-universal GP registration) supports generalizability to similar healthcare systems.
