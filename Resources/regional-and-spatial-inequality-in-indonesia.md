Indonesia's archipelagic geography creates severe spatial inequality. Urban Gini (0.395) far exceeds rural (0.299) [@unnadhiro2025]. While Java has higher measured income inequality, non-Java and rural areas show higher physical asset inequality [@lpemfebui2024]. Eastern Indonesia lags significantly in infrastructure.

Labor markets diverge sharply: Java unemployment is 7.5% vs 5.2% in non-Java, but non-Java employment concentrates in lower-productivity agriculture [@lpemfebui2024]. Informal workers account for 64% of female employment vs 57% male. Highest regional Ginis: Jakarta (0.431), West Java and Yogyakarta (0.428), South Papua (0.424) [@abdurrahman2025].

Nickel downstreaming in Sulawesi and Maluku exemplifies failed spatial equity: US$387M in environmental losses while profits flow to politically-connected companies, and emissions could cause 3,800 deaths [@askar2024].
