## Poverty and Inequality: Soeharto vs Prabowo Indonesia

### Soeharto Era (1966-1998)

Poverty fell dramatically under Soeharto. Official poverty rates dropped from approximately 45% in 1970 to around 11% by 1996. However, this claim is debated -- some studies suggest poverty was much higher than claimed, with as many as 50% of Indonesians living on US$1 a day or less in PPP terms even in the 1990s. The Gini coefficient remained relatively low during most of the New Order (estimated ~0.31-0.34), reflecting relatively low measured inequality. Income inequality existed but was masked by overall growth. The 1997 crisis reversed some poverty gains -- poverty spiked to ~24% in 1998.

### Prabowo Era (2024-present)

The poverty rate stands at 8.25% (national poverty line, September 2025) and 1.6% in multidimensional poverty (2024). The Gini coefficient is 34.4 (2025), similar to Soeharto-era levels. However, 65% of Indonesians live on less than US$8.30/day, indicating a large vulnerable middle class just above the poverty line. Inequality has risen since the post-Soeharto reform era, particularly between Java and outer islands.

### Key Differences

- **Absolute poverty**: Much lower today (~8%) than at the start of Soeharto's rule (~45%) -- a clear long-term improvement.
- **Vulnerability**: A larger share of the population is just above the poverty line today, creating a 'vulnerable middle class' that didn't exist under Soeharto.
- **Inequality trend**: Soeharto's inequality was low but underestimated due to elite capture. Today's Gini is similar but wealth concentration is arguably more visible.
- **Regional inequality**: Soeharto's centralized model concentrated wealth in Jakarta. Post-decentralization, inequality between Java and other islands persists.

### Similarities

- Gini coefficients are comparable (~0.31-0.34), suggesting structural inequality has been persistent across both eras.
- Both regimes face(d) criticism that official poverty statistics understate true deprivation.
- Both eras saw/will see poverty reduction as a key government claim to legitimacy.
- Regional economic disparities between Java and outer islands remained a challenge for both.
