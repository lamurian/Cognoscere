## Summary

Health systems that prioritize specialized care over primary care infrastructure produce several measurable societal harms. The US exemplifies these consequences: it has the highest health spending globally (20% of GDP) yet ranks lowest among peer countries on life expectancy, health equity, and access [@phillips2022].

**Higher costs without better outcomes**: Primary care accounts for 35% of healthcare visits but receives only 5 cents of every healthcare dollar in the US [@phillips2022]. The imbalance between spending on specialist care (which tends to be high-cost, high-technology, acute intervention) versus primary care (which emphasizes prevention, chronic disease management, and coordination) drives overall system costs upward without commensurate health gains [@krist2025]. The NASEM report found that countries with higher primary care investment achieve better outcomes at lower per capita costs [@krist2025].

**Workforce erosion**: The widening income gap between primary care physicians and specialists discourages medical students from choosing primary care careers. With the largest specialist-generalist wage gap among high-income countries and the highest medical tuition fees, fewer US physicians enter primary care, and those who do often leave due to burnout [@fitzgerald2022; @gumas2024]. This creates a negative spiral: fewer primary care providers leads to worse access, driving more utilization of expensive specialist and emergency care.

**Worse health equity**: Primary care disproportionately benefits disadvantaged populations who face the greatest barriers to accessing care. Systems lacking strong primary care infrastructure show wider disparities in health outcomes across income, racial, and geographic groups [@krist2025]. The US has 7,700+ primary care health professional shortage areas, accounting for 40% of counties, further entrenching inequities [@krist2025].

**Fragmented care**: Specialist-oriented systems produce fragmented care with poor communication between providers. Only 40% of US physicians report receiving information from specialists about changes to patient care plans, leading to duplicative testing, medication errors, and poor chronic disease management [@gumas2024].

## Key Points

- Specialist prioritization drives higher costs without proportional health gains
- The specialist-generalist wage gap undermines primary care workforce supply
- 40% of US counties are primary care shortage areas, worsening health inequity
- Fragmented specialist care produces duplicative testing, errors, and worse outcomes
- Robust primary care systems achieve better equity and cost efficiency
