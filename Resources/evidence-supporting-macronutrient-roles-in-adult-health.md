## Summary

Macronutrients serve three fundamental roles in adult health: providing energy, supplying essential structural components, and enabling metabolic regulation. Carbohydrates are the primary energy source for the brain, which consumes approximately 130 g of glucose daily—this is the basis for the carbohydrate RDA [@instituteofmedicineuscommitteeonuseofdietaryreferenceintakesinnutritionlabeling2003]. Dietary fibre, a carbohydrate subclass, supports digestive health and is associated with reduced cardiovascular disease risk.

Protein provides essential amino acids that the body cannot synthesise. The RDA of 0.8 g/kg body weight is derived from nitrogen balance studies and meta-analyses [@nationalacademiesofsciences2024]. Beyond quantity, protein quality matters—animal sources provide complete amino acid profiles while most plant proteins are incomplete. Dietary fats supply essential fatty acids (linoleic acid and alpha-linolenic acid) that are precursors for eicosanoids regulating inflammation, blood pressure, and immune function. Fats also enable absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).

The AMDR framework was designed precisely because macronutrients influence both nutrient adequacy and chronic disease risk. Diets very low or very high in any macronutrient increase risk of either insufficiency or chronic disease. For example, very low carbohydrate intake may cause ketosis and micronutrient inadequacy, while excessive saturated fat intake increases cardiovascular risk [@nationalacademiesofsciences2024].
