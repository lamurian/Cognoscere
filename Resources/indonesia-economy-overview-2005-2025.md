## Overview

Indonesia is the largest economy in Southeast Asia and the 10th largest economy in the world by GDP (PPP). Over the past two decades (2005–2025), the country has undergone a remarkable economic transformation, rising from a lower-middle-income country to an upper-middle-income country, with GDP per capita increasing from ~$1,200 to nearly $5,000.

## GDP Growth (Annual %)

| Period | Average Growth | Context |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| 2005–2009 | ~5.5% | Post-Asian Financial Crisis recovery; resilient through Global Financial Crisis |
| 2010–2014 | ~5.8% | Commodity super-cycle; peak growth of 6.2% in 2010 |
| 2015–2019 | ~5.0% | Moderating commodity prices; infrastructure push under Jokowi |
| 2020 | -2.1% | COVID-19 pandemic contraction (mild relative to peers) |
| 2021–2024 | ~5.0% | Steady recovery; post-pandemic normalization |
| 2025–2026 (proj.) | ~5.0–5.1% | IMF projects steady growth despite challenging external environment[^1] |

**Key observations:**
- Indonesia maintained remarkably stable ~5% average growth for two decades, one of the most consistent performances among emerging economies.
- The 2020 COVID contraction (-2.1%) was modest compared to many peers, thanks to limited lockdowns and commodity exports.
- Unlike many emerging markets, Indonesia avoided a "lost decade" after the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis and re-established stable growth by the early 2000s.

## GDP Size (Current US$)

| Year | GDP (trillions) | Milestone |
|------|----------------|-----------|
| 2005 | $0.29 T | — |
| 2010 | $0.76 T | Entered top 20 economies |
| 2017 | $1.02 T | Crossed $1 trillion threshold |
| 2024 | $1.40 T | 10th largest economy in PPP terms |

Indonesia's economy grew nearly 5x in nominal US dollar terms from 2005 to 2024.[^2]

## GDP Per Capita

GDP per capita rose from **$1,238 (2005)** to **$4,925 (2024)** — a 4x increase.[^2] GNI per capita followed a similar trajectory:

- 2004: $1,060 (lower-middle income)
- 2019: $4,020 (upper-middle income threshold crossed ~2019)
- 2024: $4,910

Indonesia transitioned from lower-middle-income to upper-middle-income status during this period, though it faces the risk of the **"middle-income trap"** without further structural reforms.[^3]

## Structural Transformation

The economy shifted from agriculture toward manufacturing and services:

- **Agriculture** declined from ~15% of GDP (2000) to ~12% (2024)
- **Manufacturing** peaked around 2000s (~29% of GDP) but declined to ~19% by 2024 — concerning signs of premature deindustrialization.
- **Services** grew steadily, now ~44% of GDP.
- **Commodities** (coal, palm oil, nickel) remain significant export earners, making the economy sensitive to global price cycles.[^4]

A key concern is the stagnation of manufacturing value-added share, which fell from ~29% (2001) to ~19% (2024), suggesting Indonesia may be struggling with the "middle-income trap" where it competes less on low-cost labor but has not yet moved up the technology ladder.[^5]

## Key Drivers & Policies

1. **Commodity Boom (2005–2014)**: Coal, palm oil, rubber, and mineral exports surged, fueling growth and fiscal revenues.
2. **Infrastructure Push (2015–2019)**: President Jokowi's administration prioritized roads, ports, railways, and airports — infrastructure spending increased from <2% to ~5% of GDP.
3. **Omnibus Law (2020)**: The Job Creation Law (UU Cipta Kerja) aimed to cut red tape, attract foreign investment, and simplify labor regulations.
4. **Nickel Downstreaming (2020–present)**: Ban on raw nickel ore exports spurred domestic processing plants (smelters), boosting manufacturing value in the battery EV supply chain.
5. **Digital Economy**: Home to "decacorns" like GoTo (Gojek + Tokopedia), Indonesia's digital economy was valued at ~$90B in 2024.[^6]

## Inflation

Inflation has been well-anchored since Bank Indonesia adopted inflation targeting in 2005:

| Period | Avg Inflation |
|--------|-------------|
| 2005–2008 | ~10% (fuel subsidy cuts caused spikes) |
| 2009–2014 | ~5–6% |
| 2015–2019 | ~3–4% |
| 2020–2024 | ~2–4% (post-COVID spike mild, peaked ~5.5%) |

Bank Indonesia's core inflation target of 2.5% ±1% has been largely maintained since 2015.[^4]

## Poverty & Inequality

- **Poverty rate** (national poverty line): fell from ~16% (2005) to ~9% (2024).
- **Extreme poverty** (World Bank $2.15/day): declined from ~43% (1999) to ~2% (2024).
- **Gini coefficient**: improved from ~0.41 (2011 peak) to ~0.38 (2024), though inequality remains moderate by regional standards.

## Challenges

1. **Middle-Income Trap**: Manufacturing share declining; R&D spending remains low (~0.3% of GDP).
2. **Commodity Dependence**: Exports still concentrated in coal, palm oil, and minerals.
3. **Infrastructure Gaps**: While improving, logistics costs remain high relative to peers.
4. **Human Capital**: PISA scores below regional averages; skilled labor shortage.
5. **Regulatory Uncertainty**: Despite Omnibus Law, implementation and legal challenges persist.
6. **Demographic Dividend Window**: The working-age population ratio is peaking; the window to capitalize is narrowing.
7. **Geopolitical Risks**: US-China tensions create both opportunities and risks for supply chain diversification.

## Recent Outlook (2025–2026)

According to the IMF's 2025 Article IV Consultation[^1]:
- Growth is projected at **5.0% (2025) and 5.1% (2026)**, driven by robust private consumption, sustained investment, and accommodative fiscal & monetary policies.
- Inflation is expected to stay within Bank Indonesia's target range (2.5% ±1%).
- The new Prabowo administration's fiscal policies (including the free meal program for schoolchildren) add both growth stimulus and fiscal risk.
- Medium-term challenges remain around raising tax revenue (tax-to-GDP ratio ~10%, among the lowest globally) and accelerating structural reforms.
