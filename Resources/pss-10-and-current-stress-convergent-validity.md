## Summary

The correlation between the PSS-10 (measuring chronic perceived stress over the past month) and current stress measures (such as DASS-21 stress subscale, single-item stress ratings, or momentary stress assessments) represents convergent validity between two stress measures. Reported values range from r = 0.40 to r = 0.65.

Schneider et al. @schneider2020 reported that PSS-10 total correlated with the BSI GSI (global symptom severity) at r = 0.49 in a clinical sample. Acoba @acoba2024 found PSS-10 correlated with DASS-21 anxiety at r = 0.454 and DASS-21 depression at r = 0.505. In the psychometric validation literature, the PSS-10 and DASS-21 stress subscale typically correlate at r ≈ 0.50-0.60, reflecting shared stress variance but distinct temporal frames and item content.

The moderate magnitude of this correlation (typically r = 0.40-0.60) supports treating PSS-10 and current stress as related but not interchangeable constructs. The asserted bound for avg_pss10 ↔ avg_stress (r > 0.05) is trivially low and would be passed by virtually any real data.

## Key Points
- PSS-10 and DASS-21 stress: r ≈ 0.40 to 0.60 (moderate convergent validity)
- The bound r > 0.05 in test #7 is statistically meaningless; any real correlation far exceeds this
- The late_corr > 0.05 in test #15 is similarly a trivial floor
