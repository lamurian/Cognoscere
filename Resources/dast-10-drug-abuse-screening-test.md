## Summary

The Drug Abuse Screening Test (DAST-10) is a 10-item self-report screening instrument designed to identify individuals who are using drugs (excluding alcohol) in problematic ways. Items assess drug use frequency, abuse, dependence symptoms, and drug-related consequences across the past 12 months. Each item is answered yes/no (scored 0/1), yielding a total score range of 0-10. The recommended cutoff for problematic drug use is >=3. Score interpretation: 1-2 low risk, 3-5 moderate, 6-8 substantial, 9-10 severe.

The original DAST (28-item version) was developed by Skinner (1982) and demonstrated strong internal consistency (alpha 0.92) and test-retest reliability (0.85) in a study of 213 substance abuse patients [@skinner1982]. The DAST-10 was developed as a shorter version to improve clinical utility while maintaining psychometric performance. Internal consistency for DAST-10 ranges from 0.86 to 0.94 across studies.

At cutoff >=3, the DAST-10 typically demonstrates sensitivity of 0.80-0.95 and specificity of 0.68-0.90 depending on the population and reference standard. In a validation study of the Chinese DAST-10 among pregnant women, using a cutoff >=2 gave AUC of 0.85 for identifying illicit drug use [@lam2015]. The DAST-20 and DAST-10 show comparable psychometric performance, though the DAST-10 has practical advantages in clinical settings. The DAST-10 correlates well with other substance use measures and effectively differentiates between substance use disorder patients and general populations.

## Key Points

- 10 yes/no items, total 0-10, past 12 months, cutoff >=3 for problematic use
- Original DAST (Skinner 1982): alpha 0.92, retest 0.85 [@skinner1982]
- DAST-10 internal consistency: 0.86-0.94
- Cutoff >=3: Se 0.80-0.95, Sp 0.68-0.90 across populations
- Brief screening tool; positive screens require comprehensive substance use assessment
- Designed to exclude alcohol (covered by AUDIT)
- Widely used in primary care, psychiatric, and addiction treatment settings
