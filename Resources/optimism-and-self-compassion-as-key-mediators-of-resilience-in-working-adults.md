Hannibal et al. [@hannibal2026] conducted a randomised controlled trial (N=352 employees) evaluating RESIST, a web- and app-based resilience intervention grounded in PASTOR theory and Strengths-Based CBT. The intervention targeted four resilience factors: self-efficacy, optimism, self-compassion, and perceived social support. Results showed significant stress reduction compared to waitlist controls (d=-0.54 post-intervention, d=-0.47 at 3-month follow-up), with improvements maintained at 6 months.

Mediation analyses revealed that optimism and self-compassion were the key mediators of the intervention's effect on both stress reduction and self-perceived resilience. Self-efficacy and social support did not show significant mediating effects. This suggests that developing a positive future outlook and a self-compassionate attitude toward oneself are the primary drivers of resilience enhancement in working populations.

The study has important methodological strengths: it was theory-driven (grounded in PASTOR), adequately powered, and included multiple follow-up points. Limitations include modest intervention adherence (average 2.2 out of 6 sessions completed) and the self-help format without human guidance. The findings suggest that resilience interventions should prioritise cultivating optimism and self-compassion over other psychosocial resources.
