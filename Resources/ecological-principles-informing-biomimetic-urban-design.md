## Summary

Life's Principles provide foundational criteria: use sunlight as primary energy, use only needed energy, recycle all materials, reward cooperation, focus on diversity, demand local expertise, and let form determine function [@verbrugghe2023]. These translate directly to homestead design: solar orientation, efficient appliances, closed-loop water, polyculture gardens, and local materials. Ecosystem services — provisioning (food, water, energy), regulating (climate, pollination), supporting (nutrient cycling, habitat), and cultural — offer a second framework where urban homesteads can produce multiple services simultaneously, e.g., a green roof with edible plants provides food, thermal regulation, biodiversity habitat, and aesthetic value [@pedersenzari2020].

Understanding trade-offs between ecosystem services is critical — maximising food production can degrade water quality if not managed holistically [@pedersenzari2020]. Ecological network analysis (ENA) offers metrics by modelling homestead resource flows like ecological food webs. The Lavasa masterplan exemplifies this: mimicking the pre-development hydrological cycle reduced runoff through permeable surfaces, multilayered roofs, and onsite water treatment [@borham2024]. This ecosystem-level approach treats the homestead as an organism within an urban ecosystem.

## Key Points
- Life's Principles provide 9 criteria translatable to homestead systems
- Ecosystem services framework enables multi-function design
- Understanding service trade-offs prevents unintended harm
- ENA provides quantitative tools for measuring circularity
