## Summary

The Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC), founded in 1602, was the world's first multinational corporation and the first company to issue stock. It was granted quasi-governmental powers including the ability to wage war and establish colonies. Between 1602 and 1796, the VOC sent nearly a million Europeans to Asia on 4,785 ships, netting over 2.5 million tonnes of trade goods. At its peak, it was the richest private company in history, with 150 merchant ships, 40 warships, and a private army of 10,000 soldiers.

The Cultivation System (Cultuurstelsel, 1830-1870) institutionalized forced labor: Indonesian farmers were compelled to use 20% of their land for cash crops (coffee, sugar, indigo). The net profit for the Dutch treasury is estimated at 4% of Dutch GDP and around 50% of total state revenue. Between 1830 and 1870, 840 million guilders (€8 billion in 2018) were extracted from the colony. The Wikipedia article on Dutch East Indies states: "The colonial exploitation of Indonesia's population and wealth contributed to the industrialisation of the Netherlands, while simultaneously laying the foundation for the industrialisation of Indonesia." The VOC's innovations in corporate governance, limited liability, joint-stock structure, and stock exchange trading created institutional templates that persist in modern capitalism.

## Key Points

- VOC was the world's first joint-stock company and first multinational corporation
- Cultivation System generated ~50% of Dutch state revenue at its peak
- Between 1830-1870, 840 million guilders extracted from East Indies
- The VOC's financial innovations (stock exchange, limited liability) shaped modern capitalism
- An estimated 1 million slaves were active in the Dutch East Indies at peak in 17th-18th century
