## Summary

Jatropha curcas (jarak) is a tough, drought-resistant small tree that makes an excellent living fence because animals will not browse on its leaves [@martin2012]. Propagated from cuttings or seeds, it forms dense hedges and palisades around kitchen gardens where goats and cattle would otherwise break through. Its deep root system allows it to grow in degraded lands with minimal water. Growth is moderate, producing large amounts of biomass for mulch and compost. Best uses: impenetrable barrier around kitchen gardens and crop fields where livestock pressure is high. Secondary benefits: seeds yield medicinal oil and are increasingly valued for biodiesel production. The soft wood is useful only for mulch rather than firewood. Plant from cuttings at start of wet season, spacing 0.5-1m for dense hedge. Thrives in full sun and well-drained soils. Avoid planting where children might access the seeds -- they are toxic if ingested. Not suitable for animal fodder. Does not fix nitrogen. Symbiotic benefits: provides year-round barrier protection without needing expensive fencing materials. The deep root system stabilizes soil and improves water infiltration. Large biomass production contributes to soil organic matter when used as mulch. The oil has medicinal and fuel applications, adding economic value to boundary plantings. Leaf litter decomposes to improve soil fertility beneath the fence line.

Key sources: @martin2012.
