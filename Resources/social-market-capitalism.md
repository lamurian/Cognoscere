Social market capitalism, also called welfare capitalism, combines a market economy with strong social safety nets, collective bargaining, and progressive taxation. The state intervenes to correct market failures, provide public goods, and redistribute resources to ensure social cohesion [@thulin2026].

This model was developed in post-war West Germany under Ludwig Erhard, influenced by ordoliberal theory from the Freiburg School. The state actively maintains competitive markets while providing unemployment insurance, healthcare, education, and pensions. Practiced in Germany and Scandinavia, it balances efficiency with equity but faces fiscal sustainability challenges from aging populations and global competition.
