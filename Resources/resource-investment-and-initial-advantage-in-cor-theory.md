Your finding that social support (initial efficacy 0.9) receives the largest allocation share is consistent with COR theory's resource investment principle. Sonnentag and Meier (2024) [@sonnentag2024] describe how resources travel in caravans—individuals with higher initial resources invest more heavily in those same resources, producing cumulative advantage. The preservation motive is stronger for resources already possessed at high levels, creating the rich-get-richer feedback loop you observed.

Egozi Farkash et al. (2022) [@egozifarkash2022] found resource gain correlated with resilience (r=0.30, p<0.01). The strongest gain items were closeness to family and doing enjoyable things, confirming social resources are preferentially invested in. Neveu and Begout [@neveu2025] formally demonstrate U(c) = product(R_j / c_j)—higher R_j generates stronger preservation drive c_j, attracting more investment. Social support's strong stress buffering effect (family support r=-0.180 with perceived stress) confirms its high utility drives preferential allocation in the softmax framework.
