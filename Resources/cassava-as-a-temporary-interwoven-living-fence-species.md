## Summary

Cassava (Manihot esculenta, singkong) is a drought-tolerant staple food crop that can serve as a temporary living fence when stems are interwoven [@reville2017]. It is not a permanent living fence species like Lannea or Gliricidia, but rather a fast-growing annual/perennial that can fill gaps or provide immediate barrier function while slower-growing species establish. Propagation is from stem cuttings (25-30cm) planted directly into soil at the start of the wet season. Growth is rapid, reaching 1-2m within 3-4 months. Best uses: temporary filler in living fence establishment, kitchen garden barriers, dual-purpose food-and-fence planting. Roots provide food security; leaves are edible as vegetable. Interwoven stems create a semi-permeable barrier that slows livestock. Avoid using cassava as a sole permanent fence species -- it does not develop woody stems strong enough to function as long-term posts. Needs replanting each season or two. Can be damaged by livestock browsing. Tubers are harvested, leaving gaps. Symbiotic benefits: provides food security for farming families in the form of carbohydrate-rich roots and protein-rich leaves. The interwoven stems trap soil particles and organic matter, building up a small berm along the fence line. When stems decompose, they add organic matter to the soil. Cassava is an excellent pioneer species that can be gradually replaced by longer-lived fence trees as the living fence matures.

Key sources: @reville2017.
