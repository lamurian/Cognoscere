Biomimetic skyscraper design operates at three levels. At the organism level, Pearl River Tower mimics sea sponge structures with openings that funnel wind into turbines, while the Swiss Re Headquarters (the Gherkin) mimics glass sponge triangulation for structural efficiency and natural ventilation [@alali2023; @alkodmany2018]. At the behavior level, the Eastgate Centre in Zimbabwe mimics termite mound ventilation to reduce HVAC energy by 100%, and the Qatar Cacti Building uses cactus-inspired sunshades that open and close with temperature [@alali2023]. At the ecosystem level, the Lavasa Hill Station in India emulates pre-development hydrology through permeable surfaces and multilayer roofs. Elven-inspired architecture naturally follows these principles: buildings 'grow' from the landscape, use local materials, harmonize with ecosystems, and create restorative environments through curved forms, natural light, and visual connection to nature [@verbrugghe2023]. Biophilic design — incorporating natural materials, greenery, sunlight, and water features — measurably reduces cortisol, improves cognitive function, and enhances well-being in high-rise occupants.
