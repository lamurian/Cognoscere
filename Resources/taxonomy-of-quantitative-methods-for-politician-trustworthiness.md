Quantitative evaluation of politician trustworthiness falls into five method classes. **Conjoint survey experiments** causally identify which attributes drive trust perceptions. A seven-country study (n = 9,425) found incompetence carries the largest trust penalty (12 pp), but authenticity — being 'in touch with ordinary people' — is as important as integrity and competence [@valgarsson2024]. This supports a three-pillar model — competence, integrity, authenticity — robust across culturally diverse democracies.

**Standardised survey scales** measure institutional trust via items on confidence in government, parliament, and parties (0-10 scales), recommended by OECD guidelines and fielded in ESS and WVS [@oecd2017]. **Open-ended content analysis** of 1,105 Norwegian responses reveals five judgement bases: predictability, intrinsic commitment, competence, responsiveness, and extrinsic commitment. Notably, trust and distrust use different bases — predictability drives trust most, lack of intrinsic commitment drives distrust most [@winsvold2023].

**Text-as-data methods** quantify speech-vote divergence as a continuous trustworthiness proxy. Lasso regression on US Congressional Record speeches (1995-2015) achieved 99.8% party-prediction accuracy; the gap between speech and voting scores correlates with higher vote totals and incumbency [@sapirogheiler2019]. **Behavioural indicators** — promise-keeping rates, corruption convictions, audit-based measures — provide direct evidence but face data-availability constraints. Each method addresses a different facet: stated preferences (surveys), revealed preferences (conjoints), citizen frames (open-ended), behavioural divergence (text analysis), and outcomes (behavioural).
