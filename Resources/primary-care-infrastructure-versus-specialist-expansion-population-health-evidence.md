## Summary

A substantial body of evidence demonstrates that health systems built on strong primary care infrastructure achieve better population health outcomes than those oriented toward specialist care. The WHO estimates that scaling up primary health care (PHC) interventions could save 60 million lives and increase average life expectancy by 3.7 years by 2030, with 75% of projected health gains from the Sustainable Development Goals achievable through PHC [@worldhealthorganization2025a]. The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine concluded that primary care is the only component of the health care system that has been shown to increase life expectancy of the US population [@krist2025].

Cross-national comparisons from the Commonwealth Fund consistently show that countries with stronger primary care systems achieve better health outcomes, greater equity, and lower per capita costs [@fitzgerald2022]. The US, which spends only 4.7% of health care dollars on primary care compared to 14% in other high-income countries, ranks lowest among peer nations on multiple population health indicators despite having the highest overall health spending [@krist2025; @fitzgerald2022].

Econometric evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa confirms that PHC expenditure has a significant positive effect on universal health coverage (UHC) index and life expectancy at birth, and a negative effect on infant mortality [@arhin2022]. However, the effect is inelastic, meaning that achieving UHC goals requires deliberate, sustained increases in PHC investment rather than marginal adjustments.

## Key Points

- WHO estimates 60 million lives could be saved and life expectancy increased by 3.7 years through PHC scale-up by 2030 [@worldhealthorganization2025a]
- Primary care is the only health system component shown to increase US population life expectancy [@krist2025]
- The US spends 4.7% on primary care vs. 14% average in other high-income countries [@krist2025]
- PHC expenditure significantly improves UHC index, life expectancy, and reduces infant mortality in Sub-Saharan Africa [@arhin2022]
- Systems with strong primary care achieve better equity, quality, and lower costs [@fitzgerald2022]
