Leadership behaviour is one of the most studied organisational-level factors modulating employee resilience. Empirical evidence demonstrates that leadership style shapes the cognitive and motivational mechanisms underlying individual resilience in the workplace.

Humble leadership -- defined as leaders who acknowledge their limits, appreciate others' contributions, and remain open to feedback -- positively predicts employee resilience through two distinct pathways [@zhu2019]. First, humble leadership increases employees' work-related promotion focus (a 'can do' motivational orientation toward growth and achievement), which in turn facilitates resilient responses to workplace adversity. Second, it enhances perceived insider identity, making employees feel valued members of the organisation, which motivates resilient coping through social exchange processes [@zhu2019].

At the team level, humble leadership also shapes work well-being through psychological safety and error management climate [@zhang2020]. Leaders who model fallibility and openness create conditions where employees feel safe taking interpersonal risks, a prerequisite for adaptive coping. Error management climate -- shared beliefs that mistakes are learning opportunities rather than failures -- further amplifies the positive effect of humble leadership on psychological safety [@zhang2020].

These findings are consistent with social information processing theory: leaders act as salient social cues that inform employees' interpretation of workplace events and their capacity to cope. When leaders model growth-oriented responses to challenges, employees internalise similar patterns of appraisal and coping, activating the individual-level mechanisms (positive appraisal, promotion focus) that PASTOR theory identifies as core to resilience [@kalisch2015].

Evidence on negative leadership styles (abusive supervision, despotic leadership) provides the counterpoint: toxic leadership erodes psychological safety, depletes cognitive resources through chronic threat monitoring, and shifts employees toward prevention-focused (rather than promotion-focused) self-regulation, weakening resilience capacity. The empirical evidence suggests leadership functions as a contextual modulator that can strengthen or weaken the individual-level cognitive and motivational mechanisms of resilience.
