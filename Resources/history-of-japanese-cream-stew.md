Japanese cream stew (クリームシチュー) is a yōshoku dish — a Japanese take on Western-style food — that was invented in Japan, not imported from Europe [@wikimediafoundation2025]. The first printed reference to a milk-based stew appears in a 1924 cookbook by Kaneko Tezuka, but the dish only became widespread after World War II through an unexpected route: the national school lunch program [@matsumoto2025].

Journalist Shichinosuke Asano, based in the US after the war, promoted relief shipments of skim milk powder to combat malnutrition among Japanese children [@chikochan2026]. The powdered milk provided vital protein and calcium, but children found it unpalatable when served as a drink. School kitchens solved this by thickening the skim milk with flour and simmering it with meat and vegetables, creating a dish called "white stew" that became a regular school lunch item [@chikochan2026; @foodinjapan2026]. This white stew is the direct ancestor of today's cream stew, and many Japanese adults still associate its taste with childhood nostalgia.

In 1966, House Foods launched "Stew Mix," the first instant cream stew roux, inspired by an employee who had fond memories of white stew from his school days [@housefoods2025]. The company studied Irish stew during development and designed the flavor to pair well with rice, Japan's staple food [@foodinjapan2026; @chikochan2026]. Solid roux blocks — similar to Japanese curry roux blocks — followed later. The instant product was a huge commercial success, cementing cream stew as a beloved winter comfort food in Japanese homes.
