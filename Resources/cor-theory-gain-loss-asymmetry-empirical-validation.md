COR theory's loss asymmetry principle—resource loss is more potent than gain—validates the simulation finding that regeneration spikes are larger when resources are low. Sonnentag and Meier (2024) [@sonnentag2024] confirmed that loss cycles move faster than gain cycles and that the gain paradox where resource value increases precisely when depleted is robust. The same intervention produces larger effects at low resource levels, matching R' = base_regeneration × (1 − R).

Egozi Farkash et al. (2022) [@egozifarkash2022] provided direct evidence from N=2,000 during COVID-19: resource loss correlated with traumatic symptoms at r=0.66 (p<0.01), while gain correlated with resilience at r=0.30 (p<0.01)—a 2.2× asymmetry. Loss explained 62.7% of PTSD variance. Neveu and Begout [@neveu2025] formally showed the preservation motive reduces resource value more for those with fewer resources, amplifying regeneration potential. The loss-to-distress effect is 2.2× larger than gain-to-resilience effect, consistent with larger regeneration spikes in low-R scenarios.
