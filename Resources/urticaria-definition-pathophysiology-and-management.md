## Summary

Urticaria, commonly known as hives, is a mast cell-driven skin disease characterised by the sudden appearance of pruritic (itchy) wheals, angioedema, or both [@zuberbier2022]. Wheals are raised, erythematous or skin-coloured lesions with central pallor that typically resolve within 24 hours without residual scarring. Angioedema involves deeper dermal and subcutaneous swelling, most often affecting the lips, eyelids, genitals, and extremities, and may persist up to 72 hours. Acute urticaria lasts fewer than 6 weeks; chronic urticaria persists beyond that threshold [@mehta2026]. The lifetime prevalence of acute urticaria is approximately 20%, while chronic spontaneous urticaria (CSU) affects roughly 1% of the global population, with higher prevalence in women aged 30–50. Quality of life impairment is comparable to or worse than psoriasis or atopic dermatitis, with significant psychiatric comorbidity including anxiety (up to 40%) and depression (7–30%) [@mehta2026].

## Pathophysiology, Classification, and Management

The central mechanism involves cutaneous mast cell and basophil degranulation, releasing histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and cytokines that cause vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and dermal oedema. CSU is primarily autoimmune: type I (autoallergic) involves IgE autoantibodies against self-antigens; type IIb involves IgG autoantibodies targeting the high-affinity IgE receptor (FcεRI) or IgE itself [@mehta2026]. Chronic inducible urticaria (CIndU) is triggered by physical stimuli such as pressure, cold, heat, exercise, sunlight, or water. Diagnosis is primarily clinical, supported by a limited laboratory workup (CBC, ESR/CRP, and thyroid studies when indicated); routine extensive testing is unnecessary [@zuberbier2022]. Stepwise pharmacotherapy begins with regular, scheduled second-generation H1-antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine), which may be up-titrated up to 4× standard dose. Refractory cases progress to omalizumab (anti-IgE), dupilumab, or cyclosporine. Short courses of systemic corticosteroids are reserved for acute flares. Prognosis is generally favourable: 30–50% remit within 12 months, though up to 30% may have symptoms persisting beyond 5 years [@mehta2026].
