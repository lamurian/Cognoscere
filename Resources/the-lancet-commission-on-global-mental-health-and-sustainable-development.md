The Lancet Commission on global mental health and sustainable development (2018) provided a comprehensive framework linking mental health to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), arguing that mental health is a fundamental human right and a global development priority [@patel2018]. The Commission called for transformative action across three pillars: treatment, prevention, and promotion.

Key recommendations include: scaling up evidence-based interventions for priority mental disorders through integration into universal health coverage; investing in prevention across the life course, particularly in early childhood and adolescence when most mental disorders first emerge; promoting mental health through structural interventions addressing social determinants; strengthening mental health workforce capacity, particularly through task-sharing with non-specialists; and ensuring human rights protections for people with mental disorders. The Commission estimated that scaling up treatment coverage to 80% for depression, schizophrenia, and alcohol use disorders would require an additional investment of US$3-4 per person per year in LMICs, with returns exceeding costs by a factor of 4:1 [@patel2018].

The Commission highlighted that mental health affects progress across multiple SDGs, including poverty reduction (SDG 1), good health and well-being (SDG 3), quality education (SDG 4), decent work (SDG 8), and reduced inequalities (SDG 10). It called for mental health to be embedded in national health policies, financing mechanisms, and information systems. A follow-up review (2025) confirmed persistent challenges including stigma, underfunding, workforce shortages, and fragmented systems, while noting increased global awareness post-COVID-19 and the emergence of promising innovations in digital health and community-based care [@abdulla2025].
