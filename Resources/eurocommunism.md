Eurocommunism was a revisionist tendency within Western European communist parties during the 1970s and 1980s. It accepted parliamentary democracy, political pluralism, and civil liberties, breaking with the revolutionary model and Soviet leadership. Major proponents included the Italian Communist Party (PCI), Spanish Communist Party (PCE), and French Communist Party (PCF) [@thulin2026].

Eurocommunists argued that socialism could be achieved through democratic means rather than revolutionary dictatorship. The PCI under Enrico Berlinguer pursued a historic compromise with Christian Democrats. Eurocommunism declined after the fall of the Soviet Union, with most parties evolving into democratic socialist or social democratic organizations.
