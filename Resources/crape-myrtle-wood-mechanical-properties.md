Crape myrtle timber, traded as Pyinma or Asian satinwood (Lagerstroemia spp.), is a moderate-density hardwood from Southeast Asia. The Wood Database reports specific gravity of 0.55–0.71, Janka hardness of 1,090 lbf, Modulus of Rupture of 14,120 psi, and Modulus of Elasticity of 1,566,000 psi [@wooddatabase2010]. These values are substantially higher than willow across all mechanical measures.

The tree reaches 40–65 ft with trunk diameters of 2–3 ft in its native range. Heartwood is golden to reddish brown and darkens with age. Grain is typically straight with common curly figure. The common ornamental crape myrtle (L. indica) is smaller — typically a multi-stemmed shrub or small tree up to 30 ft — but produces wood with similar density and hardness [@wooddatabase2010]. Crape myrtle wood works well with tools, turns and finishes well, and is stronger than willow by a wide margin.
