Marine energy harnesses power from waves, tides, salinity gradients, and temperature differences in the ocean. The global potential is estimated at 20,000 to 80,000 TWh per year. Most technologies remain pre-commercial, with tidal power being the most advanced.

## Tidal Power

**Tidal Barrage**: Dam-like structure across a tidal estuary. Water flows through turbines during incoming and outgoing tides. The Sihwa Lake Tidal Power Station (South Korea, 254 MW) is the world's largest. High construction costs and significant environmental impact on estuarine ecosystems.

**Tidal Stream**: Underwater turbines operate in fast-moving tidal currents (typically over 2 m/s). The MeyGen project (Scotland) is the largest tidal stream array. Lower environmental impact than barrages but less mature technology.

**Tidal Range**: Global potential exceeds 300 TWh/year. Predictable twice-daily cycles make tidal power uniquely forecastable among renewables.

## Wave Power

Wave energy converters (WECs) capture energy from surface waves. Four main types:
- Point absorber buoys -- oscillating bodies that drive generators
- Surface attenuators -- floating articulated structures
- Oscillating water columns -- trapped air drives turbines through wave action
- Overtopping devices -- waves fill a reservoir above sea level

Global wave energy potential is estimated at 8,000-80,000 TWh/year. Best resources are between 30 and 60 degrees latitude on western coasts. Several multi-MW array projects are moving toward commercial viability.

## Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion (OTEC)

Exploits temperature differences between warm surface water (25-30C) and cold deep water (4-5C). An organic working fluid drives a turbine in a Rankine cycle. Potential exceeds 10,000 TWh/year but is limited to tropical waters. Very low efficiency (3-5%) due to small temperature differential.

## Challenges

Marine environments present extreme engineering challenges: corrosion, biofouling, storms, and high installation/maintenance costs. LCOE for tidal and wave energy remains higher than wind and solar ($150-300/MWh), though costs are declining. Environmental concerns include collision risk for marine mammals, underwater noise, and alteration of coastal sediment transport.
