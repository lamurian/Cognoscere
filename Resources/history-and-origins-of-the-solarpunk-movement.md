Solarpunk was coined in 2008 on the blog 'Republic of the Bees' in a post titled 'From Steampunk to Solarpunk'. The author was inspired by the MS Beluga Skysails, the world's first cargo ship partially powered by a computer-controlled kite rig. The post imagined a speculative fiction subgenre focused on renewable energy and revival of older, less damaging technologies such as sailing.

In 2009, literary publicist Matt Staggs published a 'GreenPunk Manifesto' describing a technophilic genre centred on DIY technologies and positive ecological and social change. The term 'solarpunk' remained niche until 2014, when Tumblr user Olivia Louise (missolivialouise) posted concept art expanding the vision: a society interconnected with nature and art, where children learn both technology and gardening, cities are lush with vegetation, and corporate capitalism gives way to small local businesses.

Later in 2014, researcher Adam Flynn contributed to Project Hieroglyph (Arizona State University) with 'Solarpunk: Notes toward a manifesto', giving the genre academic grounding. Based on Flynn's contributions, 'A Solarpunk Manifesto' was published in 2019 by The Solarpunk Community, containing 22 principles describing solarpunk society from visual aesthetics to concepts of justice.

In 2024, solarpunk entered The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction, with James Machell pointing to Norman Spinrad's 'Songs from the Stars' (1976) as the subgenre's first text, and describing solarpunk as 'a rebellion against a rebellion, born out of dystopia fatigue.' Solarpunk Magazine launched in the same period, and the movement continues to grow across literature, art, architecture, activism, and urban planning.
