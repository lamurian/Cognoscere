## Executive Summary

This research investigates the relationship between psychological resources and psychological resilience — two closely related but conceptually distinct constructs in positive psychology. Three questions were addressed: what psychological resources are, how they are demarcated from resilience, and what the range of their empirical correlation is.

**Definition.** Psychological resources are relatively stable individual-level dispositions that function as protective factors under stress. Hobfoll's Conservation of Resources (COR) theory defines them as "entities that are either centrally valued in their own right or that act as a means to obtain centrally valued ends" [@hobfoll2002]. Key resources include self-efficacy, optimism, hope, self-esteem, emotional stability, wisdom, and — in some frameworks — resilience itself [@luthans2017; @pellerin2022; @zlobina2024].

**Demarcation.** The theoretical boundary between resources and resilience is drawn along temporal and causal lines. Resources exist prior to adversity; resilience is the observed outcome of maintaining stable functioning during and after adversity [@bonanno2004]. In COR theory, resource loss drives distress while resource gain enables resilience, but the two are not identical — loss has stronger effects than gain [@egozifarkash2022]. The PsyCap framework creates ambiguity by nesting resilience as one of four resources within psychological capital [@luthans2017]. An emerging resolution frames psychological flexibility as the mediating mechanism that connects resources to resilient outcomes [@pellerin2022].

**Correlation range.** Across meta-analyses and primary studies, the bivariate correlation between a specific psychological resource and a resilience measure typically falls between **r = 0.20 and r = 0.55**, with meta-analytic averages around **r ≈ 0.35–0.50** for the strongest predictors (self-efficacy, hope) [@zhao2022; @he2013; @egozifarkash2022]. Resource loss correlates more strongly with distress (r ≈ 0.50–0.66) than resource gain correlates with positive outcomes (r ≈ 0.20–0.30), an asymmetry consistent with COR theory's loss-primacy principle [@hobfoll2002; @egozifarkash2022]. The moderate-to-strong correlation supports the view that resources and resilience are related but distinct constructs.

## Confidence Assessment

| Question | Confidence | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 (WHY): Why are psychological resources distinct from resilience? | **High** | Multiple frameworks (COR theory, Bonanno trajectories, Pellerin & Raufaste) converge on the same temporal-causal distinction. PsyCap's nested treatment is a known but acknowledged ambiguity. |
| Q2 (HOW): How do psychological resources correlate with resilience? | **High** | Meta-analytic data from Zhao et al. (2022, 66 studies), COR theory evidence from Egozi Farkash et al. (2022, N=2,000), and multiple primary studies provide consistent effect size estimates. |

## Known Gaps

- No single meta-analysis directly aggregates the bivariate correlation between each psychological resource and resilience as a distinct outcome (most studies correlate resources with QoL or distress, not resilience per se).
- The PsyCap framework's treatment of resilience as a resource component creates measurement circularity that has not been fully resolved.
- Most evidence comes from Western and East Asian populations; cross-cultural replication in understudied regions is limited.
- Longitudinal studies tracking resource depletion and resilience trajectories over extended periods (>1 year) are scarce.
- The causal mechanisms (beyond psychological flexibility) that mediate the resource-to-resilience pathway are not fully mapped.
