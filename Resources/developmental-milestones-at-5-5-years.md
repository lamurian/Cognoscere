## Summary

At five and a half years, the child typically enters or nears kindergarten — a major life transition that demands new levels of independence, social navigation, and sustained attention. Weight averages about 20.0 kg (44.1 lb) and height 113 cm (44.5 in). The child can now follow multi-step classroom routines, maintain friendships independently, and engage in more structured learning activities [@misirliyan2023].

## Key Points

**Body Weight and Height:** Median weight ~20.0 kg (range 16.6–23.8 kg); median height ~113 cm. Growth steady. First permanent molars erupting. Loss of primary incisors may begin — "mixed dentition" stage approaches.

**Emotional Maturity:** Adjusts to school routines and transitions. Forms stable friendships with 2–3 peers. Shows empathy and can articulate others' feelings. Understands and respects classroom rules. May experience performance anxiety (fear of being wrong or failing). Self-esteem becomes tied to competence — needs encouragement for effort, not just outcomes. Can resolve simple peer conflicts with adult guidance. Shows pride in completing tasks independently.

**Psychological Development:** Concrete operational thinking emerges (Piaget). The child can now perform mental operations on concrete objects — understands conservation of number, length, and mass. Understands left and right (own body, not others'). Can sequence events and tell time on the hour. Reads some sight words; may be reading simple books. Writes own name and some other words. Understands simple addition and subtraction with objects. Draws detailed pictures with background and context.

**Motoric Development:** Gross motor: skips, gallops, and hops with proficiency; balances on one foot for 15+ seconds; jumps rope with practice; throws and catches balls of various sizes with better accuracy; rides bicycle (may be without training wheels); begins organised sports (soccer, gymnastics). Fine motor: writes name and copies most letters; draws detailed person with clothing and facial features; uses scissors precisely; ties shoelaces (some children); builds complex structures with blocks; uses tools (hammer, screwdriver) with supervision [@centersfordiseasecontrolandprevention2026].

**Speech and Language Development:** Speech completely intelligible; uses complex and compound sentences; vocabulary 2500–3000+ words; tells elaborate stories with beginning, middle, end; uses language to negotiate, explain, and persuade; understands metaphors and multiple-meaning words; recognises and produces rhyming words; segments words into syllables; letter-sound correspondence developing (phonological awareness); may still have difficulty with th, r, s, l, v sounds; can carry on extended conversations on varied topics [@nationalinstituteondeafnessandothercommunicationdisorders2022].

**Signs of Growth Retardation:** Red flags: cannot skip; cannot balance on one foot; clumsy gait; speech still unintelligible to unfamiliar people; cannot tell a coherent story; cannot follow classroom routines; unable to separate from parent; no friends; extreme aggression or withdrawal; cannot draw recognisable pictures; cannot write own name; does not understand simple number concepts (more/less, counting); short attention span (cannot focus 5+ minutes); loss of previously acquired skills.
