## Summary

The Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS) is a 14-item self-report instrument developed by Zigmond and Snaith (1983) specifically for use in non-psychiatric hospital settings. It consists of two 7-item subscales: HADS-A (anxiety) and HADS-D (depression). The items are carefully designed to exclude somatic symptoms (such as sleep disturbance, appetite changes, and fatigue) that might be confounded with physical illness symptoms, making it particularly suitable for medical populations. Each item is rated 0-3, with subscale scores ranging from 0-21. Recommended cutoff for both subscales is >=8, with severity bands: 8-10 borderline, 11-21 abnormal.

The original validation by Zigmond and Snaith (1983) in a general medical outpatient sample (n=100) found the instrument effectively discriminated between anxiety disorders, depressive disorders, and normal states, with optimal case identification at cutoff >=8 for both subscales [@zigmond1983]. A landmark review by Bjelland et al. (2002) covering 747 identified papers found that at cutoff >=8, the HADS-A demonstrated sensitivity of 0.80-0.90 and specificity of 0.80-0.88, while the HADS-D demonstrated sensitivity of 0.75-0.85 and specificity of 0.75-0.90 [@bjelland2002].

The two-factor structure (anxiety and depression as separate dimensions) is generally supported, though a bi-factor model with a general distress factor has also been proposed. Cronbach's alpha ranges from 0.68-0.93 for HADS-A and 0.67-0.90 for HADS-D across studies. The HADS is among the most widely used instruments in psycho-oncology, cardiology, and general hospital settings.

## Key Points

- 14 items: 7 anxiety (HADS-A) and 7 depression (HADS-D), 0-3 scale, total 0-21 per subscale
- Original validation (Zigmond & Snaith 1983): effective case identification at cutoff >=8 [@zigmond1983]
- Review (Bjelland 2002, 747 papers): at cutoff >=8 HADS-A Se 0.80-0.90, Sp 0.80-0.88; HADS-D Se 0.75-0.85, Sp 0.75-0.90 [@bjelland2002]
- Designed to exclude somatic symptoms to avoid confounding with physical illness
- Widely validated in medical populations: oncology, cardiology, primary care
- Two-factor structure generally supported; bi-factor model also proposed
- Among the most used instruments in psycho-oncology and hospital-based mental health research
