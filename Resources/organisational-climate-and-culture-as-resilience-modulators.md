Organisational climate -- shared perceptions of policies, practices, and procedures -- functions as a supra-individual factor that modulates the cognitive and emotional mechanisms of individual resilience.

A learning-oriented organisational climate, characterised by support for experimentation, feedback, and continuous improvement, has been linked to employee resilience through its effects on proactive work behaviours [@kuntz2017]. Employees in such climates develop stronger problem-solving capacities and are more likely to appraise challenges as opportunities rather than threats, consistent with the PASTOR framework's emphasis on positive situation classification [@kalisch2015].

Psychosocial safety climate (PSC) -- organisational policies and practices that prioritise worker psychological health -- operates through both the JD-R model's health impairment and motivational pathways [@bakker2017a]. High PSC organisations reduce hindrance demands (role conflict, organisational constraints) while enhancing job resources (autonomy, social support), creating conditions that buffer the depletion of self-regulatory resources needed for resilient coping. Research shows PSC moderates the relationship between job demands and emotional exhaustion, with employees in high-PSC contexts showing better stress recovery [@idris2014].

Social support -- the provision of psychological and material resources by social networks -- enhances individual resilience through multiple neurobiological pathways [@sippel2015]. Organisational cultures that foster strong social networks and peer support systems increase perceived functional support, which is a stronger predictor of resilience than structural support (network size). The type, source, and timing of support must match individual needs to be effective -- unsolicited or poorly timed support can be counterproductive [@sippel2015].

Error management climate -- shared beliefs that errors are learning opportunities -- moderates the relationship between humble leadership and psychological safety [@zhang2020]. In teams with high error management climate, employees experience greater psychological safety and subsequently higher work well-being. This demonstrates how organisational-level climate factors can amplify or dampen the effects of leadership on individual resilience mechanisms.

Hollaar et al. (2025) found that only 2 of 24 studies on workplace resilience interventions adopted a socioecological approach addressing both individual and organisational factors, despite organisational factors being primary drivers of stress [@hollaar2025]. This gap underscores the need for research that simultaneously measures climate-level moderators and individual-level mechanisms.
