Crape myrtle (Lagerstroemia indica and hybrids) is used for living fences and arborsculpture, particularly in Chinese landscaping traditions where young trees are slowly woven around bamboo frames to create fences, chairs, vases, and other decorative forms. The plant offers distinct aesthetic advantages: showy summer flowers in purple, lavender, white, pink, and red for 100+ days, attractive peeling bark in cinnamon, cream, and orange tones, and heat and drought tolerance once established [@moore2006].

Crape myrtle grows as a multi-stemmed shrub or small tree up to 40 feet with moderate growth rate. However, it is susceptible to powdery mildew, bark scale, Cercospora leaf spot, and aphids, particularly in humid shaded conditions [@moore2006]. It is non-native to the US (originating from China and Korea) [@cityofroundrock2025]. Desert Willow (Chilopsis linearis) serves as a native alternative in arid US regions with similar flowering display but greater ecological value for local wildlife.

Young crape myrtles are trained by bending and weaving trunks around bamboo or metal frames, secured with soft ties to prevent bark damage. The multi-stemmed habit is ideal for shaping. Plants are drought tolerant with low fertility needs and flower for over 100 days. They are slower to establish than willow with less flexible branches. Annual pruning and pest monitoring are required.
