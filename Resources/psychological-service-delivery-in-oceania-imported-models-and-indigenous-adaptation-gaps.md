## Summary

Psychological service delivery across Oceania (Australia, New Zealand, and the Pacific Islands) predominantly relies on imported Western models of assessment and intervention, creating gaps in root cause diagnosis for Indigenous and Pacific Islander populations. The evidence from New Zealand (Māori), Australia (Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples), and the broader Pacific region reveals consistent patterns of cultural mismatch.

A scoping review of psychological services for Indigenous populations across CANZUS nations found that most intervention studies used adapted Cognitive-Behavioural Therapy (CBT) or parent training programs such as Triple P—both Western-developed models [@plessas2021]. Only 57% of behavior analysts in the US worked with diverse populations, and most had little or no training in cultural adaptation. The review identified that 'the extent to which behavior-analytic interventions are offered to Indigenous populations in accessible and culturally appropriate ways is unknown.' Key barriers included cultural disconnect, distrust, limited options for culturally appropriate care, and the assumption that one model fits all communities.

In New Zealand, Māori-specific models such as Mahi a Atua and Te Whare Tapa Whā have been developed as culturally grounded alternatives, yet the Plessas review found that 'research on adapting talking therapies for Māori is scant.' A survey of Indigenous practitioners in Australia found that evidence-based programmes delivered by Indigenous practitioners remain 'largely untested.'

For the Pacific Islands, data is even more limited. The existing mental health infrastructure across Fiji, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, and other Pacific nations relies heavily on psychiatric services delivered in hospital settings, with minimal community-based psychological assessment or intervention. Standardized approaches imported through WHO programs or donor funding may not address the root causes of distress in Pacific contexts, where family systems, spirituality, and communal land relationships are central to wellbeing.

Notably, even the well-evidenced organisational interventions from Australia (e.g., workplace bullying interventions that diagnose root causes through risk audits [@li2023]) represent exceptions within a broader landscape where standardized, non-individualized approaches remain the default.

## Key Points

- Most interventions for Indigenous Oceanic populations are adapted Western models (CBT, Triple P)
- Research on culturally adapted therapies for Māori is described as 'scant'
- Indigenous practitioner-led programs remain largely untested
- Pacific Island nations have minimal community-based psychological assessment infrastructure
- Imported standardized protocols may not address root causes embedded in family, spirituality, and communal land systems
- Organisational interventions that diagnose root causes exist but are exceptions
