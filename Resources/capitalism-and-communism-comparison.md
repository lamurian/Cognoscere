Capitalism and communism differ fundamentally on ownership, allocation mechanisms, incentives, and the role of the state. Capitalism prioritizes private ownership, market prices, and profit incentives. Communism prioritizes common or state ownership, central planning, and social duty [@morganelli2025].

On ownership, capitalism vests control in private individuals and corporations; communism vests it in the state or the community. On allocation, capitalism uses market prices determined by supply and demand; communism uses central planning targets. On incentives, capitalism relies on profit and material gain; communism relies on social duty or state coercion. On the role of the state, capitalism prefers minimal or regulatory functions; communism requires the state as owner and planner.

Both are totalizing industrial-age ideologies with internal factions. Both can coexist with autocracy or democracy. Most real economies operate on the spectrum between the two ideal types.
