This executive summary synthesises research on the structural integrity of cob walls compared to modern alternatives, primarily concrete, and outlines approaches for building eco-friendly structures that last.

The core finding is that cob and concrete are fundamentally different materials serving different structural roles. Cob's compressive strength (0.45-0.89 MPa) is 20-50x lower than concrete's (17-41 MPa), yet it is sufficient for two-storey residential buildings when walls are 450-600 mm thick [@pullen2009; @morel2021]. The primary structural concern is not absolute strength but variability — cob's coefficient of variation (22%) far exceeds manufactured materials, requiring conservative design factors. Evidence is rated **high** (supported by multiple peer-reviewed studies and decades of field observation).

Tensile strength is cob's critical weakness (0.08-0.24 MPa vs 2-5 MPa for concrete), but straw fiber reinforcement provides post-crack bridging if fibers exceed 4-5 inches in length [@pullen2009]. Seismically, cob's monolithic construction outperforms adobe but remains vulnerable to out-of-plane failure without reinforcement — timber posts, TRM meshes, and roof diaphragm connections are required in seismic zones [@peri2021]. Evidence for seismic performance is **moderate** (extrapolated from rammed earth studies; specific cob shake-table data is limited).

Durability is where cob and concrete diverge most sharply. Cob's primary threat is moisture — strength drops 4x when moisture rises from 2% to 12% — while concrete's primary threat is chemical degradation (steel corrosion, carbonation) [@morel2021]. With proper protection (stem walls, eaves, breathable renders), cob buildings last centuries. Concrete structures require steel reinforcement that limits lifespan to 50-100 years due to corrosion. Sustainability strongly favours cob: it uses 1% of concrete's embodied energy, is fully recyclable, and passively regulates indoor humidity [@morel2021; @giada2019].

For eco-friendly longevity, five principles emerge: (1) moisture management through foundation isolation and roof protection, (2) conservative wall dimensions (450-600 mm), (3) structural reinforcement for seismic zones, (4) passive solar design to minimise thermal cycling, and (5) ongoing maintenance (render every 15-25 years). The main barrier to wider adoption is not technical but institutional — building codes, insurance, skills training, and perception [@morel2021].
