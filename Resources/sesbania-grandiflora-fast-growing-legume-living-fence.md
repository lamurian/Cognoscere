## Summary

Sesbania grandiflora (turi) is a fast-growing, soft-wooded leguminous tree widely used as a living fence in West Timor. In Kupang trials it showed infiltration rate of 0.75 cm/hour and organic matter improvement to 2.36% vs 1.76% in control [@matheus2009]. Best uses: edible flowers and young pods as vegetables, high-protein fodder, rapid biomass for green manure, firewood, and light construction. It is one of only five legume species in East Timor used simultaneously for food, fodder, medicine, and domestic purposes [@guterres2022]. Plant from cuttings or seeds at wet season onset. Prefers rainfall >1000mm annually and well-drained loams. Avoid in driest parts of West Timor with <800mm rainfall. Less drought-tolerant than Lannea or Gliricidia. Soft wood means shorter lifespan as fence posts. Requires regular pruning. Symbiotic benefits: fixes nitrogen through both root nodules and stem nodules (Azorhizobium caulinodum), making it uniquely efficient even in waterlogged conditions. Rapid leaf production provides continuous organic matter. Flowers attract bees and birds. Deep roots improve soil structure and water infiltration [@matheus2009].

Key sources: @matheus2009, @guterres2022.
