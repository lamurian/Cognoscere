# Solarpunk Village: Real-World Case Studies

These established ecovillages are the closest real-world approximations to a solarpunk village. Each demonstrates different approaches to size, governance, infrastructure, and livelihood.

---

## Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage (Missouri, USA)

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Founded | 1997 (mid-1990s) |
| Population | ~60 |
| Land | 280 acres (93% green space conserved) |
| Governance | Evolved from full consensus → village council + committees[^1] |
| Food self-sufficiency | 1–5% on-site initially; 15+ acres native grasses planted[^1] |
| Legal structure | 501(c)(2) land trust + 501(c)(3) education nonprofit |
| Key innovation | Vehicle cooperative, community land trust, Conservation Reserve Program management |
| Resource use | ~10% of average American consumption with equal quality of life[^1] |
| Buildings | Straw bale, clay, reclaimed lumber, cob |

Dancing Rabbit's mission: "To create a society, the size of a small town or village, made up of individuals and communities of various sizes and social structures, which allows and encourages its members to live sustainably."[^1]

---

## Findhorn Ecovillage (Scotland, UK)

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Founded | 1962 (eco-village designation later) |
| Population | ~300 |
| Land | Compact site, ~30 community/commercial buildings[^2] |
| Energy | 4 onshore wind turbines, 250 kW biomass boiler, building-mounted solar PV, solar hot water, community-owned electricity distribution network[^2] |
| Key innovation | Community-owned microgrid, EV charging, integrated renewable energy systems |
| Governance | Charitable trust + community councils |

Findhorn is one of the most energy-complete ecovillages in Europe, actively studied by Newcastle University for energy trilemma research (balancing cost, carbon, and resilience).[^2]

## Sieben Linden Ecovillage (Germany)

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Founded | 1997 |
| Population | ~140 |
| Land | ~100 hectares |
| Buildings | Straw-bale construction (pioneer of modern straw-bale techniques in Europe) |
| Food | ~80% from permaculture on-site |
| Governance | Consensus + committees |

Sieben Linden is notable for its construction innovation and high degree of food self-sufficiency.[^3]

---

## EcoVillage at Ithaca (New York, USA)

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Founded | 1996 |
| Population | ~160 |
| Land | 175 acres (96% undeveloped)[^4] |
| Governance | Cohousing clusters with consensus |
| Food | 21–50% from site (mature permaculture systems) |
| Key innovation | Cohousing model, pragmatic sustainability (energy reduction first, not sexy tech)[^4] |

> "Rather than opt for the sexiest (and often most expensive) appropriate technologies, we put our money into energy reduction measures first." — Liz Walker, EVI founder[^4]

---

## Cloughjordan Ecovillage (Ireland)

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Founded | 2009 |
| Population | ~55 households |
| Land | 67 acres |
| Energy | District heating system, solar PV |
| Food | Community farm, permaculture market garden |
| Key innovation | Built around a working train station (transport integration); strong regional economic ties |

Cloughjordan is one of Europe's best examples of transport-integrated ecovillage design, with rail access to Limerick and Dublin.[^3]

---

## Lammas Ecovillage (Wales, UK)

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Founded | 2009 |
| Population | ~9 smallholdings (~20–30 people) |
| Land | 70+ acres |
| Energy | Fully off-grid (solar, biomass, micro-hydro) |
| Buildings | Natural materials (straw, timber, cob) |
| Key innovation | Helped create Welsh Government's "One Planet Development" policy |
| Style | Low-impact, DIY, self-build[^5] |

Lammas successfully changed national planning policy through its precedent. Residents practice low-impact, off-grid living with buildings constructed from natural materials.[^5]
