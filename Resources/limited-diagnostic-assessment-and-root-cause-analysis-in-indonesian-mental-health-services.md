## Summary

Several systemic barriers in Indonesia's mental health system limit the capacity for individualized diagnostic assessment and root cause analysis, leading to a reliance on standardized or blanket interventions. First, workforce shortages are severe: Indonesia has only 0.3 psychiatrists per 100,000 population versus the WHO-recommended 3.3 per 100,000 [@anjara2019]. This forces task-sharing approaches where non-specialist GPs, trained only through brief add-on modules, provide mental health care.

Second, GPs face structural constraints. Diagnosing mental health problems in primary care is difficult because most patients present with physical ailments, time constraints limit thorough clinical interviews, and patients may be unfamiliar with articulating psychological symptoms [@anjara2019]. Up to 90% of referrals to offsite practitioners never result in an appointment. The study found that GPs may not recognize or want to spend time uncovering patients' psychological needs without a screening procedure in place.

Third, stigma and lack of community awareness create barriers. A qualitative study across four Indonesian islands found that general practitioners sometimes refused to believe depression complaints or provide referral letters [@munira2023]. Participants reported being rejected for referral letters twice before self-harm scars prompted acceptance. Only 20% of Puskesmas provide mental health services despite policy mandates [@hidayat2023].

Fourth, the reliance on pharmacological interventions predominates. The WHO mhGAP protocol emphasizes a biomedical approach, and in the Indonesian context, psychotropic medications are often prescribed without adequate monitoring or follow-up [@osborne2026]. The economic evaluation in the Anjara trial showed lower average costs in the specialist (psychologist) arm partly because fewer follow-up appointments were made, suggesting patients did not receive sustained, individualized therapeutic interventions.

## Key Points

- 0.3 psychiatrists per 100,000 vs WHO-recommended 3.3 per 100,000
- GPs lack time and training for thorough diagnostic clinical interviews
- Up to 90% of specialist referrals never result in an appointment
- GPs may refuse to believe depression complaints or issue referral letters
- Only 20% of Puskesmas provide any mental health services
- Pharmacological interventions dominate; psychosocial treatment plans are rarely developed
- Standardized mhGAP protocols do not address individual root causes or social determinants
