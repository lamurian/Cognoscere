## Summary

The Eastgate Centre in Harare, inspired by termite mounds, uses passive ventilation that reduces energy costs by 65% through night cooling, thermal mass in clay bricks, and convective air currents [@verbrugghe2023]. This scales to residential homesteads: a solar chimney, exposed thermal mass, and cross-ventilation can maintain comfort without mechanical systems. Cairo Gate Residence combines traditional wind catchers with solar energy and endemic plants, achieving 50% energy savings [@verbrugghe2023]. Water efficiency follows nature's decentralised cycling model. The Sahara Forest Project mimics the Namibian desert beetle to produce fresh water in arid climates via saltwater-cooled greenhouses that generate irrigation water from condensation [@verbrugghe2023].

At building scale, biomimetic water strategies include rainwater harvesting, gravity-driven irrigation, constructed wetlands for wastewater treatment, and bioswales for stormwater management [@borham2024]. Material efficiency draws from biological minimalism: the Gherkin Tower's diagrid structure (inspired by the Venus flower basket sponge) uses less steel than conventional frames while maximising natural light [@verbrugghe2023]. Self-healing bio-concrete and photocatalytic self-cleaning cement (inspired by lotus leaves) further reduce material lifecycle impacts. For urban homesteads, these translate to lighter structural loads, longer material lifespans, and reduced embodied carbon.
