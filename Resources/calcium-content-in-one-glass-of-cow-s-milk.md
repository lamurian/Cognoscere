## Summary

One standard glass (1 cup, 240 mL) of cow's milk contains approximately 300 mg of calcium. The US Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion reports that a cup of milk provides about 300 mg of calcium, equivalent to 23% of the Daily Value [@officeofdiseasepreventionandhealthpromotion2026]. This value aligns with the USDA Dietary Guidelines, which also cite 300 mg of calcium per cup of milk.

The exact amount varies slightly depending on fat content. According to Harvard's Nutrition Source, whole milk (3.5% fat) provides 276 mg of calcium per cup, while low-fat milk (1% fat) provides 305 mg per cup [@harvardthchanschoolofpublichealth2021]. The variation is small and attributable to the displacement of calcium by milk fat in whole milk. Fortified plant-based milk alternatives vary more widely—soy milk provides 300–450 mg per cup, while almond milk ranges from 130–460 mg depending on fortification [@harvardthchanschoolofpublichealth2021].

Given that the adult RDA for calcium is 1,000–1,200 mg/day (depending on age and sex), one glass of milk supplies roughly 25–30% of daily calcium needs. This makes milk one of the most concentrated dietary sources of highly bioavailable calcium, alongside yogurt, cheese, and fortified foods [@officeofdiseasepreventionandhealthpromotion2026].
