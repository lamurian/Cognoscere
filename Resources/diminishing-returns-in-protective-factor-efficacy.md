Your formulation Δe_f ∝ (1 − e_f)—efficacy growth slows as each factor approaches 1.0—is supported by the power law of practice (Newell and Rosenbloom, 1981), where performance improves as a power function of trials: rapid early gains diminish as performance approaches asymptote. The linear approximation captures the ceiling region where marginal improvement approaches zero.

Learning curve research identifies negative acceleration as the most common pattern in skill acquisition: rapid initial improvement followed by tapering gains at plateau. The S-shaped learning curve's plateau phase corresponds to your near-ceiling region where Delta e_f approaches zero. A recent mathematical model of skill acquisition (2025) demonstrates that on the mastery timescale, individuals exhibit learning with diminishing returns with repeated plateaus.

The convex cost function (cost = allocation^1.5) in the existing simulation captures the same principle. Allocating more resources becomes increasingly expensive per unit gain, consistent with the power law's asymptotic tail where additional effort yields progressively smaller improvements.
