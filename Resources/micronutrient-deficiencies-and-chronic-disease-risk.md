## Summary

Suboptimal diet is one of the leading preventable risk factors for non-communicable diseases (NCDs) worldwide. The Global Burden of Disease Study 2017 estimated that dietary risks account for 11 million deaths and 255 million disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) globally, making diet a larger contributor to disease burden than tobacco smoking or high blood pressure [@afshin2019]. The key dietary risk factors include high intake of sodium, low intake of whole grains, low intake of fruits, low intake of nuts and seeds, low intake of vegetables, and high intake of processed meats.

Micronutrient deficiencies operate through several mechanisms. Inadequate iron impairs oxygen transport and energy metabolism, causing anaemia and reduced work capacity. Insufficient iodine disrupts thyroid hormone synthesis, leading to metabolic dysfunction and developmental impairments. Vitamin D deficiency compromises calcium absorption and bone health, and is increasingly linked to immune dysfunction. The European Nutrition and Health Report indicates that 25–75% of people in industrialised nations have dietary intakes below the RDA for vitamins D, E, folate, selenium, vitamin A, zinc, and magnesium [@gombart2020]. These suboptimal intakes contribute to a cumulative public health burden through increased infection risk, reduced productivity, and accelerated chronic disease progression.
