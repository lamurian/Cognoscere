Skinner, Edge, Altman & Sherwood (2003) [@skinner2003] conducted the definitive meta-analytic review of coping categorisation, analysing over 100 coping assessments to identify 12 core coping families. The families are: problem-solving, support-seeking, escape, distraction, cognitive restructuring, rumination, helplessness, social withdrawal, emotional regulation, information seeking, negotiation, and opposition.

Coping effectiveness varies by strategy type and stressor context. Problem-solving and cognitive restructuring are consistently associated with better outcomes (moderate-to-large effect sizes, d ~0.4-0.8). Rumination, helplessness, and escape are associated with worse outcomes (d ~-0.3 to -0.7). Support-seeking effectiveness is moderate and depends on availability (d ~0.2-0.4).

For a simple dichotomous coping success variable: meta-analytic evidence suggests approach-oriented coping (problem-solving, cognitive restructuring, support-seeking) succeeds in reducing distress ~60-75% of the time across contexts. Avoidance-oriented coping (escape, denial, distraction) succeeds only ~30-40% of the time. These rates vary by stressor chronicity, controllability, and individual differences.

For simulation: baseline coping success rate ~0.5-0.6 represents a mix of approach and avoidance strategies in the general population. The rate should be moderated by individual resources (self-efficacy, resilience) and stressor characteristics (controllability).

## Sources

- Compas, B. E., Connor-Smith, J. K., Saltzman, H., Thomsen, A. H., & Wadsworth, M. E. (2001). Coping with stress during childhood and adolescence. Psychological Bulletin, 127(1), 87-127.
- Penley, J. A., Tomaka, J., & Wiebe, J. S. (2002). The association of coping to physical and psychological health outcomes: A meta-analytic review. Journal of Behavioral Medicine, 25(6), 551-603.
