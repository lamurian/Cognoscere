Assigning monetary values to wellbeing and resilience outcomes is conceptually and methodologically challenging. Three main approaches are used:[^4][^5]

**Willingness-to-Pay (WTP) approach.** This stated-preference method surveys individuals on how much they would pay for a specified health gain (e.g., one additional QALY or improvement in wellbeing). The contingent valuation method (asking directly) and discrete choice experiments (inferring preferences from trade-off scenarios) are the primary techniques. Nimdet et al. (2015) found WTP per QALY varies widely depending on context: improving quality of life averages 0.59x GDP per capita, while extending life averages 2.03x GDP per capita. The traditional US threshold of $50,000 per QALY, though widely used, has been criticised as outdated and not empirically grounded.[^4]

**Human Capital Approach (HCA).** This method values health improvements by the expected increase in future earnings or productivity. An employee's lost workday is valued at their daily wage (adjusted for benefits). For resilience interventions, reduced presenteeism (improved focus and productivity at work) is monetised by asking employees to rate their work performance and converting the improvement into wage-equivalent units. The main limitation is that HCA may undervalue non-work-related wellbeing and discriminates against those with lower wages.[^5]

**Friction Cost Approach (FCA).** Unlike HCA, which assumes productivity loss continues until full recovery, FCA assumes organisations eventually replace absent workers after a 'friction period' (typically 3–6 months). FCA produces lower estimates of productivity costs than HCA. The choice between HCA and FCA substantially affects whether a resilience intervention appears cost-saving.[^5]

**WHO reference thresholds.** The World Health Organization recommends 1–3 times GDP per capita per DALY averted as a cost-effectiveness threshold. For workplace interventions, adaptations translate this into employer-specific thresholds: an intervention is cost-effective if its cost per wellbeing-adjusted life year or per unit of resilience improvement falls below a proportion of average salary. However, no consensus exists on the appropriate threshold for employer-based decisions.[^2][^4]
