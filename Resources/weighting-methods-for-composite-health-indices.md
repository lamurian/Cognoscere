The choice of weighting method significantly affects composite health index rankings. The UK Health Index assessment compared three approaches: time-series factor analysis (used by ONS), principal component analysis (PCA), and optimized compensatory weights. Factor analysis assumes a latent factor underpins indicators within each subdomain and uses first-factor loadings as weights. PCA is a data reduction method that redefines uncorrelated linear combinations of variables [@frenisterrantino2022].

A key distinction exists between weights as trade-off ratios (in linear aggregation, weights represent how much one indicator can compensate for another) versus weights as importance coefficients. Optimized weights, introduced by Becker et al., decompose the correlation ratio into correlated and uncorrelated components to derive weights that truly reflect importance. For the UK Health Index, optimized weights assigned 0.45 to Healthy People, 0.16 to Healthy Lives, and 0.73 to Healthy Places, substantially different from the equal 0.33 weights [@frenisterrantino2022].

The Junaid et al. study used PCA, finding that the first component explained 51-94% of variance across subdomains. The study noted that PCA is recommended only when the first component explains at least 70% of total variability, which was not met for several subdomains [@junaid2025].
