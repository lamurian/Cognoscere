## Summary

Martin-Key et al. (2022) conducted a PRISMA-compliant systematic review of 28 studies evaluating question-and-answer–based digital mental health assessment tools against gold standard clinical interviews (SCID, CIDI, MINI). The review covered studies published 2005-2021 across 7 databases.

## Key Points

### Study Characteristics
- 28 studies met inclusion criteria from 31,271 retrieved articles
- Most studies used digitized versions of existing pen-and-paper questionnaires (e.g., GAD-7, PHQ-9, WSQ)
- Gold standard references: MINI (8 studies), SCID (11 studies), CIDI (6 studies)
- Primary conditions: MDD (8 studies), GAD (12 studies), any depressive/anxiety disorder
- Sample sizes: 100–6,361 participants; mostly primary care/general population (64%)

### Diagnostic Accuracy Findings
- Sensitivity range: 0.32–1.00; Specificity range: 0.37–1.00; AUC range: 0.57–0.98
- GAD-7 (most used tool): sensitivity 0.75–0.87, specificity 0.55–0.78 for GAD
- PHQ-9: sensitivity 0.89, specificity 0.79, AUC 0.90 for MDD
- Computerized adaptive tests (CAT-MH): excellent discrimination (AUC 0.85–0.93)
- Multi-condition tools (WSQ, e-PASS, M-3, CMFC): variable accuracy across disorders

### Risk of Bias
- High risk in 43% for patient selection (case-control designs)
- 36% high risk for index test (no prespecified thresholds)
- 50% insufficient info on reference standard interpretation
- 39% insufficient timing information between index and reference tests

### Conclusions
- Digital mental health assessment field is in early stages
- Most tools are simple digitizations, not adaptive/comprehensive systems
- High-quality evidence lacking; mixed accuracy due to methodological issues
- Need for carefully designed studies in intended clinical contexts
