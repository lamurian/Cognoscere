## Summary

The Plane Tree Cube (Platanenkubus) in Nagold, Germany (2012), designed by Ludwig.Schoenle, is the closest existing precedent to a permanent steel skeleton with living wall infill. It is a 10m x 10m x 10m cubic structure where London plane trees (Platanus hispanica) are planted across 6 levels — one at ground level, five in containers on steel platforms. Trees grow through and around the steel frame, forming living walls that shade, cool, and eventually merge into a single organism through inosculation [@mylo2023].

Critically, the steel in this project was designed as **temporary scaffolding** to be removed once trees self-support. However, the structural concept demonstrates that a steel frame can support living tree walls during their multi-decade growth period. For a permanent steel skeleton approach, the same geometry would apply but the steel would remain indefinitely as the primary structure while the trees serve as infill rather than structural replacement.

Key dimensions and specifications: 10m height (cubic), London plane trees, 6 levels of planting, automated irrigation and nutrient supply, inosculation at connection points. As the trees mature (projected maturation by 2040), the trunks thicken, branches merge, and the living wall becomes denser and more effective as a building envelope [@mylo2023]. Ferdinand Ludwig estimates Platanus can reach 30m (100ft) height, making 20m domes biologically feasible.
