## Summary

The tallest living dome built to date is the Auerworldpalast (Germany, 1998), a circular willow dome 20m in diameter and 5.5m tall [@kalberer2025]. The tallest living structure overall is the Baubotanik Tower (Stuttgart, 2009), a 9m three-story tower made of white willows on steel scaffolding, not yet self-supporting [@mylo2023]. A 20m living dome requires roughly 4x the height of any existing living dome with no direct technical precedent.

The structural gap is fundamental. A 20m dome generates far greater compressive forces at its base than any living structure has demonstrated. Living root bridges achieve 30m horizontal spans but work purely in tension [@wikipediacontributors2025a]. Willow species used for domes lack long-term structural density for 20m compression loads — they favor speed over ultimate strength. Hardwood species offer greater density but grow slower, requiring decades to centuries to reach needed girth.

Centennial lifespan is achievable with appropriate species. The Khasi living root bridges of India last 500-600 years [@wikipediacontributors2025a], proving that well-maintained living structures can function for centuries.
