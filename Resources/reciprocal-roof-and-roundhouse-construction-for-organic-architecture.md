## Summary

The reciprocal roof frame is the key structural technique enabling realistic elven-style roundhouses. Dating to 12th-century Chinese and Japanese architecture and explored by Leonardo da Vinci, this self-supporting frame uses interlocking beams arranged in a circle where each beam rests on the next, creating a compression ring that requires no central support. A typical reciprocal roof uses 8-13 roundwood poles arranged in a spiral pattern, with each beam simultaneously supporting its neighbour and being supported by its predecessor. This allows spans of 5-9 metres for common roundhouses, with experienced builders achieving up to 20 metres in diameter. The frame can support a living green roof, creating the seamless earth-integrated appearance characteristic of elven architecture [@voiceofnature2022].

Construction requires careful geometry — the critical factors are timber form, size, species, and lap joint angles. Practitioners strongly recommend volunteering on an existing build or taking a course before attempting one independently. The roof is typically clad with hardwood floorboards as a ceiling substrate, followed by insulation, waterproof membrane, drainage layer, soil, and vegetation. Roundhouses pair well with wattle-and-daub, cob, straw bale, or cordwood walls, and can be built for a few thousand pounds over a few months by an owner-builder [@voiceofnature2022].

## Key Points
- Self-supporting frame eliminates central columns, creating open-plan interior up to 20m diameter
- Works with roundwood timber from locally sourced young trees or bamboo — no milling required
- Green roof supported directly, enabling full landscape integration and additional insulation
- Proven technology: Wholewoods (UK) has been building them for 20+ years for schools and public spaces
- Best learned hands-on — volunteer on a build before starting your own
