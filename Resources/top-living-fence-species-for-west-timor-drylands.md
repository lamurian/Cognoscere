West Timor (Nusa Tenggara Timur) has a semi-arid climate with only 3-4 wet months per year and rainfall under 1500mm annually [@ngongo2022]. Living fences in this region must tolerate extended dry seasons, poor soils, and intense sun. Research and field observation have identified approximately 50 species used for living fences across eastern Indonesia, with several top performers for dry conditions [@reville2017].

The most popular living fence tree in Eastern Indonesia is Lannea coromandelica (known locally as kayu jawa or kedondong jawa), planted as large cuttings that root within months. It tolerates drought, salt, and any soil type, and its leaves are used as goat fodder during the dry season. Other top species include Gliricidia sepium (gamal), Leucaena leucocephala (lamtoro), and Sesbania grandiflora (turi), all nitrogen-fixing legumes that improve soil fertility while serving as fence lines [@reville2017]. Scientific research in Kupang district confirmed that gamal performs best among these three for improving soil organic matter, water retention, and infiltration rate in dryland conditions [@matheus2009].

Additional drought-hardy species suitable for West Timor living fences include Moringa oleifera (kelor) planted by large cuttings with highly nutritious leaves, Pterocarpus indicus (angsana/sena) used in West Timor for its protein-rich goat fodder, Euphorbia tirucalli (patah tulang) forming impenetrable succulent hedges, Thevetia peruviana (yellow oleander) found in drier West Timor regions, Jatropha curcas (jarak) for fences and biodiesel, cassava (singkong) with interwoven stems, bougainvillea for thorny drought-tolerant barriers, and pomegranate (delima) thriving in dry tropical conditions [@reville2017]. These species can be planted as cuttings, require minimal water after establishment, and provide secondary benefits including fodder, firewood, food, and soil conservation.

Key sources: @reville2017, @matheus2009, @ngongo2022.
