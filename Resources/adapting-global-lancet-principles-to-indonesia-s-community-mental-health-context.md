## Summary

The Lancet paper by Patel et al. (2023) proposes five principles for transforming mental health systems globally: targeting harmful social environments, using staging models instead of categorical diagnosis, empowering front-line providers through task-sharing, embracing rights-based approaches, and centring lived experience [@patel2023]. Indonesia's context requires specific adaptations of each principle.

**Targeting social environments**: Indonesia's expert consensus identified five key drivers of mental health urgency, including multi-sectoral impacts, youth vulnerability, inadequate information, low prioritisation, and socioeconomic/cultural root causes. The first 1,000 days of life and adolescent mental health are priority areas. The PIS-PK (Healthy Indonesia through Family Approach) programme already uses family-based visits to assess 12 health indicators including mental health treatment, providing a mechanism for social environment targeting [@hidayat2023; @basrowi2024].

**Empowering front-line providers**: Task-sharing is essential given Indonesia's ratio of 0.3 psychiatrists per 100,000. Puskesmas nurses and community health cadres (kader) are the primary points of contact. However, the Munira et al. study found that general practitioners lack mental health literacy and may refuse referral letters. Training in the WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP) is recommended to address this gap [@munira2023].

**Rights-based approach**: Indonesia's Mental Health Act 2014 (Article 86) criminalised pasung, and the Law on Rights of Persons with Disabilities (No. 8, 2016) prohibited stigma and discrimination. However, implementation remains weak. The 2023 Health Law's merger of the Mental Health Act raises concerns about maintaining these protections [@hidayat2023].

## Key Points
- PIS-PK programme provides family-based mechanism for social environment targeting
- Task-sharing through Puskesmas nurses and kader is essential given workforce ratios
- mhGAP training needed for primary care workers to improve mental health literacy
- Rights-based protections exist in law but face implementation gaps
- Community Caucus Caring for Mental Health (2023) represents civil society mobilisation
- Expert consensus identified 5 recommendations: campaigns, research, curriculum, technology, first-aid

## Confidence: Moderate
Global principles from Patel et al. (2023) are well-established; Indonesia-specific adaptations are supported by policy analysis and expert consensus but lack RCT evidence.
