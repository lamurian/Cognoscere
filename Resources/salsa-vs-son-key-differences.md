Salsa and son are closely related but distinct. Son cubano is the 19th-century Cuban root genre — a specific musical form with defined subgenres (son montuno, son oriental). Salsa is a commercial label that emerged in 1960s-70s New York, primarily developed by Puerto Rican musicians, combining son with mambo, guaracha, and other Latin American styles. As the NPS explains, "son montuno is a type of Cuban song that preceded salsa music" [@alfonso2023].

The core rhythm is the same: salsa uses son clave almost exclusively (2-3 or 3-2 pattern) [@wikipediacontributors2025f]. Both share the verse-montuno section structure where call-and-response (coro-pregón) drives the energy. Instrumentation differs: son features the tres guitar prominently; salsa uses keyboard or piano for guajeos. Son historically uses smaller ensembles; salsa bands have fuller horn sections (trumpets, trombones, saxophones). Rhythmically, son emphasizes offbeats (beats 4 and 8), producing a "contra-tiempo" feel that dancers describe as elegant and smooth. Salsa emphasizes beats 1 and 5, feeling more energetic and aggressive [@flaishon2023].

Within Cuba, son evolved into timba (sometimes called "Cuban salsa") and songo — independently of New York salsa. Son was revived internationally by Buena Vista Social Club in the late 1990s. The essential distinction: son is a specific Cuban genre; salsa is a broader marketing category for son-derived dance music from multiple countries. Key difference summary: son cubano is a specific Cuban genre; salsa is a commercial category born in 1960s-70s New York. Both share son clave and the verse—montuno structure. Instrumentation differs (tres vs keyboard, smaller vs larger ensembles). Rhythmic emphasis differs (offbeats in son, downbeats in salsa) [@wikipediacontributors2025d; @flaishon2023].
