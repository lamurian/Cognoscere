Hydropower harnesses the gravitational potential or kinetic energy of falling or fast-running water to produce electricity. It generates about 15% of global electricity, with installed capacity reaching nearly 1,400 GW in 2021 -- the highest among all renewable technologies.

## Types of Hydropower

**Reservoir/Dam-Based**: Water stored in a reservoir is released on demand through turbines. The Three Gorges Dam (China, 22.5 GW) is the world's largest. Provides dispatchable power and flood control, but requires large land area and has significant environmental impact.

**Run-of-River**: Channels a portion of river flow through a turbine without significant storage. Lower environmental impact but depends on seasonal river flow. Better suited to rivers with consistent year-round flow.

**Pumped Storage**: Two reservoirs at different elevations. Water is pumped uphill during low demand and released to generate during peak demand. Round-trip efficiency of 75-85%. As of 2023, 181 GW installed globally -- the largest form of grid energy storage.

**Small/Micro/Pico Hydro**: Small-scale systems (micro: 5-100 kW, pico: under 5 kW) suitable for rural electrification in remote areas. Lower capital costs and minimal environmental footprint compared to large dams.

## Efficiency and Power

Power output depends on hydraulic head (height difference) and volumetric flow rate. The formula: Power = efficiency x density x flow x gravity x head. Francis turbines achieve up to 90% efficiency. Modern hydro plants operate at 85-95% efficiency, among the highest of all generation technologies.

## Environmental Considerations

Large dams can: (1) displace communities, (2) alter river ecosystems and fish migration, (3) trap sediment, (4) produce methane from submerged vegetation (though far less than fossil fuels). Run-of-river and small-scale hydro have significantly lower environmental impact.

## Cost and Capacity Factor

Hydropower has high upfront capital costs but very low operating costs (no fuel). Capacity factors range 30-80% depending on water availability. LCOE for new hydropower averages $40-100/MWh. Existing plants often produce the cheapest electricity due to fully amortised infrastructure.
