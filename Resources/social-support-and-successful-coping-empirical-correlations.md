## Summary

Social support and adaptive coping strategies show a small-to-moderate positive correlation, typically in the range r = 0.20 to 0.40. The relationship varies by type of support (family, friends, significant other) and coping strategy (problem-focused, emotion-focused, support-seeking).

Acoba [@acoba2024] reported that family support correlated with lower depression (r = -0.30) and higher positive affect (r = 0.25), while significant other support correlated with higher positive affect (r = 0.27). These effects were partially mediated by perceived stress, consistent with Lazarus and Folkman's stress-coping theory.

Schäfer et al. [@schfer2023] found the strongest edge in their resilience factor network was between coping using emotional support and perceived social support (partial r = 0.37), indicating that people who seek emotional support from others also perceive higher social support availability.

A meta-analysis by Prati and Pietrantoni (2009) found that social support and coping strategies both contributed to posttraumatic growth, with social support showing moderate effect sizes. The social support-coping link is bidirectional: support resources enable adaptive coping, and effective coping strengthens social networks.

In the French lockdown study [@pellerin2022], social support was indirectly associated with resilient trajectories through psychological flexibility, suggesting that support resources facilitate flexible coping adaptation.

## Key Points
- Coping via emotional support and perceived social support: partial r = 0.37 [@schfer2023]
- Family support with depression: r = -0.30 (small-to-moderate) [@acoba2024]
- Social support enables adaptive coping, especially emotional support-seeking
- The relationship is moderated by stressor type: active coping is less adaptive in uncontrollable situations
