## Summary

Indonesia's mental health policy has evolved through three historical eras, from a colonial-inherited biomedical model to an increasingly community-oriented framework. The current architecture rests on several key policies: Law No. 36 of 2009 (Health Law), Mental Health Act No. 18 of 2014 (now repealed into the 2023 Health Law), and Ministerial Decree No. 54 of 2017 on Stop Pasung. The 2009 Health Law explicitly mandated community-based mental health services as part of integrated care, shared responsibility across government, local government, and community, and mental health education and information rights [@hidayat2023].

However, implementation gaps are severe. Only 20% of Puskesmas (community health centres) provide mental health services, despite policy mandates. The WHO Mental Health Atlas 2020 reports that 66.1% of the mental health budget is directed to mental hospitals rather than community-based primary care. The expert consensus identified that mental health law implementation has been slow with significant gaps in enforcement, resources, and practical guidelines for integrating mental health with other services [@basrowi2024].

West Java was the first province to issue a Regional Regulation for Mental Health (Perda No. 5, 2018) with 88 articles covering preventive, promotive, curative, and rehabilitative services. However, even this progressive regulation requires further gubernatorial regulations to operationalise, and primary care clinics lack mental health expertise [@hidayat2023].

The decentralised system means local governments manage health service delivery, but this has led to service fragmentation. Puskesmas mental health nurses operate with limited resources, training, medications, and referral pathways. Psychiatric and psychological clinics are often inaccessible due to remoteness, limited hours, and absence of emergency services [@hidayat2023].

## Key Points
- 20% of Puskesmas provide mental health services despite policy mandates
- 66.1% of mental health budget goes to hospitals, not community care
- West Java's 2018 Perda requires further regulations to operationalise
- Decentralisation causes service fragmentation without coordination
- Puskesmas nurses lack training, medications, and referral pathways
- 2023 Health Law merged Mental Health Act, raising implementation concerns

Policy analysis of 18 documents (Hidayat et al. 2023) and WHO Atlas data (Basrowi et al. 2024).
