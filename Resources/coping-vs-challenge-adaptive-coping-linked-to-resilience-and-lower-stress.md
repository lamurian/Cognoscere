Adaptive coping strategies, particularly cognitive reappraisal and active coping, are positively associated with challenge appraisal and resilience and negatively associated with perceived stress.

Thomas and Zolkoski [@thomas2020] found Cognitive Reappraisal vs BRS r = 0.35 (p < .05) and Cognitive Reappraisal vs PSS-10 r = -0.25 (p < .05) in 277 undergraduates. Path analysis confirmed reappraisal as a significant predictor of resilience (beta = 0.40, p < .05), which in turn predicted lower stress. Chmitorz et al. [@chmitorz2018] reported that BRS positively correlated with active coping, positive reframing, acceptance, and humor.

Within the challenge-hindrance framework [@cavanaugh2000; @searle2015], people who use adaptive coping strategies are more likely to appraise stressors as challenges rather than hindrances. Challenge appraisal activates approach-oriented coping, which enhances resilience and reduces the negative impact of stress. Reappraisal reframes threat-based interpretations into growth-oriented ones, breaking the helplessness cycle.
