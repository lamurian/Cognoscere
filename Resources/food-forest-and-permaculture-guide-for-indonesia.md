## Overview

Building a food forest or permaculture system in Indonesia does not mean importing a Western model. The country already has living traditions of agroforestry, communal land management, and ecological farming. The most resilient approach reinforces these existing systems and builds forward from them.

## Cultural Foundations

### Gotong Royong (Mutual Cooperation)

The Javanese/Indonesian principle of collective cooperation is the social infrastructure for permaculture. Gotong royong manifests in neighbourhood associations (RT/RW), village work parties, and shared resource management. A food forest managed under gotong royong means shared planting days, rotating harvest duties, and collective maintenance of swales, composting, and nursery beds.[^1][^6]

### Subak (Bali's Water Temple System)

Bali's ancient subak system manages shared water across hundreds of farmers through democratic councils, spiritual ritual, and sophisticated hydrological engineering. Operating for over 1,000 years, subak offers a ready-made governance model for managing shared permaculture infrastructure — irrigation, seed banks, processing facilities.[^1]

### Pekarangan (Traditional Home Gardens)

The pekarangan is the Javanese and Sundanese traditional home garden — a layered system of fruit trees, vegetables, medicinal plants, and livestock managed by families on house-compound land. These are already small-scale food forests: canopy layer (coconut, mango, petai), understory (coffee, cacao, banana), shrub layer (cassava, taro, chili), ground cover (kangkung, sweet potato), and climbers (sirih, passionfruit). Modern permaculture design should study and improve existing pekarangan rather than replace them.[^1]

### Wanatani (Traditional Agroforestry)

Wanatani or wanafarma refers to Indonesia's traditional agroforestry systems, especially common in Sumatra (repong damar in Krui, talun in West Java) and Kalimantan. These complex systems integrate timber, fruit, resin, medicinal plants, and food crops in multi-story forest gardens that mimic natural forest succession. The damar resin agroforests of Lampung are a world-recognized example of sustainable indigenous land management.[^5]

### Adat (Customary Law and Ecological Governance)

Indonesia's diverse adat systems contain rules about forest stewardship, water rights, land use, and seasonal harvest. Many adat communities (Masyarakat Adat) have sophisticated ecological calendars based on flora/fauna indicators, lunar cycles, and monsoon patterns. Permaculture design should consult adat councils (Kerapatan Adat Nagari in West Sumatra, Desa Pekraman in Bali) before altering land or water systems.[^1][^6]

### Local Beliefs and Rituals

Traditional ceremonies like selamatan bumi (earth thanksgiving), seren taun (Sundanese harvest festival), and various wetonan (birthday-based planting calendars) embed ecological stewardship in cultural life. Participating in these rituals signals respect and builds the social trust needed for community-managed food forests.[^6]

## Practical Steps to Build a Food Forest in Indonesia

### Step 1: Observe the Local System

Before planting, study what already works in your area. Walk the local forest (hutan desa) and identify thriving species. In Indonesia's humid tropics, this means observing:

- Canopy species already dominant in your bioregion
- Natural succession patterns on disturbed land
- Where water flows during monsoon vs dry season
- What wild edibles locals already forage (sayur hutan)
- Existing pekarangan gardens and what families grow

### Step 2: Design with Water — Monsoon to Dry Season

Indonesia's challenge is not scarcity of water but its seasonality. Key techniques:

- **Swales on contour** to capture monsoon runoff and recharge groundwater, using the A-frame or water level.
- **Bioswales and infiltration ditches** to prevent erosion on steep slopes (common in Sulawesi, Java, Sumatra).
- **Embung** (village ponds) for dry-season irrigation, traditional across Nusa Tenggara.
- **Mulching heavily** with biomass (cocopeat, rice straw, banana trunks, leaf litter) to reduce evaporation during dry season.
- **Terasering** (terracing) with vetiver grass strips to stabilize slopes without heavy machinery.

### Step 3: Use Succession and Layering

Design for the seven layers of a tropical food forest:

1. Canopy (20m+): Coconut, durian, petai, jengkol, kemiri, sukun
2. Understory (5-15m): Mango, rambutan, duku, cempedak, nangka, sirsak
3. Shrub layer (1-5m): Coffee, cacao, pepper, banana, papaya, jambu biji, belimbing
4. Herbaceous layer: Taro (talas), cassava (singkong), galangal, kunyit, jahe, kencur, serai
5. Ground cover: Kangkung, ubi jalar, kacang tanah, kacang panjang
6. Root layer: Gembili, ganyong, uwi (various yams)
7. Climbers: Sirih, cengkeh, lada, passionfruit, ciplukan

### Step 4: Build Soil with Available Biomass

In the tropics, biomass decomposes fast. Feed the soil continuously:

- Compost from kitchen waste, livestock manure, and market waste
- Biochar from coconut shells and rice husks
- Green manure using legumes (lamtoro, kaliandra, gamal, mimba)
- Kascing (worm composting using Lumbricus rubellus)
- Bokashi using Effective Microorganisms (EM), widely available in Indonesia

### Step 5: Establish Guilds with Local Species

Indonesian tropical guilds can be built around keystone species:

- **Durian guild**: Durian canopy + coffee understory + kunyit ground cover + lamtoro N-fixer
- **Kelapa guild**: Coconut canopy + cacao understory + nanas ground cover + vanili climber
- **Petai/Jengkol guild**: Nitrogen-fixing canopy + pepper vine climbing trunk + talas shade floor

The N-fixing trees of Indonesia (petai, jengkol, lamtoro, kaliandra, gamal, sengon) are essential for building fertility without imported fertilizer.

### Step 6: Integrate Animals

Traditional Indonesian integrated farming (ternak-terpadu):

- **Ayam kampung** (free-range village chickens) for pest control, scratching, manure, and eggs
- **Bebek** (ducks) for snail control in wet areas and rice paddies
- **Kambing** (goats) for brush clearing and manure — staple across Indonesian villages
- **Ikan** (fish) in embung/kolam — tilapia (nila), catfish (lele), and gourami (gurame) integrated with garden irrigation
- **Lebah** (bees/stingless bees) — trigona (klanceng) honey cultivation in agroforestry systems

### Step 7: Engage the Community

A food forest managed individually fails in the long term. Follow the gotong royong model:

- Hold a musyawarah (community deliberation) before breaking ground
- Involve the lurah (village head), RT/RW, and tokoh masyarakat (community leaders)
- Hold gotong royong planting days with shared meals
- Run demplot (demonstration plots) to show results
- Teach through Sekolah Lapang (field school) methods — learning by doing
- Share hasil (yield) fairly: participants get first share of harvest

## Maintenance Practices

### Rainy Season (November-April)

- Focus on planting — trees establish best with consistent water
- Control gulma (weeds) — lamtoronisasi, legume ground cover, and heavy mulching
- Monitor erosion on swales; repair gabion and rock walls
- Harvest fast-growing annuals between tree rows

### Dry Season (May-October)

- Deep mulching with rice straw, cocopeat, or leaf litter
- Misting beds and shaded nursery for seedlings
- Pruning canopy trees to reduce evapotranspiration
- Harvest fruits, dry spices, process commodities
- Build and repair infrastructure — fences, terraces, composting

### Continuous

- Monitor soil health — test pH, organic matter, biological activity
- Rotate animal grazing zones
- Save seeds from best-performing plants
- Maintain social cohesion — regular gotong royong workdays

## Key Resources in Indonesia

### Books and Manuals

- **IDEP Permaculture Reference Book** — Published by IDEP Foundation (Bali, 2006). The most comprehensive Indonesian permaculture manual, available in Bahasa Indonesia. Covers integrated farming, soil management, water systems, and appropriate technology for the Indonesian context.[^2]
- **Facilitator's Handbook for Permaculture** — Developed specifically for Indonesian rural communities by foodforestdesignnetwork.com.[^3]
- **Buku Panduan Permakultur for Rural Indonesia** — Training manual used by Indonesian NGOs for village-level permaculture.

### Organisations and Places

- **IDEP Foundation** (Ubud, Bali) — Pioneering permaculture training and disaster response. Runs courses in Bahasa Indonesia. PO Box 160, Ubud 80571, Bali.[^2]
- **Mawun Valley Farm** (South Lombok) — Working permaculture farm with food forest, animal integration, and closed-loop systems. Offers stays and skill-sharing in a Sasak cultural context.[^4]
- **Willie Smits / Masarang Foundation** (Sulawesi) — Large-scale village-based agroforestry and reforestation. Proven model for integrating permaculture with local cooperatives.[^5]
- **Bumi Langit Institute** (Yogyakarta) — Permaculture learning centre focused on tropical organic farming.
- **Jiwa Garden** (Bali) — Permaculture garden and learning centre with food forest, composting, and workshops.
- **Permatil** (Timor Leste / East Nusa Tenggara) — Permaculture foundation working in dryland tropical conditions.

### Online Communities

- **Permies.com Indonesia forum** — Active discussion on permaculture projects across the archipelago.
- **Petaniku / Petani Muda** — Indonesian farmer networks sharing regenerative agriculture practices.

## Integration with Adat and Local Governance

- Consult the Kerapatan Adat Nagari (West Sumatra), Desa Pekraman (Bali), or similar traditional councils before any major land transformation.
- Respect tanah ulayat (customary land) rights — community land is not free for taking.
- Involve pemuda (youth groups) in the physical work — they have energy and will inherit the system.
- Share technology (biogas, solar drying, water pumps) with the community immediately, not after the system is built.
- Participate in selamatan and pengajian even if you are not Muslim/Balinese Hindu. Cultural participation builds trust faster than any technical demonstration.
