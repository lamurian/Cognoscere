## Summary

Indonesia faces a severe mental health treatment gap despite rising prevalence. The 2018 Riskesdas found depression prevalence at 6.1% among those aged 15+, with emotional and mental disorders increasing from 6% in 2013 to 9.8% in 2018, affecting over 19 million people. Crucially, 91% of individuals with mental disorders in Indonesia receive no treatment [@munira2023]. By 2023, estimates suggest approximately 20% of the Indonesian population experiences mental health challenges [@basrowi2024].

The economic burden is substantial. Mental health disorders are projected to cost Indonesia 21.9% of total GDP from 2012 to 2030, making mental health the second-largest contributor of non-communicable diseases after cardiovascular diseases [@basrowi2024]. Mental disorders ranked as the second highest cause of years lived with disability (YLDs) in Indonesia in 2019 at 1,304.36 YLDs per 100,000 population [@basrowi2024].

The WHO Mental Health Atlas 2020 country profile for Indonesia confirms these gaps. Indonesia has only 0.3 psychiatrists per 100,000 people, far below the global average. Seven of 34 provinces lack dedicated mental health hospitals. The health budget allocation to mental health remains approximately 2%, with 66.1% of that directed to mental hospitals rather than community-based services [@basrowi2024].

## Key Points
- 91% treatment gap: 91% of Indonesians with mental disorders receive no treatment
- Prevalence rising: 6% (2013) to 9.8% (2018) for emotional/mental disorders; ~20% by 2023
- Economic cost: projected 21.9% of GDP from 2012-2030 for mental health disorders
- Workforce shortage: 0.3 psychiatrists per 100,000 vs. global average of ~4
- Infrastructure gap: 7 of 34 provinces have no mental health hospital

## Confidence: High
Multiple peer-reviewed sources (Riskesdas 2018, WHO Atlas 2020, expert consensus 2024) converge on these figures.
