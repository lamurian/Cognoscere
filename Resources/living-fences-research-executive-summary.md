Willow is the superior choice for rapid, structurally complex woven living fences due to easy rooting from cuttings, fast growth (6 ft/year), and highly flexible branches. Willow fedges can be established in a single season, reaching full height in 2-3 years, though they require heavy maintenance (2-3 pruning sessions per year) and must be sited at least 33 feet from structures due to aggressive root systems [@keiren2023; @albert2024; @penn2024].

Crape myrtle offers different value: exceptional ornamental display with 100+ days of summer blooms, attractive peeling bark, and drought tolerance, but slower establishment and less flexibility for intricate weaving [@moore2006]. Chinese landscaping traditions have developed techniques for training crape myrtle over frames to create living furniture. The choice depends on priority: willow for fast structural complexity, crape myrtle for ornamental flowering living fences.

Confidence assessment: Willow as best for intricate woven structures is rated High (supported by multiple authoritative sources). Crape myrtle as ornamental flowering living fence is rated Moderate (fewer specialized sources, mainly general horticultural references). Willow requiring heavy maintenance and 33+ foot setback from structures is High. Crape myrtle needing pest management but less frequent pruning is High. Both creating intricate structures through different techniques is Moderate.
