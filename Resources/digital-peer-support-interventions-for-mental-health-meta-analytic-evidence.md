## Summary

Digital peer support interventions — where peers connect through apps, social media, or video platforms to share mental health experiences — produce large positive effects on mental health. Yeo et al. conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of 73 studies (118 effect sizes) on mental health and 47 studies (76 effect sizes) on physical health, finding a standardised mean difference of 0.53 (p < 0.001) for mental health outcomes and 0.35 for physical health [@yeo2025].

Informal, naturally occurring peer support (where individuals share similar health concerns without formal training) was more effective for mental health (SMD = 0.74) than formal peer support from trained peers (SMD = 0.37). Notably, informal digital peer support was comparable in effectiveness to professional online therapy. This suggests that the organic, stigma-reduced nature of peer interactions may facilitate self-disclosure and therapeutic alliance [@yeo2025].

Platform interactivity matters. Interventions delivered through apps, social networking sites, and video conferencing produced larger effects than forums, websites, or email-based support. Longer intervention durations paradoxically decreased effectiveness, possibly due to co-rumination or negative peer interactions. Effects were stronger in Eastern countries for mental health outcomes, suggesting cultural fit for collectivist contexts.

## Key Points

- Digital peer support shows large effects on mental health (SMD = 0.53), comparable to professional online therapy
- Informal, naturally occurring peer support outperforms formal trained peer support for mental health
- Interactive platforms (apps, social media, video) produce larger effects than static ones (forums, websites)
- Longer intervention duration is associated with reduced effectiveness
- Effects sustained for physical health but weakened for mental health over time
- Stronger mental health effects in Eastern vs. Western countries
