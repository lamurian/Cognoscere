## Summary

Aaron Antonovsky's Salutogenic Model, developed in the 1970s-80s, directly challenges the pathogenic paradigm that defines health as the absence of illness. Instead, it redefines health as a dynamic continuum between ease and dis-ease, emphasizing the human capacity to adapt, manage, and find meaning in life's challenges [@kenya2026]. This aligns precisely with the user's argument that health is defined by society's self-regulatory approach to eliminating illness.

The central mechanism is the Sense of Coherence (SOC), comprising three components: comprehensibility (cognitive clarity), manageability (confidence in available resources), and meaningfulness (motivational belief that challenges are worth engaging). Individuals with strong SOC demonstrate greater resilience, preventive behaviour, and adaptive capacity [@kenya2026].

Crucially, salutogenesis identifies Generalized Resistance Resources (GRRs) as the factors that enable health: social networks, spiritual faith, education, financial security, cultural stability, and autonomy. These are explicitly social and communal resources, not merely individual traits. The model recognizes health as a collective resource sustained by the interplay between individual agency, community support, and environmental conditions [@kenya2026].

Salutogenesis reconceptualizes prevention as health creation—not merely avoiding disease but actively building capability, coherence, and participation. This framework explains why removing an individual from their social context for isolated treatment is insufficient: health is a property of how people relate to their environment and community.

## Key Points

- Health is not the absence of illness but the capacity to adapt, find coherence, and use resources to manage life's unpredictability.
- SOC (Sense of Coherence) predicts resilience, preventive behaviour, and long-term well-being across diverse populations.
- GRRs (Generalized Resistance Resources) are social and communal—not merely individual—assets that sustain health.
- Salutogenesis shifts prevention from risk management to capacity building: from "fixing disease" to "building health."
- The model provides scientific grounding for the argument that health is a self-regulatory societal process, not an individual absence-of-disease state.
