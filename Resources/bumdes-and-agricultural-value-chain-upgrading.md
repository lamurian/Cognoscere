## The Core Problem

Indonesian villages are dominated by low-value subsistence agriculture. Farmers sell raw commodities (coconuts, coffee, cocoa, spices) to middlemen at thin margins. Most value is captured downstream -- processing, branding, and retail happen in cities or abroad. Modernization means capturing that value at the village level.

## BUMDes: Village-Owned Enterprises

BUMDes (Badan Usaha Milik Desa) are collectively-owned enterprises established under the Village Law. By 2020, 39,844 BUMDes had been created. They serve as the institutional vehicle for:
- Collective processing facilities (drying, fermenting, grading, packaging)
- Joint marketing and bulk purchasing
- E-commerce operations
- Waste management and recycling
- Village tourism management
- Microcredit and savings cooperatives

When functioning well, BUMDes increase farmer bargaining power, reduce middleman margins, and retain economic value within the village economy.

## Value Chain Interventions

The biggest GDP gains come from processing at source:

- **Coffee** -- Selling green beans vs. roasted ground coffee: 3-5x price uplift
- **Coconut** -- Selling copra vs. virgin coconut oil, coconut flour, or craft products: 4-6x uplift
- **Cocoa** -- Selling wet beans vs. fermented dried beans vs. chocolate bars: 10x+ uplift at retail
- **Spices** -- Selling fresh vs. dried, ground, and branded: 2-3x uplift
- **Cassava** -- Selling raw tubers vs. cassava flour, tapioca starch, or snacks: 3-4x uplift

## Digital Market Access

The traditional rural economy is trapped by geography. Digital platforms break this:
- dapurkita.com in Ende, East Nusa Tenggara connects farmers to consumers via WhatsApp ordering, with BUMDes handling delivery
- Integration with national e-commerce platforms (Tokopedia, Shopee) for wider market reach
- E-aggregators that pool supply from multiple villages
- Fintech solutions for smallholder credit that bypass informal lenders charging high interest

## What Makes BUMDes Work vs. Fail

Historical lessons from the KUD (Koperasi Unit Desa) system of the 1970s-90s, which largely failed:
- KUDs failed because they were imposed uniformly from above and used as government tools rather than member-driven organizations
- They focused exclusively on on-farm activities with limited value addition
- They relied on government support rather than building self-sufficiency

**Successful BUMDes today share:** genuine community ownership, professional management (not political appointees), transparent accounting, digital integration, and linkages to real markets.
