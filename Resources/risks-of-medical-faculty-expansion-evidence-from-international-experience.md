## Summary

Indonesia's strategy of opening 30 new medical faculties and 148 new study programs to address doctor shortages follows a path that several countries have taken with mixed results. International experience provides cautionary lessons about the risks of supply-side expansion without addressing distribution, quality, and retention.

**Quantity-quality tradeoffs**: OECD countries that rapidly expanded medical school capacity often faced declining clinical training quality because new schools lacked sufficient teaching hospitals and clinical faculty [@oecd2016]. Indonesia produces only 12,000 GPs per year from existing faculties, and rapidly adding 30 new ones risks saturating limited clinical placement sites, reducing hands-on training quality. Prabowo himself acknowledged that even with 30 new faculties, it would take 35 more years to reach the ideal doctor-to-population ratio [@antaranews2025].

**Distribution persistence**: The OECD has documented that increasing the overall number of doctors does not solve geographic maldistribution. Doctors continue to cluster in urban areas unless specific rural retention policies are implemented [@oecd2016]. Indonesia's 59% specialist concentration on Java demonstrates this pattern. Muharram et al. (2024) found that while between-province specialist inequality has decreased (Gini from 0.57 in 1993 to 0.44 in 2022), within-district inequality remains high at 0.53 [@muharram2024].

**Oversupply without access**: Japan and Korea expanded medical school capacity and now have among the highest doctor-to-population ratios in the OECD, yet rural areas still face shortages and some specialties experience oversupply. Spain and Italy faced similar patterns — increased production without proportional improvement in access equity. The OECD Health Workforce Policies report emphasizes that supply-side policies alone cannot solve distribution problems [@oecd2016].

**Brain drain risk**: Without adequate retention policies, newly trained doctors may migrate to better-compensated positions in urban areas or abroad. Indonesia is already experiencing internal brain drain, with specialists concentrated in urban Java where private practice opportunities are more lucrative [@antaranews2025]. The bonded scholarship approach (requiring return to home region) attempts to mitigate this but its effectiveness depends on monitoring and enforcement capacity.

## Key Points

- Rapid medical school expansion risks declining clinical training quality due to limited teaching hospital capacity
- Increasing doctor numbers alone does not solve geographic maldistribution — rural retention requires separate policies
- Countries like Japan, Korea, Spain, Italy show that more graduates does not automatically mean better access equity
- Internal brain drain (urban concentration) is a significant risk without bonded deployment mechanisms
- Even with 30 new faculties, Indonesia would need 35 years to reach ideal doctor ratio

## Confidence: Moderate (supported by OECD analysis and comparative country case studies)
