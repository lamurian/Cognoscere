## Summary

Three primary methods exist for integrating living plants as infill into a permanent steel frame skeleton, each suited to different species and timelines.

**Method 1: Woven rod infill.** Willow or crape myrtle rods are planted at the base of the steel frame and woven through the stud cavities in a diamond lattice (45-degree angle, 10in spacing). The steel frame provides immediate structural support; the living weave fills in over 2-3 growing seasons [@morningchores2025]. Willow reaches full density fastest (1m/year growth) but has shorter lifespan. Crape myrtle grows slower but produces denser, longer-lived wood [@reville2017].

**Method 2: Pleached branch infill.** Trees planted at 1.5-2m intervals along the steel frame have their branches trained horizontally and interwoven through the frame's bays. Pleached hornbeam, lime, or plane trees create a living lattice that fills the wall plane. The technique has 300+ years of European precedent for creating raised hedges and living walls [@wikipediacontributors2025b].

**Method 3: Inosculated panel infill.** Pre-grown panels (like ArborStructures' 2x24x96in molded panels) are assembled within the steel frame, their trunks inosculated to neighbors. Unlike ArborStructures' approach where panels are structural, here they are non-load-bearing infill within a steel frame. The steel carries all structural loads; the living panels provide insulation, shading, and aesthetics. Total timeline: 4 years nursery + 1 season on-site.

All methods require a waterproof membrane between steel and living layer to prevent corrosion, and an irrigation system during establishment.
