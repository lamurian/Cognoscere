## Summary

The Floating Raft System (FRS) in IMTA is a structural platform that suspends cultivation lines for seaweeds and bivalves in the water column, positioned to intercept nutrient plumes from fed aquaculture cages. The University of New Hampshire offshore IMTA raft (deployed 2015) integrated steelhead trout (2 tons/yr), blue mussels (6 tons/yr), and sugar kelp (1 ton/yr), demonstrating four-season multi-trophic production [@buck2018].

Species selection criteria: (1) trophic compatibility — extractive species must match waste type from fed species; (2) hydrodynamic tolerance — seaweeds like Saccharina latissima develop streamlined morphology in high-energy conditions; (3) market value — each IMTA component must be commercially viable [@buck2018]. Key extractive genera: Gracilaria, Saccharina, Ulva for seaweeds; Mytilus, Crassostrea for bivalves; Holothuria, Parastichopus for sea cucumbers.

Spatial arrangement is critical. Extractive species should be positioned within the nutrient plume but at appropriate distances to avoid entanglement and optimize uptake. In the offshore wind farm multi-use concept, fish cages form a central ring, with bivalves encircling them as a first defense line and seaweeds as an outer ring [@buck2018]. Nutrient uptake ratios guide sizing: kelp-to-fish biomass ratios of 6.7:1 (N), 4.8:1 (P), 5.8:1 (C) for Alaria esculenta, and 12.9:1 (N), 10.5:1 (P), 10.2:1 (C) for Saccharina latissima [@buck2018].
