## Executive Summary

Organisation-based interventions to improve psychological resilience can be cost-effective, with workplace mental health promotion programmes consistently showing returns of $1.50 to $7 per dollar invested. The strongest evidence supports targeted (indicated) approaches that screen for at-risk employees and deliver psychological interventions, group-based educational workshops combining resilience skill-building with mindfulness or CBT techniques, and blended delivery models that pair digital self-guided content with periodic in-person sessions.

Cost-effectiveness analysis (CEA) defines 'value' as the ratio of incremental costs to incremental health gains, measured as natural units (e.g., depression cases averted), QALYs, or monetary equivalents. The dominant frameworks are CUA (cost per QALY gained, with thresholds of $50,000/QALY in the US or £20,000–30,000/QALY in the UK) and ROI (financial returns per dollar invested). Monetising wellbeing outcomes relies on willingness-to-pay surveys, the human capital approach (valuing productivity at wage rates), or the friction cost approach. The choice of valuation method substantially affects whether an intervention appears cost-saving.

## Known Gaps

- Few economic evaluations directly assess resilience as the primary outcome; most proxy it through clinical or productivity measures. This creates a disconnect between the intervention goal (resilience) and the outcome that is valued.
- The socioecological approach—combining individual resilience training with organisational-level changes—is under-studied in economic terms despite theoretical promise.
- Cost-effectiveness evidence from low- and middle-income countries is virtually absent; most studies come from high-income Western contexts.
- The appropriate willingness-to-pay threshold for employer-based resilience decisions (as opposed to healthcare decisions) has not been established.
- No standardised method exists for assigning monetary values to resilience score improvements on validated scales.
