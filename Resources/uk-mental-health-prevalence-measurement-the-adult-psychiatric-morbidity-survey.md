The Adult Psychiatric Morbidity Survey (APMS) is the UK's primary source of national prevalence statistics for mental health conditions among adults aged 16+ in private households in England. Conducted every seven years since 1993, it is commissioned by the Department of Health and run by NatCen and the University of Leicester for NHS Digital. The survey samples approximately 8,000 households using a two-phase design: phase one screens for common mental disorders, psychotic disorders, personality disorders, substance use, PTSD, suicidal thoughts, self-harm, and autism spectrum disorders; phase two conducts in-depth clinical interviews with a subsample triggered by phase one responses [@frenisterrantino2022].

Core disorders measured include common mental disorders (anxiety and depression), psychotic disorders, antisocial and borderline personality disorder, ADHD, alcohol and drug dependence, PTSD, and suicidal thoughts. The headline statistic from APMS 2007 was that 23.0% of adults in England had at least one psychiatric disorder. The survey excludes homeless populations and those in institutions, likely undercounting the most severe conditions. Unlike the England Health Index which produces composite scores, APMS provides disaggregated prevalence rates per disorder without aggregating into a single mental health index. APMS is designated as National Statistics and datasets are available via the UK Data Service [@frenisterrantino2022].
