The PSS-10 shows a small-to-moderate positive correlation with objective stress exposure, typically measured using stressful life events checklists such as the College Student Life Events Schedule or the Holmes-Rahe Social Readjustment Scale.

Cohen, Kamarck, and Mermelstein [@cohen1983] found PSS-14 correlated with the number of life events at r = 0.20 (college students, n = 332), r = 0.24 (second college sample, n = 114), and r = 0.39 (community smoking-cessation sample, n = 64). Cohen and Williamson [@cohen1988] reported r approx 0.22 between PSS-10 and life event counts in a US national probability sample.

The modest effect size (r approx 0.20-0.40) is theoretically important. Perceived stress is not reducible to the count of objective stressors. The PSS-10 captures the subjective appraisal of whether events feel overwhelming, which depends on coping resources, personality, and context. This aligns with Lazarus and Folkman's transactional model, where stress arises from the person-environment transaction, not from the stimulus alone.
