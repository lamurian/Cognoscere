The Perceived Stress Scale-10 (PSS-10) shows a small-to-moderate positive correlation with objective stress exposure, typically measured via stressful life events checklists (e.g., College Student Life Events Schedule, Holmes-Rahe Social Readjustment Scale).

In the original PSS validation across three samples, Cohen, Kamarck, and Mermelstein (1983) found PSS-14 correlated with the number of life events at r = 0.20 (college students, n = 332), r = 0.24 (second college sample, n = 114), and r = 0.39 (community smoking-cessation sample, n = 64). Cohen and Williamson (1988) reported a correlation of r ≈ 0.22 between PSS-10 and life event counts in a US national probability sample.

The modest effect size (r ≈ 0.20–0.40) is theoretically meaningful: it confirms that perceived stress is not reducible to the mere count of objective stressors. The PSS-10 captures the subjective appraisal of whether events feel overwhelming, which depends on coping resources, personality, and context — not just whether events occurred. This is consistent with Lazarus and Folkman's transactional model, where stress arises from the person-environment transaction, not from the stimulus alone.
