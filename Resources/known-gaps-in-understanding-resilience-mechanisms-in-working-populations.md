Several important gaps remain in the scientific understanding of psychological resilience mechanisms in working-age populations.

First, most studies rely on self-report measures of resilience and appraisal style, which may not capture the automatic, implicit nature of these processes. Objective behavioural and neuroimaging measures are needed to validate self-report findings.

Second, there is limited longitudinal evidence tracking how resilience mechanisms change across the working lifespan (ages 25-65). Most studies span months, not decades. It remains unclear whether the same mechanisms operate at different career stages and whether cumulative stress exposure alters their effectiveness.

Third, the PASTOR theory [@kalisch2015] has been critiqued for potentially overemphasising positive appraisal. Freund and Staudinger [@freund2015] argue that negative appraisals can serve adaptive functions — the key may be accurate appraisal rather than uniformly positive appraisal. More research is needed on context-dependency and flexibility in appraisal.

Fourth, the specificity of intervention mechanisms remains unclear. While optimism and self-compassion mediated effects in the RESIST trial [@hannibal2026], it is unknown whether different interventions target different mechanisms or whether common pathways exist.

Fifth, organisational-level factors (leadership quality, organisational culture, job design) are understudied as modulators of individual resilience mechanisms. The JD-R model suggests organisational factors may be as important as individual factors, but direct evidence linking organisational interventions to neurobiological mechanisms is lacking.
