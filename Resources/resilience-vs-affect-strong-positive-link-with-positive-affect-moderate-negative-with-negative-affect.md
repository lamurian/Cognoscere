Resilience shows a robust differentiated pattern with affective states: a strong positive association with positive affect and a moderate-to-strong negative association with negative affect.

Montero-Marin et al. (2015) found using SEM with 622 Spanish primary care physicians: CD-RISC vs Positive Affect γ = 0.70 (strong positive) and CD-RISC vs Negative Affect γ = -0.35 (moderate negative). This demonstrates that resilience is more strongly tied to the presence of positive affect than to the absence of negative affect — a distinction with clinical implications.

Bajaj and Pande (2016) found that resilience mediated the relationship between mindfulness and life satisfaction and affect. The broaden-and-build theory of positive emotions [@fredrickson2001] explains this: positive emotions broaden cognitive and behavioral repertoires, building enduring resources that comprise resilience. Conversely, low-resilience individuals experience more negative affect because they lack the psychological resources to regulate distress effectively.
