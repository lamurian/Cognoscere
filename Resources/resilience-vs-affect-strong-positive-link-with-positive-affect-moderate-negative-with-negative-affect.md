Resilience shows a robust differentiated pattern with affective states. It has a strong positive association with positive affect and a moderate-to-strong negative association with negative affect.

Montero-Marin et al. [@monteromarin2015] used SEM with 622 Spanish primary care physicians and found CD-RISC vs Positive Affect gamma = 0.70 (strong positive) and CD-RISC vs Negative Affect gamma = -0.35 (moderate negative). This shows that resilience is more strongly tied to the presence of positive affect than to the absence of negative affect, a distinction with clinical implications.

Bajaj and Pande [@bajaj2016] found that resilience mediated the relationship between mindfulness and life satisfaction and affect. The broaden-and-build theory of positive emotions [@fredrickson2001] explains this pattern. Positive emotions broaden cognitive and behavioral repertoires, building enduring resources that comprise resilience. People with low resilience experience more negative affect because they lack the psychological resources to regulate distress effectively.
