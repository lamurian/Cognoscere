## Summary

OECD countries have developed a range of policies to address health workforce shortages and maldistribution that offer relevant lessons for Indonesia. The OECD has identified four categories of policy levers for influencing doctor distribution: financial incentives, education pipeline management, practice regulation, and service delivery redesign [@oecd2016].

**Numerus clausus and training capacity management**: Many OECD countries regulate medical school admissions through numerus clausus systems that align training numbers with projected workforce needs. However, large cyclical variations in medical school quotas have historically caused problems — periods of overproduction that led to unemployment followed by austerity-driven cuts that exacerbated shortages [@oecd2016]. Finland uses a centrally planned numerus clausus system with medical schools producing approximately 574 graduates annually (10.4 per 100,000), below the EU average, relying instead on high nurse density and interprofessional task-sharing to meet population needs [@schneider2022a].

**Rural distribution policies**: The OECD recommends several strategies for improving geographic distribution: 1) financial incentives (rural practice bonuses, loan repayment, higher salaries in underserved areas); 2) admission of students from rural backgrounds to medical schools; 3) decentralised medical education with clinical placements in rural areas; 4) regulatory measures requiring service in underserved areas after graduation; and 5) improved working conditions and professional support in rural settings [@oecd2016].

**Task-sharing and skill-mix optimisation**: Nordic countries achieve strong health outcomes with doctor densities at or below the OECD average by maximising the role of nurses and other non-physician clinicians. Finland has 14.3 nurses per 1,000 population (vs EU average 8.9), with a nurse-to-doctor ratio of 4.3 [@schneider2022a]. Expanding the scope of practice for nurses, midwives, and community health workers can extend the reach of the health system without requiring proportional increases in physician numbers.

**Integrated health labour market analysis**: The WHO-supported Health Labour Market Analysis (HLMA) approach that Indonesia is now implementing examines the entire healthcare labour market comprehensively — how workers are trained, employed, distributed, and retained [@worldhealthorganizationindonesia2026]. This approach was recommended by the OECD as best practice [@oecd2016], as it identifies bottlenecks across the entire pipeline rather than focusing solely on training expansion.

**Lessons for Indonesia**: Indonesia's strategy of opening 30 new medical faculties should be complemented with: strengthened primary care and community health worker deployment; bonded scholarship programs with robust enforcement; financial incentives for rural practice (higher pay, housing, professional development); and investment in clinical training infrastructure (teaching hospitals, supervision capacity). Without these complementary policies, increased graduate numbers alone will not solve distribution or quality challenges.

## Key Points

- Numerus clausus management should avoid cyclical overproduction-underproduction patterns
- Rural doctor distribution requires 5 complementary strategies beyond training alone
- Nordic task-sharing models (high nurse-to-doctor ratios) extend system reach without proportional physician increase
- HLMA approach provides comprehensive workforce pipeline analysis — Indonesia has begun implementation
- Training expansion must be complemented with distribution, retention, and quality policies
