## Summary

The correlation between perceived social support and coping success or coping effectiveness is positive and small-to-moderate. Reported values range from r ≈ 0.20 to r ≈ 0.40, depending on how coping success is operationalized (coping self-efficacy, adaptive coping style use, or perceived coping effectiveness).

This association is theoretically grounded in Lazarus and Folkman's transactional stress model, where social support functions as a key coping resource that enhances an individual's perceived ability to handle stressors. A meta-analytic review of the coping literature suggests that perceived social support correlates with problem-focused coping at approximately r = 0.25-0.35, with emotion-focused coping at lower magnitudes.

Importantly, the social support ↔ coping success correlation is vulnerable to measurement overlap. When both are measured as general perceived ability (rather than situation-specific appraisals), shared method variance inflates the correlation. Situation-specific measures show lower associations (r ≈ 0.15-0.25).
