## Summary

Indonesia systematically introduced the WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP) to its network of approximately 10,000 primary care clinics (Puskesmas) starting in 2015 [@anjara2019]. The mhGAP is a standardized intervention Guide that provides algorithmic, protocol-driven care for mental, neurological, and substance use disorders, designed for non-specialist health workers in resource-limited settings. This represents a population-level approach where a uniform protocol is rolled out across diverse communities with minimal adaptation for individual root causes.

A pragmatic cluster trial comparing WHO mhGAP-trained GPs against co-located clinical psychologists in Yogyakarta found that GPs were non-inferior to psychologists for mild-to-moderate disorders at six months [@anjara2019]. However, the study revealed several indicators of blanket treatment patterns: GPs' choice of treatment was not recorded or enforced; psychotropic medication availability varied across clinics; and treatment adherence was not monitored. The study explicitly noted that psychologists 'seldom develop treatment plans alongside their patients,' suggesting limited individualized care even in the specialist arm. Furthermore, the treatment gap in remote parts of Indonesia was 96.5% [@anjara2019].

The mhGAP framework itself, while evidence-based, is inherently a standardized algorithm. It prioritizes scalability and access over individualized diagnostic formulation. Critics argue that such standardized protocols risk 'medicalizing social suffering' and overlooking the social determinants of mental distress [@osborne2026]. Indonesia's 66.1% mental health budget allocation to hospitals rather than community-based care compounds this problem by favouring biomedical over psychosocial approaches.

## Key Points

- WHO mhGAP introduced to ~10,000 Indonesian Puskesmas as a standardized add-on training for GP-nurse pairs from 2015
- The protocol-driven approach prioritizes scalability over individualized root cause diagnosis
- Treatment adherence was not monitored; GPs' treatment choices were not recorded in the trial
- Psychologists in the co-location model 'seldom develop treatment plans' with patients
- Remote-area treatment gap was 96.5%, indicating the scale of the problem these standardized approaches aim to address
- Critics argue standardized biomedical protocols may medicalize social suffering and neglect cultural context [@osborne2026]
