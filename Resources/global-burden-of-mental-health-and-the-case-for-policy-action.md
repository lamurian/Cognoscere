Mental disorders now affect nearly one in eight people worldwide, making them one of the leading causes of disability globally. Mental disorders account for over 400 million Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) per year, representing approximately 16% of all DALYs globally [@abdulla2025]. In South Africa, mental health conditions are the leading cause of DALYs at 13.8% of disease burden, exceeding HIV (11.8%) and musculoskeletal disorders (10.4%) [@southafricadepartmentofhealth2023].

Despite this burden, a vast treatment gap persists. Up to 75% of people with mental disorders in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) receive no treatment due to stigma, underfunding, workforce shortages, and fragmented health systems [@abdulla2025]. In some countries, up to 90% of people with severe mental health conditions receive no care at all [@worldhealthorganization2025]. Treatment rates are alarmingly low: only 11.1% of severe cases in China and 10.4% in Nigeria had received any treatment in the previous 12 months [@rathod2017].

The economic burden of mental ill-health is enormous and growing. Projected costs are estimated to exceed US$6 trillion by 2030 globally, driven by lost productivity, healthcare expenditure, and social welfare costs [@abdulla2025]. In South Africa, depression and anxiety alone cost the economy more than US$3.6 billion (R61.2 billion) in lost earnings, with lifetime costs of perinatal depression and anxiety amounting to US$2.8 billion per annual birth cohort [@southafricadepartmentofhealth2023]. Crucially, the cost of inaction far exceeds the cost of scaling up evidence-based interventions: investment cases demonstrate significant return on investment for mental health care [@patel2018].
