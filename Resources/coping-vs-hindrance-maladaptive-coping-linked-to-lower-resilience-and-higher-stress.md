Maladaptive coping strategies are associated with hindrance appraisals, lower resilience, and higher psychological distress.

Chmitorz et al. [@chmitorz2018] found in a population-based German sample (n = 1,481) that the BRS was negatively correlated with denial, venting, substance use, religion, and self-blame. Smith et al. [@smith2008] originally found BRS negatively correlated with behavioral disengagement and self-blame. Thomas and Zolkoski [@thomas2020] found that suppression showed no significant correlation with resilience (r = 0.10, n.s.) or perceived stress (r = 0.02, n.s.), which means not all maladaptive strategies are equally impactful.

Within the challenge-hindrance framework, hindrance appraisal occurs when a stressor is perceived as obstructing goal attainment with no compensating benefit. This appraisal triggers avoidance-oriented coping such as denial, venting, and substance use. These strategies deplete resources and reinforce the sense of helplessness. The negative correlations between resilience and maladaptive coping are small-to-moderate (r range approx -0.15 to -0.30), suggesting that people with low resilience default to avoidance strategies.
