Maladaptive coping strategies are associated with hindrance appraisals, lower resilience, and higher psychological distress.

Chmitorz et al. (2018) found in a large population-based German sample (n = 1,481) that the BRS was negatively correlated with the coping strategies of denial, venting, substance use, religion, and self-blame. Smith et al. (2008) originally demonstrated that BRS negatively correlated with behavioral disengagement and self-blame. Thomas and Zolkoski (2020) found that the suppression emotion regulation strategy showed no significant correlation with resilience (r = 0.10, n.s.) or perceived stress (r = 0.02, n.s.), indicating not all maladaptive strategies are equally impactful.

Within the challenge-hindrance framework, hindrance appraisal occurs when a stressor is perceived as obstructing goal attainment with no compensating benefit. This appraisal triggers avoidance-oriented coping (denial, venting, substance use), which depletes resources and reinforces the sense of helplessness. The negative correlations between resilience and maladaptive coping (small-to-moderate, r range -0.15 to -0.30) suggest that low resilience individuals default to avoidance strategies.
