The correlation between PSS-10 and general self-efficacy (GSE) is consistently negative but varies substantially by population. Higgins [@higgins2016] reported a strong Pearson r = -0.57 (p < 0.01, n = 813) in a mixed Irish sample, with self-efficacy as the strongest predictor (β = -0.39) in regression. Bodys-Cupak et al. [@bodyscupak2022] found a weaker Spearman rho = -0.167 (p = 0.0034, n = 307) in Polish nursing students, where self-efficacy became non-significant when optimism and life satisfaction were included.

The wide range (|r| ≈ 0.17-0.57) suggests population heterogeneity moderates the effect. General population samples show larger effects, occupation-restricted samples show smaller ones. Self-efficacy consistently predicts lower perceived stress, but its unique contribution depends on which other resources are controlled. Sources: @higgins2016, @bodyscupak2022.
