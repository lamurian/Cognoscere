Positive affect is positively associated with perceived social support across different support sources, while negative affect is negatively associated.

Acoba (2024) found in a Filipino adult sample (n = 426): Family Support vs Positive Affect r = 0.249 (p < .01); Friend Support vs Positive Affect r = 0.201 (p < .01); Significant Other Support vs Positive Affect r = 0.273 (p < .01). Social support also negatively predicted anxiety (r = -0.12 to -0.22) and depression (r = -0.18 to -0.30).

From the broaden-and-build perspective, positive affect expands attention and builds social bonds, which in turn attract more social support — a reciprocal upward spiral. Conversely, the stress-buffering model [@cohen1985] posits that perceived availability of support resources directly enhances positive affect by providing a sense of security and belonging.

The effect sizes are small-to-moderate (r ≈ 0.20-0.27), indicating that social resources are one contributor to positive affect among many, including personality, activity levels, and health status.
