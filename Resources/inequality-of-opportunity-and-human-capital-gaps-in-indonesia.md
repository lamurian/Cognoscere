At least one-third of Indonesia's consumption inequality stems from birth circumstances — parents' education and region — that individuals cannot control [@worldbank2016]. Childhood stunting (37%) is the critical driver, far exceeding Vietnam (23%) and Thailand (16%). Stunted children develop slower cognitively, reach lower education levels, and earn less as adults. Poorer children receive less prenatal care and lack access to clean water [@worldbank2016].

Education quality, not enrollment, is the binding constraint. While primary enrollment is near-universal, 74% of Indonesian 15-year-olds fail to achieve basic Level 2 skills on PISA math and science tests — the 5th worst of 82 countries [@worldbank2016]. Rural schools lack trained teachers; Grade 3 children in Java read 26 words/minute faster than eastern Indonesia.

Family planning decline reversed a positive trend. Poorer households' shrinking family sizes reduced the Gini by 2.5 points in the 1990s, but this reversed after 2000. If 1990s trends had continued, the 2014 Gini would be 37 rather than 41 [@worldbank2016].
