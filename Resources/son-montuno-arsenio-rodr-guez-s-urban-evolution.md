Son montuno is a subgenre of son cubano developed by blind tresero and bandleader Arsenio Rodríguez in the 1940s. The term "montuno" originally meant "from the mountains," but Arsenio repurposed it to denote a sophisticated, urban approach to son. His innovations made son montuno the direct template for salsa, timba, and songo [@wikipediacontributors2025e].

Arsenio expanded the septeto into the conjunto format by adding piano, congas, a second and third trumpet, and a cowbell for the bongo player during the montuno section. He introduced layered guajeos (interlocking ostinato melodies) where multiple instruments play contrapuntal riffs simultaneously. His piano guajeos departed from the vocal melody to create independent contrapuntal material, and he developed clave-specific bass tumbaos that gave each composition a unique bass melodic identity [@wikipediacontributors2025e].

The montuno section itself — the climactic call-and-response (coro-pregón) passage where the sonero improvises over a repeating harmonic vamp — became the defining feature. It carries the highest energy, with horn arrangements (moñas), piano solos, and gradual tempo acceleration. Beny Moré further evolved the genre by incorporating guaracha, bolero, and mambo. Key recordings: "Dile a Catalina" (1942), "Dame un cachito pa' huelé" (1946), and "Santa Isabel de las Lajas" (1955). Key artists: Arsenio Rodríguez, Beny Moré, Celia Cruz, Conjunto Casino [@wikipediacontributors2025e; @alfonso2023].
