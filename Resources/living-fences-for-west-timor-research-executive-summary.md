This research examined plant species suitable for living fences in West Timor's semi-arid climate, drawing on field observations across eastern Indonesia, scientific hedgerow research conducted in Kupang, West Timor, and documentation of local Timorese farming practices. The top recommended species are Lannea coromandelica (easiest propagation, most drought-tolerant), Gliricidia sepium (best for soil conservation and fodder), and Leucaena leucocephala (deep-rooted, high-protein fodder, culturally established in West Timor) [@reville2017; @matheus2009; @ngongo2022].

Scientific evidence from Kupang confirms that hedgerow systems with legume trees significantly improve soil organic matter, water retention, and infiltration rates in dryland conditions, with Gliricidia outperforming Leucaena and Sesbania in soil physical property improvement [@matheus2009]. All three produce biomass that can be used as mulch and livestock fodder, creating integrated crop-livestock-conservation systems. Leucaena leucocephala cv. Tarramba (psyllid-resistant) has been successfully adopted by West Timor farmers, particularly in the Amarasi region, for integrated cattle feeding and farming systems [@ngongo2022].

Confidence assessment: Lannea coromandelica as top living fence species for Eastern Indonesia is rated Moderate (single detailed field source @reville2017, but extremely consistent across locations). Gliricidia and Leucaena for hedgerow soil conservation is High (supported by peer-reviewed research in Kupang @matheus2009 and extensive documentation @ngongo2022). Multiple benefits of living fences (erosion control, fodder, biodiversity) is High (consistent across all sources). The traditional Mamar and hedgerow systems being indigenous to West Timor is High (peer-reviewed evidence @ngongo2022).
