## Summary

The Perceived Stress Scale-10 (PSS-10) and resilience (typically measured by the Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale, CD-RISC-10) show a moderate-to-strong negative correlation. The most commonly cited range is r = -0.40 to -0.50.

Wollny and Jacobs [@wollny2021] reported r = -0.49 between the CD-RISC-10 and PSS-4 in a German student sample (N = 360), indicating that higher trait resilience is associated with lower perceived stress. The CD-RISC manual reports a broader range of r = -0.40 to -0.76 across various validation samples [@wollny2021].

This negative correlation is consistent across populations. It reflects the conceptual overlap: resilience is defined as the capacity to cope with stress and adversity, so individuals with higher resilience appraise potentially stressful situations as less threatening and report lower perceived stress.

## Key Points
- PSS-10 and CD-RISC-10 correlation: r ≈ -0.40 to -0.50 (moderate-to-strong negative)
- The correlation is robust across student, clinical, and general population samples
- Resilience buffers the relationship between perceived stress and negative mental health outcomes [@wollny2021]
