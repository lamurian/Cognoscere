## Location Criteria

1. Water access — perennial rivers or high rainfall for micro-hydro
2. Affordable land — outside major tourist zones (Bali interior > Bali coast)
3. Lax building codes — rural regencies where natural building is permitted
4. Existing communal traditions — areas with strong gotong royong and adat
5. Supportive neighbours — non-hostile existing village population
6. Accessibility — within 2-4 hours of a regional city for supplies and healthcare

## Top Candidate Regions

### 1. West Sumatra (Minangkabau Highlands)
- Why: Strongest adat system in Indonesia. Nagari (village republics) are autonomous, matrilineal, with established consensus governance.[@Dharmaraj2024Digital]
- Culture: Gotong royong is central. Minangkabau have a diaspora tradition (merantau) so outsiders integrating is culturally understood.
- Climate: Cool highlands (600-1500m), volcanic soil, abundant rainfall — ideal for permaculture.
- Land: Relatively affordable outside Padang. Nagari land is communally owned (tanah ulayat).
- Challenge: Land cannot be bought by outsiders under ulayat system — must lease or partner with nagari.

### 2. Bali (interior, not coast)
- Why: Subak irrigation system is a UNESCO-recognised model of democratic ecological governance operating for 1,000+ years.[@GlobalEcovillageNetwork]
- Culture: Hindu-Balinese have strong village banjar (neighbourhood councils) and mutual cooperation (ngayah).
- Land: More expensive than other regions, especially in south. Interior (Buleleng, Karangasem, Bangli) is cheaper.
- Challenge: High tourist presence means land speculation and some local resentment of foreign-owned projects. Foreigners cannot own freehold land (Hak Milik) — must use Hak Guna Bangunan (building rights) or leasehold.

### 3. East Java (Bromo-Tengger area)
- Why: Tenggerese communities retained pre-Hindu animist-Buddhist traditions, communal decision-making, and relative isolation.
- Climate: Cool highlands, volcanic soil, distinct wet/dry seasons.
- Land: More affordable, less tourist pressure.
- Challenge: Remoter areas mean limited infrastructure.

### 4. Sulawesi (Tana Toraja / Lake Poso)
- Why: Torajan adat is elaborate with strong communal house-building traditions (tongkonan). Land is relatively cheap.
- Climate: Highland tropical, good for diverse agriculture.
- Challenge: Some areas (Poso) have historical sectarian conflict. Need careful local relationship building.

## Legal Reality

Foreigners in Indonesia cannot own freehold land (Hak Milik). Options:
- Hak Guna Bangunan (Building Rights): 30 years + 20 year extension, for buildings
- Hak Pakai (Right to Use): for individuals, 25 years + renewable
- Hak Sewa (Leasehold): contractual, simpler
- PMDN (Domestic Investment Company): an Indonesian company with foreign ownership can own HGB titles
- Partnership with adat community: Some nagari/desa allow long-term leases on tanah ulayat (customary land) through formal agreement with the Kerapatan Adat Nagari (adat council)

Recommendation: Partner with an existing nagari or desa by offering skills, infrastructure, and market access in exchange for a long-term land agreement. This is the most culturally legitimate path.
