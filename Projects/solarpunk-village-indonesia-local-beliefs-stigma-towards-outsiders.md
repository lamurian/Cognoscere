## Core Cultural Framework

### Adat (Customary Law)
Adat governs every aspect of village life in Indonesia — social relations, land use, marriage, dispute resolution, and religious practice.[^5] It varies by region (there are 19+ adat law areas). Outsiders who ignore adat are seen as disrespectful at best, dangerous at worst.

### Common Beliefs About Outsiders

1. Orang asing = rich, potentially exploitative — Many rural communities have experienced extractive industries (mining, logging, plantations) run by outside corporations. A new group of foreigners buying land easily triggers suspicion of being another extraction project.

2. Religious difference is the primary divider — In most rural areas, Islam is deeply integrated into daily life. Non-Muslim or secular groups may face: restricted participation in communal prayers, suspicion of missionary intent (if Christian/secular), exclusion from mosque-based decision making.

3. Communal land is sacred — Tanah ulayat (customary land) is not just property; it is the physical embodiment of the community's relationship with ancestors and spirits. Proposals to 'buy' or 'develop' it can be spiritually offensive.

4. Gotong royong expectation — Communities expect newcomers to participate in communal work. Refusing to join gotong royong (road cleaning, mosque repairs, ceremonies) creates immediate social exclusion.

5. Slametan (communal feasts) are mandatory social events — The Javanese slametan ritual is the bedrock of village social cohesion. Absence is noted and resented.

### The 'Exo-Village' Stigma

Intentional communities face specific suspicion:
- Cult labelling — Closed communal groups are often assumed to be cults (aliran sesat). Indonesia has a history of state persecution of 'deviant' sects.
- Political fear — Left-leaning or anarchist frameworks trigger historical trauma from 1965-66 massacres. Terms like 'komunis' (communist) are still used as slurs.
- Moral panic — Communal living with non-traditional family structures, open relationships, or queer inclusion will trigger moral condemnation and potential vigilante action from hardline groups (FPI, FUI).

## Trust-Building Principles

- Slow entry: Live in the adjacent village for 6-12 months before proposing anything. Participate in everything.
- Find a local patron: A respected tokoh masyarakat (community leader) or kyai (Islamic teacher) who vouches for you.
- Transparency: Every meeting, plan, and budget must be discussed openly in the balai desa (village hall).
- Contribute first: Build a public facility (clean water, solar lighting for mosque, health clinic) before starting on private housing.
- Learn bahasa Indonesia and local dialect: Bahasa Indonesia is a unifying tool, but local languages (Javanese, Sundanese, Minangkabau) signal genuine commitment.
