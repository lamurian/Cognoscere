## The Land Problem

Foreigners cannot own freehold land (Hak Milik) in Indonesia under the 1960 Basic Agrarian Law (UUPA). This is non-negotiable and rooted in post-colonial land sovereignty principles.

## Legal Vehicles Available

### 1. Hak Guna Bangunan (HGB — Building Rights)
- Available through PMDN company (foreign-owned Indonesian legal entity)
- Initial 30 years + 20 year extension
- Can own buildings but not the land itself
- Requires minimum investment (BKPM regulations: ~Rp 10 billion/~$650K for certain sectors)
- Best for: Large integrated projects where capital is pooled

### 2. Hak Pakai (Right to Use)
- Available to individual foreigners with KITAS/KITAP (temporary/permanent stay permit)
- 25 years + renewable
- Limited to one property, must be used as residence
- Best for: Individual founders on Path B (embedded households)

### 3. Leasehold (Hak Sewa)
- Contractual, no land title transfer
- Duration negotiable (typically 10-30 years)
- Simpler paperwork, less protection
- Best for: Pilot phase before committing to larger structure

### 4. Partnership with Adat Community (Tanah Ulayat)
- Customary land is owned by the community, not individuals
- The Kerapatan Adat Nagari (KAN) or Desa Pekraman council can grant usage rights
- Not registered in formal land office (BPN), so legal enforcement is weak
- Requires strong personal relationship and ongoing legitimacy
- Best for: Joint Venture Village model

### 5. Yayasan (Foundation)
- Non-profit legal entity, can own land through HGB
- Requires Indonesian founders, foreign can be board members
- Used for educational, social, environmental purposes
- Solarpunk could qualify as environmental or educational yayasan
- Best for: Path C (pesantren hybrid) or Path A

## Recommended Strategy

Phase 1 (Year 0-1): Use leasehold or Hak Sewa for initial land. Keep capital exposure low. Build relationships simultaneously.

Phase 2 (Year 1-3): Form a Yayasan with mixed Indonesian-foreign board. The yayasan applies for HGB on the land. Indonesian board members provide legal standing and cultural bridge.

Phase 3 (Year 3+): If land trust model is desired, transfer HGB to a koperasi (cooperative) where all residents — Indonesian and foreign — are members. This mirrors the community land trust (CLT) model used by ecovillages worldwide.[^7]

## Political Risk Factors

- Regional Autonomy (Otonomi Daerah): Since 1999, district heads (bupati) have significant discretion over permits. A supportive bupati can fast-track everything; a hostile one can block everything.
- Mining concessions: Check if the land overlaps with a mining permit (IUP). Indonesia's mining cadastre is opaque, and overlapping claims are common.
- Forest area (Kawasan Hutan): ~63% of Indonesia is classified as forest zone under Ministry of Environment and Forestry. Building there requires complex permits. Avoid unless you have legal expertise.
- Land mafia (Mafia Tanah): Documented in many areas. Always use a reputable notary (PPAT) and conduct due diligence through BPN.
