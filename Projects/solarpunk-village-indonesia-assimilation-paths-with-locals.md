## Principle: Embed, Don't Replace

Indonesian solarpunk should not import a Western ecovillage model. It should reinforce existing communal and ecological knowledge — gotong royong, subak, kampung culture, adat — and build forward from there.[^3]

## Assimilation Path A: The Joint Venture Village

**Model**: Partner with an existing nagari (West Sumatra) or desa adat (Bali) where traditional governance is strong.
- The adat council (Kerapatan Adat Nagari / Desa Pekraman) grants land use rights
- The solarpunk group brings technical skills: renewable energy, water systems, permaculture design, global market access
- The local community provides land, labour, cultural legitimacy, and political protection
- Governance is shared through a musyawarah (deliberative council) with equal local-outsider representation
- **Example parallel**: Subak systems already manage shared water across hundreds of farmers through democratic councils. Solarpunk infrastructure could be managed the same way.

## Assimilation Path B: The Skill-Host Model

Instead of one large village, place 2-3 solarpunk households in an existing desa. Each household:
- Lives as a normal RT/RW (neighbourhood unit) member
- Participates in all gotong royong duties
- Runs a visible public good: home-scale solar charging station, seed bank, waste recycling point, permaculture demo garden
- Hosts weekly open workshops (open to all neighbours)
- Employs local labour fairly (above minimum wage, with training)
- **Goal**: Prove concepts work, then expand when local trust is earned (~3-5 years)

## Path C: The Pesantren Ecovillage Hybrid

Indonesia has thousands of pesantren (Islamic boarding schools). Some are progressive (Nahdlatul Ulama affiliated) and already practice community self-sufficiency. Offer to co-develop solarpunk infrastructure as part of the pesantren:
- Solar PV for the mosque and classrooms
- Permaculture garden for student food
- Biogas from kitchen waste
- Composting toilets (culturally sensitive: must consult with kyai)
- This path gives immediate religious legitimacy and bypasses outsider stigma.

## Required Behaviours for Acceptance

1. **Learn bahasa Indonesia to conversational level before moving.** English-only isolates.
2. **Participate in slametan (communal feasts) and pengajian (Quran study groups).** Even if not Muslim, attendance signals respect.
3. **Celebrate local holidays (Lebaran, Nyepi, Galungan) publicly.** Close the village, share food, visit neighbours.
4. **Hire local for construction, not imported skilled labour.** Train locals in natural building.
5. **Share resources immediately.** Solar charging for phones, water pumps, grinding mills — open to village.
6. **Contribute to the mosque/school/church/pura maintenance fund.** Even a small monthly amount is noted.
7. **Respect adat taboos:** In Minangkabau areas, do not touch people on the head. In Bali, do not point with left hand. In Java, do not use left hand for giving or receiving.
8. **Send children to local schools** (even if also supplementing with alternative education). Nothing builds trust faster than shared schooling.

## Expected Timeline

- **Year 0-1**: Living in or near target village, learning language, building relationships, no construction.
- **Year 1-2**: First visible contribution (public good project), formal agreement with adat/lurah.
- **Year 2-3**: Move onto land, start infrastructure, hire locally.
- **Year 3-5**: Village reaches seed scale (20-40), daily life integrated with local community.
- **Year 5+**: Stable integration — local people participate as members, not just employees.
