# The Economic Foundation of Healthcare Decisions

At its core, the science of economics studies human behaviour as a relationship between ends and scarce means that have alternative uses. This classic definition, articulated by Lionel Robbins, directly frames the fundamental challenge of healthcare resource allocation. In any health system, from sub-Saharan Africa to North America, available resources are never sufficient to fund all possible health interventions. Consequently, choices are imperative, and these choices often involve profoundly difficult decisions about which services to provide and which to forgo. The basic economic problem is how to best allocate these scarce resources to satisfy human wants.

Economic evaluation provides a coherent, explicit, and theoretically-based framework to help decision-makers navigate this challenge. Its primary goal is to address the efficiency objective: how can the finite resources dedicated to healthcare be used to maximize the health gain obtained from them? While maximizing health is not the sole objective of a health system—which may also prioritize fairness, education, or research—it remains a critically important goal. These methods offer a structured approach to measuring and valuing costs and outcomes, dealing with choice, and handling uncertainty.

This document serves as a detailed, practical guide to the fundamental stages of costing within a health economic evaluation. It outlines the systematic process of identifying, measuring, valuing, and adjusting costs, with a particular focus on adopting a comprehensive societal perspective. A clear understanding of these methodological steps is essential for producing transparent, credible, and comparable analyses that can meaningfully inform health policy.

# Defining the Scope of 'Cost' in Health Economic Evaluation

A crucial first step in any economic evaluation is to clearly define the scope of costs to be included in the analysis. This decision is dictated by the analytical perspective adopted. A comprehensive evaluation requires a broad view that encompasses all resources consumed as a consequence of an intervention, extending beyond direct medical expenditures to include costs borne by patients, their families, and other sectors of society. This ensures that the full economic impact of a healthcare decision is captured.

The primary categories of costs considered in a broad-based economic evaluation are as follows:

* **Healthcare Sector Costs:** These represent the most direct costs of providing a medical service. They include the value of all healthcare resources involved in the diagnosis and treatment of a condition, such as staff time, pharmaceuticals, consumables, and hospital stays.
* **Patient and Family Costs:** These are the out-of-pocket expenses and time costs incurred by patients and their informal caregivers. This category includes a wide range of resources, such as patient travel to and from appointments, over-the-counter medications, accommodation, child care expenses, and the time and expenses associated with informal care provided by family and friends.
* **Costs to Other Sectors (Productivity Costs):** This category captures the economic consequences of illness on an individual's ability to work, representing the value of lost productivity from paid work due to illness, treatment, or premature death.

Once these distinct cost categories have been identified based on the chosen analytical perspective, the next step involves the systematic process of quantifying their value.

# A Step-by-Step Framework for Comprehensive Cost Analysis

Performing a rigorous cost analysis is a systematic, multi-stage process that ensures transparency, accuracy, and comparability. The framework can be broken down into three core stages: the identification and enumeration of all relevant resources consumed, the measurement of the quantity of those resources in natural physical units, and the subsequent valuation of each resource unit in monetary terms. This structured approach is essential for building a credible and robust economic evaluation.

## Stage 1: Identification of Resources

The initial stage involves a detailed accounting of all relevant resource items, which can be thought of as developing a "production function" for the healthcare program under evaluation. This requires a thorough understanding of the disease process and the specific resources needed to deliver the intervention. The level of detail in this process is determined by the costing approach adopted.

| **Costing Approach** | **Definition** |
|-----------------------|----------------|
| **Gross-Costing (Top-Down)** | This approach views resources in aggregate bundles. An example would be using an average per-diem cost for a hospital stay, which encompasses a wide range of underlying inputs without itemizing them individually. |
| **Micro-Costing (Bottom-Up)** | This method requires the detailed identification and quantification of all underlying activities and inputs. For a hospital stay, this would involve measuring specific consumables, diagnostic tests, and the precise amount of time contributed by different staff members. |

In practice, many economic evaluations use a hybrid approach, combining the detail of micro-costing for key resource differences with the practicality of gross-costing for others.

## Stage 2: Measurement of Resource Quantities

After identifying the relevant resources, their quantities must be measured. This involves collecting data on how much of each resource was used. The primary methods for this data collection include:

1. **Administrative Data:** This involves extracting information from routinely collected sources such as electronic medical records, trial-specific case report forms, and hospital data systems. These sources can provide reliable data on items like medications administered or procedures performed.  
2. **Patient Questionnaires and Diaries:** To capture resource use that occurs outside of formal medical settings (e.g., primary care visits, informal care), researchers often rely on patient-reported instruments. These can range from detailed diaries to structured questionnaires, such as the patient cost questionnaire used in a large prospective study of therapies for type 2 diabetes.  
3. **Direct Measurement or Observation:** In some research contexts, particularly for micro-costing studies, resource use may be measured through direct observation and timing of procedures by research staff.

When using patient questionnaires, a critical methodological choice is the recall period. This decision involves a trade-off: a longer recall period increases the risk of recall error as patients may forget events, while a shorter period risks providing incomplete information if it fails to capture infrequent but important resource use. There is no single optimal recall period; the choice depends on the specific objectives of the data collection.

## Stage 3: Valuation of Resources

The final stage is to assign a monetary value to the measured quantities of resources. The guiding economic principle for valuation is **opportunity cost**, which is the direct consequence of the scarcity described earlier—the value of the benefit forgone by using a resource for one purpose instead of its next best alternative. In practice, however, market prices are commonly used as a reasonable and accessible approximation of opportunity cost.

Common sources for these unit costs include:

* **National Tariffs and Payment Systems:** Many countries have established systems that can be used for valuation. Examples include Diagnosis-Related Groups (DRGs) in the United States, which classify hospital cases into groups with similar expected resource use, and the National Reference Costs in England, which are based on Healthcare Resource Groups (HRGs). These systems are practical applications of the gross-costing (top-down) approach, as they provide an aggregate cost for a bundle of services associated with a specific diagnosis or procedure.
* **Published Compendiums:** Official publications that compile unit costs for a wide range of services are invaluable resources. In the United Kingdom, for example, the *Unit Costs of Health and Social Care* is a widely used source.

In some situations, a market price does not exist for a resource. A common example is the time provided by unpaid informal caregivers. In these cases, a technique known as **shadow pricing** is used, where the price of a comparable item for which a market price does exist is used to estimate the value.

### Special Considerations in Valuation

Valuing certain categories of costs, particularly those that fall outside the direct healthcare sector, requires specific methodological approaches.

The valuation of productivity costs is a subject of considerable debate, with three main approaches:

| **Approach** | **Definition** | **Rationale** | **Key Criticisms** |
|--------------|----------------|----------------|--------------------|
| **Human Capital Approach (HCA)** | Values lost productivity as the gross wage rate multiplied by the amount of working time lost due to illness, treatment, or death. It is the most widely used method. | Views an individual's earnings as a proxy for their economic contribution to society. | Can be seen as discriminatory against those not in the paid labor force (e.g., retirees, stay-at-home parents), whose productivity would be valued at zero. |
| **Friction Cost Approach (FCA)** | Limits productivity costs to the "friction period"—the time it takes for a sick worker to be replaced and for the replacement to reach the same level of productivity. | Argues that in economies with unemployment, a sick worker can be replaced, so the true societal cost is limited to the short-term disruption. | Rejects standard micro-economic assumptions and may not be applicable in economies with low unemployment where replacements are not readily available. |
| **US Panel Approach** | Values time invested in treatment ('time costs') using the HCA, but posits that broader productivity losses from illness ('morbidity costs') should not be separately valued. | Aims to avoid double-counting by arguing that the impact of illness on income and work is already captured within patient-reported quality-of-life measures. | The assumption that quality-of-life measures inherently capture the full value of morbidity-related income loss is debated. |

To value the contribution of informal care, specialized questionnaires can be used to measure the time dedicated by informal caregivers, which can then be valued using shadow pricing methods. This is particularly relevant when conducting an analysis from a societal perspective. Once all relevant resources have been identified, measured, and valued, a final set of adjustments is required to ensure all costs are comparable across time and geography.

## Analytical Adjustments for Comparability

When cost data are collected over different time periods or from multiple countries, adjustments are necessary to ensure comparability. These adjustments address **inflation**, **currency differences**, and the **time value of money**.

* **Inflation and Currency Adjustments**
  * **Inflation:** Costs from different years must be adjusted to a common price year using a price index. While a general index like the Consumer Price Index (CPI) can be used, a sector-specific index, such as the Hospital and Community Health Services (HCHS) index in the UK, is preferred as it more accurately reflects inflation within the healthcare sector itself.
  * **Currency Conversion:** Costs from different countries must be converted to a common currency. While market exchange rates are readily available, they are volatile and do not always reflect the relative prices of non-tradable goods like healthcare services. **Purchasing Power Parities (PPPs)** are generally preferred. PPPs are calculated based on the cost of a standardized basket of goods and services in different countries, providing a more stable and relevant comparison of purchasing power.
  * **Order of Operations:** It is critical to perform these adjustments in the correct order: costs must first be adjusted for inflation in their local currency before being converted to a common currency using PPPs.
* **Discounting**
  * **Rationale:** Discounting is the process of adjusting the value of future costs and benefits to reflect their present-day value. It is applied in any analysis with a time horizon longer than one or two years. This is based on the principle of time preference: a general preference to receive benefits sooner and incur costs later.
  * **Discounting Costs:** Future costs are valued less than present costs. This is because resources not spent today can be invested to generate a real rate of return. Furthermore, future societies are expected to be wealthier (due to GDP growth), making a future cost less burdensome relative to total resources.
  * **Discounting Health Benefits:** Future health benefits (e.g., Quality-Adjusted Life-Years, or QALYs) are also discounted, reflecting the principle of time preference—a health benefit is valued more highly if it is received today rather than in the future.

The systematic application of this framework—encompassing identification, measurement, valuation, and subsequent analytical adjustments—provides a transparent and defensible foundation for the costing component of a health economic evaluation, leading into the final consideration of analytical perspective.

# The Critical Role of Analytical Perspective

The analytical perspective is one of the most crucial methodological choices in a health economic evaluation, as it fundamentally determines which costs and consequences are included in the analysis. The perspective defines the viewpoint from which the evaluation is being conducted and, therefore, which impacts are considered relevant to the decision-maker.

The **Societal Perspective** is the broadest and most comprehensive viewpoint. It demands the inclusion of all costs and consequences of an intervention, regardless of who bears them, thereby encompassing all three major categories defined previously: healthcare sector costs, patient and family costs, and costs to other sectors (productivity costs). This perspective is rigorously grounded in welfare economic theory, which posits that an efficient allocation of resources must consider the full range of effects on all members of society.

Under a societal perspective, the included cost categories are extensive:

* Medication cost  
* Intervention cost (e.g., surgery, diagnostics)  
* Primary care visits  
* Hospital and emergency visits  
* Productivity costs (losses from paid work)  
* Patient and family costs (e.g., travel, child care, informal care time)

In contrast, many health technology assessment agencies recommend a narrower **Payer Perspective** for their primary analyses. For example, the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) in England and Wales recommends a primary analysis from the perspective of the National Health Service (NHS) and Personal Social Services (PSS). This perspective would include direct medical and social care costs but would exclude broader societal costs, such as patient out-of-pocket expenses and productivity losses.

Ultimately, a rigorous, transparent, and methodologically sound approach to costing, grounded in a clearly defined and justified analytical perspective, is essential for producing credible health economic evaluations. Only through such careful and systematic analysis can these evaluations reliably inform the complex policy and resource allocation decisions that shape modern healthcare.
