# Solarpunk Village: Infrastructure & Energy Systems

Physical infrastructure for a solarpunk village is designed for **regenerative integration** with the local ecosystem, not extraction or domination of it. Buildings, energy, water, and transport all embody solarpunk aesthetics and values [@Kasper2008Redefining; @Lockyer2018Community; @NewcastleFindhorn].

## Clustering & Land Use

The fundamental planning principle: **build small, conserve large**.

| Land Use Category | Typical Allocation | Examples |
|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Built area** (dense cluster) | 5–10% | Homes, common house, workshops |
| **Agriculture** | 15–25% | Gardens, orchards, pasture, food forest |
| **Conservation / green space** | 65–90% | Native prairie, forest, wetlands, wildlife corridors [@Lockyer2018Community] |

Findhorn: ~30 buildings on a compact site. Dancing Rabbit: ~20 acres built out of 280 total (93% green space) [@Lockyer2018Community]. Architect Greg Ramsey recommends 90% green space in rural ecovillages [@Kasper2008Redefining].

## Buildings & Construction

Solarpunk architecture blends **natural building** and **green building** techniques:

- **Natural building**: cob, straw bale, cordwood, rammed earth — local, low-embodied-energy, highly insulating [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Green building**: high-tech insulation, passive solar design, energy-efficient windows, heat recovery systems [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Common features**: green roofs, living walls, rainwater downspouts integrated into water catchment
- **Orientation**: buildings face the sun (not the road) for passive solar heating and PV efficiency [@Kasper2008Redefining]

All 8 ecovillages studied by Kasper (2008) prioritise energy efficiency, recycled materials, and solar aspect. Ecovillage C aims for 100% renewable energy powered homes; Ecovillage F builds "energy centres" adaptable to future solar collectors or fuel cells [@Kasper2008Redefining].

## Renewable Energy

| System | Typical Scale | Examples |
|--------|--------------|----------|
| Solar PV (rooftop + ground-mount) | 50–200 kW for 50–150 people | Findhorn, Dancing Rabbit [@NewcastleFindhorn] |
| Small wind turbines | 1–3 turbines (5–50 kW each) | Findhorn (4 onshore turbines) [@NewcastleFindhorn] |
| Biomass boiler / district heating | 250 kW (centralised) | Findhorn, Cloughjordan [@NewcastleFindhorn] |
| Solar hot water | Building-integrated | All ecovillages [@Kasper2008Redefining] |
| Micro-hydro (if creek/river present) | 5–20 kW | Lammas [@UKERCLammas] |
| Community microgrid + battery storage | Islandable from grid | Aspirational in most; F still grid-connected [@Kasper2008Redefining] |

Findhorn's community-owned electricity network distributes power from 4 wind turbines, a 250kW biomass boiler, building-mounted PV, and solar hot water systems — plus EV charging points [@NewcastleFindhorn].

## Water & Waste

- **Rainwater catchment** — roof collection → storage tanks → household use
- **Greywater systems** — shower/sink water → constructed wetland → irrigation
- **Composting toilets** — dry composting systems (may face local code barriers in rural areas) [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Blackwater treatment** — reed beds, biogas digesters
- **Solid waste** — near-zero via composting, repair cafes, and shared purchasing

Zoning and building codes are the biggest infrastructure challenge. Ecovillages often choose rural locations specifically because codes are more lenient toward composting toilets, alternative energy, and natural building methods [@Kasper2008Redefining].

## Transportation

- **Pedestrian-scale village** — walkable paths, no internal car traffic
- **Bicycle + cart infrastructure** — bikes, cargo trikes, handcarts for moving goods [@Lockyer2018Community]
- **Vehicle co-operative** — shared cars/trucks parked at village perimeter [@Lockyer2018Community]
- **Proximity to rail** — Cloughjordan ecovillage is built around a train station (ideal but rare)
- **EV charging** — shared charging points for electric vehicles [@NewcastleFindhorn]

Dancing Rabbit keeps vehicles on the village perimeter except during construction. Lockyer (2018) notes the "pedestrian-scaled village" reduces both energy use and social isolation [@Lockyer2018Community].

## Internet & Connectivity

Most ecovillages maintain high-speed internet for:
- Remote work (external income source)
- Education (online courses, children's remote learning)
- Outreach (website, virtual tours, workshops)
- Governance tools (shared calendars, decision platforms, wiki)

## Phased Infrastructure Build-Out

| Phase | Infrastructure Priority |
|-------|-------------------------|
| **Year 0–1** | Land acquisition, road, well, solar-battery for common building, composting toilet |
| **Year 1–3** | First clustered homes, common house, expanded solar, water catchment, garden irrigation |
| **Year 3–5** | Workshop buildings, food processing facility, second solar array, district heat if feasible |
| **Year 5–10** | Advanced energy storage, EV charging, commercial kitchen, visitor/education centre |
