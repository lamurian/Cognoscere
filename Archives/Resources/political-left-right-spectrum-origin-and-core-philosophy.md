## Overview

The **left–right political spectrum** is a framework for classifying political ideologies, parties, and positions along a continuum. This note covers its historical origin and the core philosophical distinction that underpins it across cultures.

---

## Origin: The French Revolution (1789)

During the French Revolution, the National Assembly divided seating by faction:

- **Left side** — Radical revolutionaries who sought to abolish the monarchy, reduce Church power, and push for egalitarian reform.
- **Right side** — Conservatives who wanted to preserve the monarchy, the aristocracy, and the Church's institutional role.[@MITSpectrum2012]

This seating arrangement became a permanent metaphor: *la gauche* (the left) and *la droite* (the right), spreading worldwide over the following centuries.

---

## Core Philosophical Distinction

Modern political science identifies **attitudes toward equality** as the single most consistent dividing line between left and right across cultures and eras.[@LSEEuropp2023]

| Dimension | Left Wing | Right Wing |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Core value** | Favours greater **equality** — seeks to reduce economic and social inequality | Sees society as **naturally hierarchical** — accepts or defends inequality as necessary or desirable |
| **Attitude to change** | Open to reform, progress, and structural change | Prefers tradition, order, stability, and incremental change |
| **Economy** | Supports redistribution, welfare state, public ownership, regulation of capitalism | Supports free markets, low taxes, private property, limited government intervention |
| **Society** | Emphasises collective welfare, solidarity, minority rights | Emphasises individual responsibility, authority, national/cultural identity |
| **Key historical thinkers** | Jean-Jacques Rousseau, Karl Marx, John Maynard Keynes | Edmund Burke, Friedrich Hayek, Milton Friedman |

> *"The left favours greater equality, while the right sees society as inevitably hierarchical."* — Jost, Glaser, Kruglanski & Sulloway (2003)[@Jost2003Conservatism]

---

## Key Clarifications

- **Left and right are relative, not absolute** — a position considered "left-wing" in one country may be centrist or even right-wing in another (see [[global-variation-of-the-left-right-political-spectrum]]).
- **The spectrum is multi-dimensional** — economic left-right (redistribution vs. free market) and social/cultural left-right (progressivism vs. traditionalism) do not always align.
- **Populism cross-cuts** — both left-wing populism (anti-elite, pro-worker) and right-wing populism (anti-immigrant, nationalist) exist, complicating the traditional axis.
