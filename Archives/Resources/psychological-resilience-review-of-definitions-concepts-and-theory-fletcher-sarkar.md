## Source
- **Title:** Psychological Resilience: A Review and Critique of Definitions, Concepts and Theory
- **Authors:** David Fletcher (Loughborough University) & Mustafa Sarkar (Middlesex University)
- **URL:** https://irep.ntu.ac.uk/id/eprint/8063/1/221275_PubSub2839_Sarkar.pdf
- **Published in:** *European Psychologist* (2013), Vol. 18(1), 12–23

## Summary

This paper provides a comprehensive review and critique of the definitions, concepts, and theories of psychological resilience. The authors identify three core areas of contention in the resilience literature.

**Definitions.** Most definitions of resilience centre on two core concepts: **adversity** and **positive adaptation**. However, the field struggles with how to operationalise adversity — whether it should be limited to statistically significant predictors of maladjustment (threshold-based) or encompass everyday hassles and ostensibly positive life events (e.g., a job promotion or marriage) that nonetheless demand adaptive resources. Positive adaptation must be domain-appropriate (e.g., academic achievement for school children vs. absence of psychiatric symptoms for military personnel) and its stringency calibrated to the severity of adversity faced. The authors also highlight the sociocultural context: Western-centric definitions of competence (academic success, healthy relationships) may lack ecological sensitivity across cultures.

**Concepts.** The paper distinguishes between resilience as a **trait** vs. a **dynamic process**. The trait view holds that resilience is a constellation of protective characteristics (hardiness, positive emotions, self-efficacy, spirituality, etc.) that individuals either have or lack. The process view — which the authors favour — sees resilience as an interactive influence of psychological characteristics within the stress process, varying contextually and temporally. Crucially, the authors argue that **resilience is conceptually distinct from recovery and coping**:

- *Resilience vs. recovery:* Recovery involves a temporary dip in functioning followed by gradual restoration; resilience involves maintaining normal functioning throughout.
- *Resilience vs. coping:* Resilience operates *prior to* and *during* appraisal (shaping how stressors are perceived), whereas coping comprises the strategies deployed *after* a stressful encounter has been appraised as taxing. Resilience augurs positive responses; coping strategies can be positive or negative.

The paper proposes an integrated definition: *"Psychological resilience is the role of mental processes and behaviour in promoting personal assets and protecting an individual from the potential negative effect of stressors."*

**Theories.** Over a dozen resilience theories are summarised, including those specific to families, nurses, athletes, medical students, military personnel, police officers, and adolescents. The authors critically examine Richardson's (2002) metatheory of resilience and resiliency — the most commonly cited generic theory — finding it conceptually flawed: it is linear, ignores simultaneous multiple stressors, does not account for meta-cognition/meta-emotion, and conflates resilience with coping.

The paper concludes with implications for policy and practice: carefully managing environmental demands (moderate adversity can inoculate rather than harm), providing community-based resilience-building opportunities, and delivering evidence-based workshops on appraisal and meta-reflective strategies.

## Key Points

- Resilience definitions converge on two pillars: **adversity** and **positive adaptation**, but there is no consensus on how to operationalise either.
- Ostensibly positive life events (promotions, marriage) also demand resilience — the field should broaden its scope beyond risk-related adversity.
- Positive adaptation indicators must be **domain-appropriate** (academic, psychological, social) and **culturally sensitive**.
- Resilience is best conceptualised as a **dynamic process**, not a static trait. It changes over time and across contexts.
- **Protective factors** (hardiness, optimism, self-efficacy, social support) buffer against stressors; **promotive factors** confer independent salutary benefits.
- Resilience is **conceptually distinct from coping**: resilience shapes appraisal *before* coping strategies are selected.
- Richardson's resiliency model is widely cited but has significant conceptual drawbacks, including a linear structure that cannot handle multiple simultaneous demands and a bias toward coping-oriented processes.
- Moderate lifetime adversity can be beneficial (stress inoculation), challenging the assumption that all adversity is harmful.
- Practice implications: manage immediate environments carefully, deliver training on appraisal and meta-reflection (e.g., challenging catastrophic thinking, cultivating gratitude).

## Relevance

This is a foundational review for anyone studying or applying psychological resilience. It identifies the conceptual disagreements that still plague the field and provides a clear roadmap for researchers and practitioners. The paper's critique of resilience as a trait (vs. process) directly informs how resilience should be measured and developed in applied settings.
