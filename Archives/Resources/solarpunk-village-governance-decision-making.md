# Solarpunk Village: Governance & Decision-Making

Governance is the backbone of a solarpunk village. Without robust, trusted decision-making processes, communities fracture. This document synthesises governance practices from established ecovillages [@Kasper2008Redefining; @Lockyer2018Community; @EckerdEcovillages].

## Core Model: Layered Consensus

Successful ecovillages use a **layered governance system** that balances direct democracy with efficiency:

```
Full Community (all members)
  │  ──  Major decisions: land purchase, mission, new members
  ▼
Village Council (3–5 elected reps)
  │  ──  Weekly operational decisions
  ▼
Committees (standing / ad hoc)
      ──  Land Use, Finance, Membership, Buildings, Education, Food
```

### 1. Full-Group Business Meetings

- **Frequency**: 1–2 times per month (average across 8 studied ecovillages) [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Decision method**: Consensus (43 of 61 US ecovillages use consensus; 8 use other methods) [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Format**: Facilitator, scribe, time-keeper assigned; circular seating; centering moment; evaluation round at end [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Outcome**: Even members who disagree feel heard — "having the opportunity to voice concerns" is cited as key to satisfaction [@Kasper2008Redefining]

### 2. Village Council

As communities grow beyond ~60, full-group consensus becomes unwieldy. Dancing Rabbit evolved from full-group consensus to a **representative village council** with consensus-based internal decisions [@Lockyer2018Community]. The council handles routine operations and delegates deliberation to committees.

### 3. Standing Committees

Every adult serves on at least one committee. Common committees:

| Committee | Responsibilities |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Land Use** | Leasehold approvals, design review, green space boundaries, ecological planning [@Lockyer2018Community] |
| **Finance** | Budget, fee collection, insurance, grants |
| **Membership** | Visitor process, provisional member review, orientation |
| **Buildings & Energy** | Construction standards, renewable energy, maintenance priorities |
| **Food** | Agricultural planning, garden leaseholds, community meals |
| **Outreach** | Tours, workshops, website, publications |

## Legal Structures

Ecovillages typically use a **two-entity model**:

1. **Land Trust (501(c)(2))** — owns the land collectively, holds it in trust [@Lockyer2018Community]
2. **Research & Education Nonprofit (501(c)(3))** — runs outreach, courses, workshops

The land trust model **removes land from the speculative market**. Members lease individual plots for a monthly fee, own improvements (buildings, orchards), and can sell those improvements to another member if they leave. But no one can sell the land itself [@Lockyer2018Community].

> "The land trust model takes land out of the speculative market while still providing an ownership-like option for our members." — Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage [@Lockyer2018Community]

## Rules & Policy

Every ecovillage has codified policies that govern:

- **Membership process** — application, provisional period (often 1–2 years), full membership
- **Building and design standards** — natural materials, solar aspect, footprint limits [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Land use** — leasehold boundaries, conservation areas, agricultural zones
- **Labour requirements** — minimum hours/week contributed (5 of 8 ecovillages require it) [@Kasper2008Redefining]
- **Diet and consumption** — composting, biodegradable materials, shared resources
- **Leaving the community** — process for selling improvements, lease termination

Policies evolve through the consensus process — they are "open to adjustment as the community deems necessary" [@Kasper2008Redefining].

## Conflict Resolution

Conflict is inevitable. Ecovillages use:

1. **Regular "process" or "evaluation" segments** in meetings — members assess what went well and what didn't [@Kasper2008Redefining]
2. **Dedicated facilitation team** — trained in nonviolent communication and consensus
3. **Annual retreats** — dedicated time for deeper issues, mission review, long-range planning [@Lockyer2018Community]
4. **Formal grievance process** — escalation path from informal conversation to facilitated mediation to council hearing

## Elinor Ostrom's Principles Applied

Dancing Rabbit and similar communities embody Ostrom's 8 principles for commons management [@Lockyer2018Community]:

1. Clearly defined boundaries (land use zones, leaseholds)
2. Proportional equivalence between benefits and costs (labour requirements)
3. Collective choice arrangements (all members vote on rules)
4. Monitoring (land use committee inspects compliance)
5. Graduated sanctions (warnings → fines → membership review)
6. Conflict resolution mechanisms (facilitation team)
7. Minimal recognition of rights to organise (legal entities)
8. Nested enterprises (committees → council → full community)
